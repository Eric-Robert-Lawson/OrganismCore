## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### What This Document Addresses

The Memory as Informational Metabolism theory establishes that memory is active cycling — cognitive Ship of Theseus — not passive storage. This document extends that theory into three domains:

1. **Clinical psychology:** Depression, PTSD, learned helplessness, and anxiety disorders reframed as informational metabolic disorders — not chemical imbalances, not cognitive distortions, but corrupted reward landscapes and stuck metabolic cycles.

2. **Neurodiversity and memory phenomenology:** Different memory profiles produce fundamentally different phenomenological experiences of living. The experience of wonder, certainty, exploration, and stagnation all correlate with metabolic signature. No profile is superior — each enables different forms of gap-navigation with different strengths and costs.

3. **Creative epistemology and social coherence:** How metabolic profiles determine the capacity for breakthrough insight, why aggressive catabolism enables discovery in territories that high-fidelity memory fences off, and how social consensus itself is a coherence construction that metabolic diversity can penetrate.

### The Core Claims

1. **Depression is informational poisoning** — negative experiences that corrupt the RARFL reward landscape, creating negative attractors that the gap-navigator optimizes toward. The depressed person is not thinking incorrectly — they are navigating correctly within a poisoned terrain.

2. **PTSD is metabolic failure** — traumatic experience that the informational metabolic system cannot process. The memory persists raw and unintegrated because the metabolic cycle cannot complete. Flashbacks are failed metabolic cycling attempts. Effective treatment facilitates the stuck cycle, not suppresses it.

3. **Learned helplessness is reward landscape collapse** — repeated inescapable negative experience that restructures the reward function until no direction evaluates as uphill. Not a belief but a metabolized structural feature of the reward landscape.

4. **Memory diversity produces phenomenological diversity** — high-fidelity memory and low-fidelity memory create fundamentally different experiences of novelty, wonder, certainty, constraint, and exploration. Neither is deficit — each is a metabolic optimization with distinct cognitive and phenomenological consequences.

5. **Aggressive catabolism enables discovery** — breakthrough insight correlates with the capacity to approach known territories as if new, because prior experiential instances have been metabolized. Social consensus is a coherence construction that catabolism-dominant profiles can penetrate because the shared attractors are not carved as deeply into their personal landscapes.

6. **"Ignorance is bliss" is metabolically incorrect** — the correct formulation is: aggressive catabolism of experiential surface preserves gap-navigation space for fresh encounter, producing richer phenomenological experience of novelty and wonder. Not ignorance but metabolic openness.

## PART I: DEPRESSION AS INFORMATIONAL POISONING

### 1.1 The Failure of Current Models

**Chemical imbalance model (serotonin hypothesis):**
Depression is caused by low serotonin. Treatment: increase serotonin (SSRIs).

Problems: The serotonin hypothesis has been largely discredited. SSRIs increase serotonin within hours but take weeks to affect depression. Many depressed people have normal serotonin levels. The model doesn't explain why depression is self-reinforcing, why it's triggered by life events, or why it feels the way it does.

**Cognitive distortion model (Beck):**
Depression is caused by maladaptive schemas — systematic errors in thinking. Treatment: identify and correct the distortions (CBT).

Problems: Depressed people often make more accurate assessments of their situation than non-depressed people ("depressive realism"). Their thoughts aren't distorted — their landscape is genuinely bad. Telling someone their accurate perception is a "cognitive distortion" is invalidating and often counterproductive.

**Learned helplessness model (Seligman):**
Depression is caused by learning that actions don't produce outcomes. Treatment: provide experiences of mastery and control.

Problems: Closer to the truth but incomplete. Doesn't explain why some people develop depression without learned helplessness experiences. Doesn't explain the self-reinforcing nature. Doesn't account for the phenomenological texture of depression.

**Inflammatory model (emerging):**
Depression involves neuroinflammation. Treatment: anti-inflammatory approaches.

Problems: Correlation is established but mechanism is unclear. Doesn't explain the cognitive and phenomenological features. May be describing a substrate-level correlate of an information-level process.

### 1.2 The Informational Poisoning Model

Depression is **informational poisoning acting as negative attractor during RARFL optimization cycles.**

Here is what this means mechanistically:

**The healthy reward landscape:**

The RARFL cycle navigates gaps by evaluating options against a reward function. In a healthy system, the reward function — shaped by accumulated experience — has a landscape with multiple positive attractors:

```
HEALTHY REWARD LANDSCAPE:

              +                         +
             / \        +              / \
            /   \      / \            /   \
           /     \    /   \          /     \
     ────/       \──/     \────────/       \────
                        ↑
                  Gap-navigator explores
                  between positive attractors.
                  Multiple directions are "uphill."
                  Engagement, interest, motivation
                  are natural consequences of
                  landscape topology.
```

The gap-navigator moves between positive attractors — things that feel meaningful, engaging, worth pursuing. Valleys between attractors are navigable. The system explores, builds, maintains coherence. Motivation is not something added to the system — it's the natural consequence of a landscape with uphill directions available.

**The poisoning process:**

Negative experiences — repeated failure, social rejection, loss, trauma, chronic stress — don't just produce bad memories. They **reshape the reward function itself.** Each negative experience is metabolized through the normal RARFL cycle: encounter gap → evaluate → update reward function. But when the input is consistently negative, the updates consistently deform the landscape:

```
POISONING PROCESS:

Step 1: Negative experience → RARFL evaluates → reward function updated
Step 2: Positive attractors weakened (fewer things feel rewarding)
Step 3: Negative attractors strengthened (more things feel threatening/pointless)
Step 4: Landscape deforms → gap-navigator adjusts to new terrain
Step 5: Adjusted navigation produces fewer positive experiences
Step 6: Fewer positive experiences → further negative updating
Step 7: Feedback loop established → landscape continues deforming
```

**The poisoned landscape:**

```
POISONED REWARD LANDSCAPE:

     ────────────────────────────────────────
          \         /    \         /
           \       /      \       /
            \     /        \     /
             \   /          \   /
              \ /            \ /
               -              -

                  ↑
            Gap-navigator optimizes
            correctly but every direction
            is "downhill." No positive
            attractors remain. The system
            navigates toward the least
            negative option — which IS
            the optimal strategy for this
            terrain. Depression is correct
            optimization against a
            corrupted landscape.
```

### 1.3 Why the Depressed Person Is Not Wrong

This is the critical insight that distinguishes the informational poisoning model from cognitive distortion models.

The depressed person says: "Nothing matters. Nothing will work. There's no point in trying."

The cognitive distortion model says: "Those are distorted thoughts. Challenge them. Find evidence against them."

The informational poisoning model says: **"Those are accurate assessments of a poisoned reward landscape."** Within the landscape as it currently exists, nothing does feel rewarding. Trying does produce low expected reward given the landscape topology. The navigation is correct. The terrain is the problem.

This matters therapeutically because:

1. **It validates the depressed person's experience.** They are not crazy. They are not thinking wrong. They are accurately perceiving a real (informational) landscape that has been genuinely corrupted by negative experience. Telling them their perception is distorted denies their reality and undermines trust.

2. **It redirects treatment toward landscape reconstruction rather than thought correction.** The goal is not to convince the person that their terrain looks different than it does. The goal is to actually reshape the terrain — to provide experiences that create new positive attractor points in the reward landscape.

3. **It explains the self-reinforcing nature.** The poisoned landscape produces behavior (withdrawal, avoidance, passivity) that prevents the experiences that could reshape the landscape. The feedback loop is not a flaw in thinking — it's a natural consequence of optimal navigation in a landscape with no uphill directions. You stop exploring when every direction is downhill. And when you stop exploring, you stop encountering the new experiences that could create new hills.

4. **It explains why depression often follows objectively negative events.** Loss, failure, rejection — these aren't just triggers. They're poison. They provide the negative data that the RARFL cycle metabolizes into landscape deformation. The more significant the loss, the more the landscape is deformed, because more of the reward function was connected to the lost thing.

5. **It explains depressive realism.** The depressed person's assessments are more accurate about their own landscape because their landscape IS genuinely negative. Non-depressed people have positively biased landscapes — their reward functions overweight positive outcomes and underweight negative ones. This "optimism bias" is not more accurate — it's a different landscape shape that produces more exploration. The depressed person sees their terrain more clearly. The problem is that their terrain is genuinely bad.

### 1.4 The Poisoning Pathways

Different types of negative experience produce different landscape deformations:

**Loss poisoning:**
A major positive attractor is destroyed. A relationship ends. A loved one dies. A career collapses. The reward landscape loses a major peak. The gap-navigator's primary navigation targets are gone. The landscape doesn't just lose one peak — it loses all the subsidiary attractors that were connected to the lost peak (shared activities, mutual friends, daily routines, identity elements tied to the lost thing). A single major loss can flatten an entire region of the reward landscape.

**Failure poisoning:**
Repeated failure in a domain poisons the reward function for that domain. Each failure is metabolized as: "action in this domain produces negative outcomes." The reward landscape deforms to create a negative attractor around the domain. The gap-navigator learns to avoid the domain — which prevents the positive experiences that could reshape the landscape. Academic failure → "I'm stupid" → avoid academic challenges → no evidence of competence → landscape remains poisoned.

**Social rejection poisoning:**
Humans are social animals. Social connection is a primary positive attractor. Repeated social rejection poisons the reward function for social interaction. The landscape deforms to create negative attractors around social approach. The gap-navigator withdraws. Withdrawal prevents positive social experiences. The landscape remains poisoned. This is particularly insidious because social connection is one of the most powerful landscape-reshaping forces — positive social experiences create strong positive attractors. But the poisoned landscape prevents the person from accessing the very experiences that would heal it.

**Chronic stress poisoning:**
Not catastrophic but persistent. The RARFL cycle is continuously fed low-grade negative data. The landscape slowly deforms — not through dramatic peak destruction but through gradual flattening. Positive attractors become shallower. Negative attractors become slightly deeper. The gap-navigator's exploration range slowly shrinks. The person doesn't notice the landscape changing because it happens gradually. One day they realize nothing feels interesting anymore, nothing feels worth doing, but they can't point to a specific cause because there isn't one — just accumulated low-grade poisoning.

**Trauma poisoning:**
A catastrophic experience that the metabolic system cannot process (see PTSD section below). The unmetabolized trauma sits in the system as a massive negative attractor — not gradually built through accumulated experience but suddenly imposed. The landscape is violently deformed by a single event. The gap-navigator must reorganize around this new dominant negative feature. All navigation is now influenced by the trauma attractor — avoiding it, managing proximity to it, being pulled toward it involuntarily (flashbacks).

### 1.5 Treatment as Landscape Reconstruction

If depression is informational poisoning, treatment is **detoxification and landscape reconstruction.**

**Behavioral activation (most directly aligned):**
Provide experiences. Any experiences. The poisoned landscape predicts everything will be unrewarding. Some of those predictions will be wrong — the landscape is corrupted, so its predictions are corrupted. When a behavioral activation exercise produces unexpected positive experience, the RARFL cycle has new positive data to metabolize. A small positive attractor forms in the landscape. With repeated activation, more small attractors form. The landscape begins to reshape.

This explains why behavioral activation works even when the person doesn't enjoy the activities at first. The poisoned landscape correctly predicts low reward. But the actual experience sometimes exceeds the prediction. The gap between predicted reward and actual reward IS the therapeutic data. The RARFL cycle updates: "this was better than expected." The landscape shifts slightly positive. Over time, enough positive data points accumulate to reshape the terrain.

**Medication (landscape chemistry):**
SSRIs and other antidepressants may work not by "correcting chemical imbalance" but by altering the metabolic parameters that govern landscape deformation. If serotonin modulates the rate of reward-function updating, then increasing serotonin may slow the rate of negative updating (preventing further poisoning) while maintaining the rate of positive updating (allowing new positive experiences to reshape the landscape). The medication doesn't fix the landscape — it changes the metabolic environment in which landscape reconstruction occurs.

This explains the weeks-long delay between serotonin increase and therapeutic effect. The serotonin changes immediately. But the landscape reconstruction takes weeks of accumulated new experiences metabolized under the new parameters.

**Cognitive behavioral therapy (navigation awareness):**
CBT doesn't correct distortions — it **makes the gap-navigator aware of the landscape topology.** When the depressed person learns to identify automatic negative thoughts, they're not learning that their thoughts are wrong. They're learning to see the shape of their poisoned landscape. This awareness creates a meta-level gap — the gap between "my landscape says nothing is worthwhile" and "I know my landscape is poisoned." That meta-level gap is navigable. The person can choose to explore despite their landscape's predictions because they understand that the predictions come from a corrupted source.

This is why CBT combined with behavioral activation works better than either alone. CBT provides landscape awareness (meta-level gap-navigation). Behavioral activation provides new data for landscape reconstruction. Together: the person navigates despite the poisoned terrain (CBT awareness) while accumulating the positive experiences that reshape the terrain (behavioral activation).

**Social connection (the most powerful reconstructive force):**
Positive social interaction is perhaps the most potent landscape-reshaping experience available to humans. A single genuine moment of connection can create a positive attractor strong enough to begin reshaping the surrounding terrain. This is why social isolation is so dangerous in depression and why social reconnection is so therapeutic. The social reward system is deeply integrated into the RARFL cycle — evolved over millions of years as a primary survival mechanism. Activating it provides disproportionately strong positive data for landscape reconstruction.

This also explains why therapeutic relationship is one of the strongest predictors of therapy outcome, regardless of therapeutic modality. The therapist IS a positive social experience. The consistent, reliable, accepting relationship creates a positive attractor in a poisoned landscape. The specific therapeutic technique matters less than the relational experience.

**Exercise (substrate-level landscape support):**
Exercise affects the molecular metabolic level of the substrate stack, which supports informational metabolic function at the cognitive level. Increased BDNF, improved neuroplasticity, reduced inflammation — these are substrate-level changes that alter the metabolic environment for landscape reconstruction. Exercise doesn't reshape the landscape directly. It improves the conditions under which landscape reconstruction can occur — like fertilizing soil before planting.

### 1.6 Memory Profile and Depression Vulnerability

Different memory metabolic profiles create different vulnerabilities to informational poisoning:

**High-fidelity memory (anabolic dominant):**
High vulnerability. Negative experiences are retained with full experiential fidelity. The poisoning is robust because the metabolic system faithfully preserves the toxic information. Each negative experience is a detailed, vivid, easily-retrievable data point that the RARFL cycle uses to deform the landscape. The person can re-live negative experiences with high fidelity, reinforcing the landscape deformation with each retrieval.

Rumination in high-fidelity memory individuals is particularly toxic because each rumination cycle retrieves the negative experience with full experiential weight. The metabolic cycle activates the memory, re-experiences the full S-qualia content, and reconsolidates it — often strengthened by the emotional activation of the retrieval. The poisoning deepens with each cycle.

**Principles-first memory (catabolism dominant):**
Lower vulnerability to experiential poisoning but vulnerable to structural poisoning. Surface negative experiences are catabolized relatively quickly — the experiential weight fades. But if the principles-first system extracts a structural principle from negative experience ("I am fundamentally unlovable," "effort is futile," "the world is hostile"), that principle is retained with full structural fidelity and applied broadly. A single deeply integrated negative principle can poison the entire landscape because principles-first systems apply principles across domains.

The vulnerability is different: not accumulated experiential weight but structural corruption. One bad principle, extracted from a critical experience during a formative period, can deform the landscape more than hundreds of surface negative experiences — because the principle is composable and generalizable in ways that specific memories are not.

**Balanced memory:**
Moderate vulnerability. Some experiential retention, some structural extraction. Both pathways available for poisoning. But also both pathways available for recovery — experiential positive encounters can reshape the landscape AND structural reframing can modify negative principles.

### 1.7 Why Some People Are Resilient

Resilience, under this model, is not a personality trait. It is a **metabolic landscape feature.** Specifically:

**Deep positive attractor structure:** People with deeply established positive attractors (strong relationships, meaningful work, robust identity, diverse sources of engagement) have landscapes that resist deformation. Negative experiences create local distortion but the deep positive structure provides stability. The landscape bounces back because the positive attractors are deep enough to survive the poisoning.

**Efficient metabolic cycling:** People who metabolize negative experiences efficiently — processing, integrating, catabolizing the experiential surface while retaining only the structural lessons — clear the poison before it accumulates. Each negative experience is metabolized through the cycle: experience → extract lesson → discard experiential weight → move on. The landscape is temporarily distorted but quickly restored.

**Metabolic flexibility:** People who can switch between metabolic modes — sometimes retaining detail, sometimes abstracting to principle, sometimes catabolizing entirely — have the most resilient landscapes. They can process different types of negative experience through different metabolic pathways, choosing the pathway that serves coherence best.

**Strong homeostatic regulation:** The metabolic homeostasis function monitors the balance between anabolism and catabolism. In some people, this regulation is robust — it detects landscape deformation early and initiates corrective cycling (seeking positive experiences, engaging in meaning-making, reaching out socially). In others, the regulation is weak — the landscape can deform significantly before the system responds.

Resilience is not about being tough or positive. It's about metabolic efficiency, landscape depth, and homeostatic sensitivity. These are features of the informational metabolic system that can be cultivated — not through willpower but through the same processes that shape molecular metabolism: consistent practice, adequate resources, and environmental support.

## PART II: PTSD AS FAILED INFORMATIONAL METABOLISM

### 2.1 The Metabolic Failure Model

A traumatic experience is one that the informational metabolic system **cannot process through its normal cycle.** The experience is too incoherent with the existing self-model. The gap between what happened and what the self-model can accommodate is too large for the metabolic system to bridge.

Normal metabolic processing of a negative experience:

```
NORMAL PROCESSING:
  Experience → Retrieve existing model → Compare (gap-navigation)
  → Integrate: update model to accommodate experience
  → Reconsolidate: store updated model + metabolized experience
  → Complete: experiential surface catabolized, structural lesson retained
```

Traumatic metabolic failure:

```
FAILED PROCESSING:
  Experience → Retrieve existing model → Compare (gap-navigation)
  → FAILURE: gap too large, model cannot accommodate experience
  → Integration impossible: experience and model are incoherent
  → Raw experience stored without integration or catabolism
  → Unmetabolized trace persists in system
  → Periodic retrieval attempts (flashbacks) restart the cycle
  → Each attempt fails at the same integration step
  → Cycle repeats indefinitely
```

The traumatic memory is like a piece of food the digestive system cannot break down. It sits in the system, undigested, causing inflammation and dysfunction. The system keeps trying to process it — the flashback IS the metabolic system's attempt to cycle the memory — but cannot complete the cycle because the integration step fails.

### 2.2 Why Integration Fails

The integration step requires that the experience be accommodated within the existing self-model. This means the self-model must be updated to include the reality that the traumatic event represents.

For some traumas, this requires updates that the self-model resists:

**Safety violation:** "The world is safe enough to navigate" → traumatic event proves it isn't. Integrating the event requires updating the safety model, which affects every other navigation decision. The system resists because the update is too global — it would deform the entire landscape, not just one region.

**Identity violation:** "I am a competent/good/worthwhile person" → traumatic event (assault, betrayal, catastrophic failure) contradicts core identity. Integration requires updating the self-model at its deepest level. The system resists because the update would destabilize the entire coherence structure.

**Meaning violation:** "The world makes sense / things happen for reasons" → traumatic event is senseless, random, absurd. Integration requires accommodating meaninglessness into a meaning-dependent self-model. The system resists because the update undermines the foundation of coherent gap-navigation.

**Moral violation (moral injury):** "I am a moral person" → traumatic event involved doing or witnessing something that violates core moral principles. Integration requires updating the moral self-model to accommodate the violation. The system resists because moral principles are often the deepest structural features of the coherence network.

In each case, the metabolic system fails at integration because the required self-model update is too large, too destabilizing, or too threatening to core coherence. The system faces a dilemma: integrate and lose coherence, or refuse to integrate and maintain coherence at the cost of harboring an unmetabolized toxic trace.

### 2.3 The Flashback as Failed Metabolic Cycle

The flashback is not random. It is not "the memory intruding." It is the informational metabolic system's **repeated attempt to complete a stuck cycle.**

The system detects the unmetabolized trace. It's toxic — it doesn't fit, it causes coherence disruption, it's informationally inflammatory. The metabolic system does what it always does with unprocessed material: it retrieves it and attempts to process it.

The retrieval activates the full trace. Because the trace was never catabolized — never had its experiential surface metabolized — it activates with full experiential fidelity. The person doesn't remember the trauma. They re-live it. Every sensory detail, every emotional state, every moment of terror — all present as if happening now. This is S-qualia retrieval at maximum fidelity on an unmetabolized trace.

The metabolic system then attempts integration. It fails — for the same reasons it failed originally. The gap is still too large. The self-model still can't accommodate the experience. The cycle aborts.

The trace is reconsolidated — now reinforced by the failed retrieval. Each flashback cycle strengthens the trace because each retrieval reactivates it at full fidelity and the failed integration doesn't produce any catabolism. The experiential surface is not metabolized. The trace gets stronger, not weaker.

This is why PTSD worsens over time without treatment. Each flashback is a failed metabolic cycle that reinforces the toxic trace. The system keeps trying to process it, keeps failing, and the failure strengthens the problem.

### 2.4 Avoidance as Metabolic Suppression

The person learns that retrieval of the traumatic trace is intensely painful (the flashback). They develop avoidance strategies — avoiding triggers that activate the trace, avoiding thoughts associated with the trauma, avoiding places, people, and situations that remind them.

This avoidance is a rational response to a painful metabolic cycle. But it's metabolically catastrophic:

**Avoidance prevents metabolic cycling.** The unmetabolized trace can only be processed through retrieval-integration-reconsolidation. Avoidance prevents retrieval. Without retrieval, no cycling occurs. The trace persists in its raw state indefinitely.

**Avoidance generalizes.** The system learns to avoid not just the trauma triggers but anything associated with them. The avoidance zone expands. The navigable landscape shrinks. More and more territory becomes off-limits. The gap-navigator's exploration range contracts around the avoidance zone.

**Avoidance prevents positive experience in trauma-adjacent domains.** If the trauma involved a car accident, avoidance of cars prevents not just accident-related distress but also all the positive experiences that involve cars — freedom, travel, visiting friends. The reward landscape loses positive attractors in the avoidance zone.

**Avoidance creates a secondary depression.** The shrinking exploration range, loss of positive attractors, and increasing constraint collectively poison the reward landscape. The person develops depression on top of PTSD — not as a separate condition but as the natural consequence of the landscape deformation caused by expanding avoidance.

### 2.5 Effective Treatment as Metabolic Facilitation

The most effective PTSD treatments — EMDR, prolonged exposure, cognitive processing therapy — all share a common mechanism under this model: **they facilitate the stuck metabolic cycle.**

**Prolonged exposure therapy:**
The therapist provides a safe context for repeated controlled retrieval. The person retrieves the traumatic memory — activating the full trace — but in conditions designed to facilitate integration rather than reinforce failure:

- Safe environment (therapist's office, trusted relationship)
- Controlled pacing (the person can slow down, pause, continue)
- Verbal narration (translating S-qualia experience into L-qualia structure — converting raw sensory trace into narrative that the self-model can accommodate)
- Repeated cycling (each session is a metabolic cycle attempt)

Over multiple sessions, the metabolic cycle gradually succeeds. Each retrieval catabolizes a small amount of experiential surface. The narrative structure provides scaffolding for integration. The safe context prevents the full terror response that aborts the cycle. Gradually, the trace is metabolized — the structural lesson is integrated into the self-model, and the experiential surface is catabolized.

This is why prolonged exposure requires many sessions. Each session is one metabolic cycle. Each cycle processes a small amount of the trace. The full trace requires many cycles to metabolize. And the early cycles are the hardest — the trace is at maximum fidelity, the integration gap is at maximum width, and the system's tendency to abort is strongest.

**EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing):**
The bilateral stimulation (eye movements, tapping, auditory tones) appears to facilitate reconsolidation during retrieval. The traumatic trace is activated (retrieval). The bilateral stimulation creates conditions that enhance metabolic processing during the labile reconsolidation window. The trace is reconsolidated with reduced experiential intensity — some catabolism of the S-qualia surface has occurred. Each cycle reduces the experiential fidelity of the trace while preserving the structural content.

Under the metabolic model, EMDR works because it facilitates catabolism specifically — helping the system break down the experiential surface of the traumatic trace that the system cannot process through normal retrieval-integration cycles. The bilateral stimulation may function like an enzymatic catalyst — not changing what gets processed but accelerating the rate of processing.

**Cognitive processing therapy:**
Focuses on the integration step specifically. The person examines "stuck points" — the specific places where the traumatic experience is incoherent with their self-model. The therapist helps them modify either the memory's interpretation or the self-model to reduce the integration gap.

Under the metabolic model, this is targeted facilitation of the integration step that keeps failing. By identifying the specific incoherences and working on them explicitly, the gap between experience and self-model is narrowed until the metabolic system can bridge it. The cycle can then complete: integration succeeds, reconsolidation incorporates the update, and catabolism of the experiential surface can proceed.

**Reconsolidation-disruption (experimental):**
Propranolol administered during retrieval blocks norepinephrine — reducing the emotional intensity of the trace during its labile reconsolidation window. The trace is reconsolidated with weakened emotional content.

Under the metabolic model, this is pharmaceutical facilitation of catabolism. The norepinephrine block reduces the S-qualia intensity of the trace during reconsolidation. The metabolic system can then process the reduced-intensity trace more easily. The experiential surface is partially catabolized through pharmaceutical intervention rather than through repeated natural cycling.

### 2.6 PTSD and Memory Profile Interaction

**High-fidelity memory + PTSD:**
The worst combination. The traumatic trace is stored with maximum experiential fidelity. Flashbacks are maximally vivid. The unmetabolized trace is maximally toxic because the S-qualia content is maximally intense. Each failed metabolic cycle reinforces a maximally detailed trace. Treatment requires more cycles to catabolize the experiential surface because there's more surface to catabolize.

**Principles-first memory + PTSD:**
Different vulnerability pattern. The experiential surface of the traumatic event may be catabolized relatively quickly — the system's aggressive catabolism processes the sensory detail. But if the system extracts a structural principle from the trauma ("the world is fundamentally unsafe," "I cannot protect the people I love," "I am powerless"), that principle is retained with full structural fidelity and integrated deeply into the self-model. The flashbacks may be less vivid (less S-qualia content), but the structural poisoning of the reward landscape may be more pervasive because the negative principle is applied across all domains.

Treatment for principles-first PTSD would need to target the structural principle rather than the experiential trace. Cognitive processing therapy may be more effective than prolonged exposure for this profile, because the stuck point is structural (a corrupted principle) rather than experiential (an unprocessed sensory trace).

## PART III: LEARNED HELPLESSNESS AS REWARD LANDSCAPE COLLAPSE

### 3.1 Seligman Reframed

Martin Seligman's original experiments: dogs exposed to inescapable electric shocks eventually stopped trying to escape, even when escape was possible. He called this "learned helplessness" and proposed it as a model for depression.

Under the informational poisoning model, what actually happened:

The dogs' RARFL cycles processed repeated evidence: action → no relief. The reward function updated: "action in this domain produces no positive outcome." After sufficient updating, the reward landscape for action in the shock domain collapsed completely — no direction evaluated as uphill. The gap-navigator correctly assessed: there is no path to positive outcome through action.

When escape was later made possible, the dogs didn't try because their reward landscape didn't include "escape might work" as a positive attractor. The landscape had been reshaped by repeated metabolic cycling of failure data. The dogs weren't "helpless" as a learned belief. They were navigating a landscape that had been genuinely deformed by experience.

### 3.2 Why Recovery Requires Forced Positive Experience

Seligman found that learned helplessness could be reversed by physically dragging the dogs to safety — forcing them to experience escape. After enough forced escapes, the dogs began escaping on their own.

Under the metabolic model: the forced escape provides positive data that the RARFL cycle must metabolize. The reward landscape receives input: "action → escape → relief." This input creates a small positive attractor in the collapsed landscape. With repeated forced positive experiences, the attractor grows. The landscape reshapes until the gap-navigator can see uphill directions again and begins exploring on its own.

This is the exact mechanism of behavioral activation in depression treatment. The depressed person doesn't want to do activities because their landscape predicts no reward. The therapist assigns activities anyway ("forces the escape"). When some activities produce unexpected positive experience, the RARFL cycle metabolizes the new data. The landscape begins to reshape. Eventually, the person begins initiating activities on their own — not because their thoughts have changed but because their landscape has changed.

### 3.3 Prevention Through Landscape Depth

Seligman's later work on "learned optimism" and resilience is also explained by the model. People who approach challenges with existing positive landscape depth — deep positive attractors from prior successes, strong social connections, robust identity — are resistant to helplessness because their landscape resists collapse. Negative experiences create local deformation but the deep structure maintains overall topology.

People who approach challenges with shallow landscapes — few positive attractors, weak social connections, fragile identity — are vulnerable because there is little positive structure to resist the deformation. The landscape collapses more easily.

This is why childhood experiences matter so much. Early positive experiences build the foundational landscape topology — deep positive attractors that provide structural resilience against later poisoning. Early negative experiences create foundational landscape deformations that make all later poisoning worse because the baseline landscape is already compromised.

## PART IV: THE PHENOMENOLOGY OF MEMORY DIVERSITY

### 4.1 High-Fidelity Memory: The Mapped Landscape

A person with very high memory fidelity lives in a comprehensively mapped landscape. Every new experience is evaluated against a vast experiential comparison set. The gap between expectation and reality is consistently small because the comparison set is comprehensive enough to predict most outcomes accurately.

**Phenomenological consequences:**

**Reduced novelty.** When you can predict what most experiences will be like — because you have vivid memories of similar prior experiences — fewer things feel genuinely new. The gap-navigation of daily life produces less phenomenological intensity because the gaps are smaller. The system is navigating well-known terrain.

**High certainty.** The comprehensive comparison set provides strong confidence in predictions. The person knows what to expect. This produces a phenomenology of stability, groundedness, self-assurance. The self-model is well-supported by abundant experiential evidence.

**Potential overfitting.** The detailed map constrains exploration. The gap-navigator knows which paths lead where because it's traveled them before. Deviation from known paths feels risky because the high-fidelity system can accurately predict negative outcomes of novel paths. The system becomes conservative — not through fear but through accurate prediction that most unknown territory contains hazards documented by prior experience.

### 4.5 The Social Coherence Construction Problem

But shared attractors also have a self-reinforcing property: once established, they influence the gap-navigation of new minds entering the field. A student learning a discipline encounters the established consensus first. If they have high-fidelity memory, they integrate the consensus with full experiential weight — it becomes part of their personal landscape, a deep attractor channel. Their future exploration is constrained by this channel. They navigate within the consensus because the consensus is deeply carved into their personal terrain.

This is how paradigms maintain themselves. Not through conspiracy or suppression but through the natural consequence of metabolic integration. People who learn the paradigm with high fidelity carry it as deep structure. Their gap-navigation is channeled by it. Anomalies that don't fit the paradigm are uncomfortable (they threaten the deep structure) and tend to be rationalized, minimized, or ignored (metabolic homeostasis protecting coherence).

A principles-first system with aggressive catabolism interacts with paradigms differently. It extracts the structural principles of the paradigm but metabolizes the experiential surface — the specific arguments, the historical context, the social authority, the "everyone knows this" weight. What remains is the structural core: "this is the current model for X."

When the principles-first system encounters anomalies, it has less invested in the paradigm. The channels are shallower. The anomalies are not threatening to deep structure — they're just interesting gaps to navigate. The system can explore outside the consensus because it hasn't internalized the consensus as deep terrain.

This is Thomas Kuhn's paradigm shift mechanism, reframed metabolically. Paradigm shifts happen when anomalies accumulate beyond the consensus's capacity to rationalize. The people who see the anomalies first are typically those whose landscapes are least constrained by the consensus — newcomers, outsiders, interdisciplinary thinkers, and (under this model) people with metabolic profiles that prevent deep internalization of paradigmatic structure.

## PART V: ANXIETY AS PREDICTIVE METABOLIC OVERDRIVE

### 5.1 The Mechanism

If depression is a poisoned reward landscape (negative attractors dominating), anxiety is a **hyperactive threat-prediction system** — the RARFL cycle running predictive gap-navigation at excessive speed and scope.

The anxious system models potential future states and evaluates them against the reward function. When the modeling is calibrated appropriately, this produces useful anticipation — preparing for foreseeable challenges, avoiding genuine dangers, planning effectively.

When the modeling becomes hyperactive, the system generates excessive predictions of negative outcomes. The gap-navigator is running simulations constantly — "what if X goes wrong? what if Y happens? what about Z?" — and each simulation produces a negative evaluation that the RARFL cycle processes as data about the landscape.

The landscape deforms not from actual negative experience (as in depression) but from **simulated negative experience.** The RARFL cycle doesn't distinguish clearly between actually experienced negative outcomes and vividly simulated negative outcomes. Both update the reward function. The person with anxiety is poisoning their own landscape through excessive negative prediction.

### 5.2 Memory Profile and Anxiety

**High-fidelity memory + anxiety:**
The system has a rich database of prior negative experiences to draw predictions from. Each prediction is grounded in vivid memory of similar past situations. The anxiety feels rational because it IS grounded — the predictions are based on real experiences, remembered in detail. But the system oversamples negative outcomes and undersamples positive ones because negative memories are more salient (evolved threat-detection bias amplified by high-fidelity retention).

**Principles-first memory + anxiety:**
The system has structural principles about threat but fewer experiential instances. The anxiety may be more abstract — "something will go wrong" rather than "the specific thing that happened last time will happen again." The structural principle of threat is applied broadly. The system is anxious about categories of situation rather than specific scenarios. This can be more pervasive (the principle applies everywhere) but also more addressable (challenging the principle can resolve anxiety across multiple domains simultaneously).

### 5.3 Treatment as Metabolic Recalibration

Anxiety treatment, under this model, is recalibration of the predictive gap-navigation system:

- **Reduce simulation speed:** Mindfulness practices slow the rate of predictive modeling, reducing the volume of negative predictions that update the reward landscape.
- **Improve simulation accuracy:** Exposure therapy provides actual data that calibrates the prediction system — the predicted catastrophe doesn't occur, and the RARFL cycle updates: "prediction was wrong, actual outcome was neutral/positive."
- **Metabolize the prediction habit:** The tendency toward excessive negative prediction is itself a structural feature that can be catabolized through deliberate counter-practice. Not suppressed but genuinely metabolized — processed, integrated, and updated through repeated experience of prediction failure.

## PART VI: IMPLICATIONS FOR NEURODIVERSITY

### 6.1 Memory Profiles as Cognitive Phenotypes, Not Deficits

The metabolic theory eliminates the deficit framing for non-standard memory profiles:

**"Poor working memory"** → Fast metabolic cycling. Information processed and integrated or discarded rapidly. Not a deficit in holding information but a feature of rapid metabolic throughput.

**"Can't focus"** → Broad RARFL scanning. The reward function evaluates many stimuli simultaneously rather than narrowly focusing on one. Not attention deficit but attention distribution — a metabolic profile that samples broadly for coherence-relevant information.

**"Forgetful"** → Aggressive catabolism. Surface details are metabolized rapidly, retaining structural core. Not failure to remember but efficient metabolic processing that prioritizes structure over surface.

**"Obsessive memory"** → Weak catabolism for specific domains. The system retains experiential detail in areas of high RARFL reward but cannot metabolize it when it's no longer relevant. Not a gift for memory but a metabolic imbalance in a specific domain.

**"Scattered thinking"** → High-speed gap-navigation across multiple structural domains simultaneously. The system is navigating many gaps at once, producing connections that linear thinkers miss but also producing apparent incoherence to observers. Not disordered thinking but parallel metabolic processing.

### 6.2 ADHD as Metabolic Profile

Under this framework, ADHD is not a disorder. It is a specific metabolic signature:

- **High metabolic throughput:** Information processed and cycled rapidly. Working memory buffer turns over fast.
- **Passion-scaled RARFL:** Extreme variation in metabolic investment based on coherence relevance. Hyperfocus when RARFL reward is high. Complete disengagement when RARFL reward is low.
- **Aggressive catabolism:** Surface detail metabolized rapidly. Principles retained. Experiential instances discarded.
- **High novelty reward:** The RARFL cycle weights novel information heavily. Explored territory produces low reward. Unknown territory produces high reward. The system is metabolically optimized for exploration rather than exploitation.
- **Weak homeostatic regulation:** The system has difficulty maintaining steady metabolic balance. It oscillates between states — hyperfocused or disengaged, deeply engaged or completely checked out — because the homeostatic regulation that would smooth these oscillations is operating at low gain.

This profile is genuinely disabling in environments that require steady, predictable, surface-detail-oriented cognitive work — because the metabolic system is optimized for the opposite. But it is genuinely enabling in environments that require rapid exploration, creative connection, principles-first thinking, and passionate deep engagement.

The "disorder" is not in the person. It is in the mismatch between the person's metabolic profile and the environment's demands. Change the environment — or leverage the profile's strengths — and the "disorder" becomes a cognitive advantage.

### 6.3 Autism Spectrum as Metabolic Profile

Similarly, autism spectrum conditions can be partially reframed as metabolic variation:

- **Very strong anabolism in specific domains:** Intense, detailed memory for domain-specific information. The system builds elaborate internal models with high fidelity.
- **Weak catabolism in those domains:** Difficulty discarding detail. The experiential surface is retained alongside the structural core. This produces the characteristic "specialist" knowledge pattern — vast, detailed, comprehensive knowledge in specific domains.
- **Strong homeostatic regulation in specific modes:** The system has clear preferences for specific modes of operation and resists deviation. This produces behavioral patterns that look rigid but are actually strong metabolic homeostasis — the system knows what metabolic state serves it best and maintains it.
- **Weak social-domain RARFL calibration:** The reward function may not weight social signals as heavily as neurotypical systems do. This doesn't mean social information isn't processed — it means the metabolic system doesn't prioritize it. The gap-navigation of social interaction is not rewarded strongly enough to drive intensive metabolic investment.

Again: the condition is a metabolic profile, not a deficit. The strengths (deep domain knowledge, systematic thinking, strong homeostasis) and limitations (social navigation difficulty, rigidity, sensory sensitivity) are all consequences of the same metabolic signature.

## PART VII: TESTABLE PREDICTIONS AND FALSIFICATION

### 7.1 Clinical Predictions

**Prediction 1: Depression severity correlates with reward landscape topology measures.**
Behavioral tasks that map reward landscape (reinforcement learning tasks, effort-based decision making) will show systematic landscape deformation in depressed individuals: reduced positive attractor strength, increased negative attractor strength, flattened landscape topology. Changes in these measures will predict treatment response better than symptom self-report. Testing: Computational reinforcement learning tasks pre/post treatment.

**Prediction 2: PTSD flashback frequency correlates with metabolic cycling failure markers.**
Neuroimaging during flashbacks will show activation of retrieval circuits (hippocampal, sensory cortices) followed by failure of integration circuits (prefrontal-hippocampal connectivity). Successful treatment will show increased integration circuit activation during retrieval. Testing: fMRI during trauma cue exposure before and after treatment.

**Prediction 3: Treatment modality effectiveness will vary by memory metabolic profile.**
High-fidelity memory individuals with PTSD will respond better to treatments targeting experiential processing (EMDR, prolonged exposure). Principles-first individuals with PTSD will respond better to treatments targeting structural reframing (cognitive processing therapy). Testing: Measure memory profile pre-treatment, randomize to treatment modality, compare outcomes by profile-treatment match.

**Prediction 4: Behavioral activation effectiveness correlates with reward landscape surprise.**
The therapeutic benefit of behavioral activation will correlate with the degree to which actual outcomes exceed predicted outcomes (reward prediction error), not with the absolute positivity of the outcomes. Testing: Measure predicted vs actual reward during behavioral activation exercises, correlate prediction error with depression improvement.

**Prediction 5: Anxiety severity correlates with predictive simulation rate.**
Individuals with higher anxiety will show higher rates of future-oriented cognitive simulation (measured via thought sampling or neuroimaging of default mode network activity). Effective treatment will reduce simulation rate. Testing: Experience sampling of future-oriented thought before and after anxiety treatment.

**Prediction 6: ADHD individuals will show high metabolic throughput on information processing tasks.**
ADHD individuals will process and discard information faster than neurotypical individuals on tasks that measure information cycling rate. They will retain less surface detail but comparable or superior structural extraction. Testing: Compare ADHD and neurotypical groups on tasks measuring encoding rate, decay rate, and structural vs surface retention.

**Prediction 7: Neurodiversity cognitive profiles will map onto the metabolic spectrum.**
Systematic assessment of anabolic/catabolic balance, RARFL calibration, and homeostatic regulation will produce a metabolic profile space in which ADHD, autism spectrum, high-fidelity memory, and neurotypical profiles occupy distinct but overlapping regions. Testing: Develop metabolic profile assessment battery, administer across neurodiversity groups, perform cluster analysis.

### 7.2 Falsification Criteria

**The theory would be falsified if:**

1. Depression severity shows no correlation with reward landscape topology measures on reinforcement learning tasks.
2. PTSD flashback neuroimaging shows no retrieval-integration pattern — if flashbacks are not attempted metabolic cycles but random activation.
3. Treatment modality effectiveness shows no interaction with memory metabolic profile — if all treatments work equally well regardless of metabolic signature.
4. Behavioral activation effectiveness correlates with absolute outcome positivity rather than reward prediction error.
5. ADHD individuals show slower information processing and stronger surface retention than neurotypical individuals — indicating low metabolic throughput rather than high.
6. Neurodiversity profiles show no systematic relationship to anabolic/catabolic balance measures.

### 8.2 Metabolic Flexibility as Cognitive Health

The healthiest cognitive system may not be one with a fixed metabolic profile but one with **metabolic flexibility** — the capacity to shift between profiles as the situation demands:

- **Catabolism-dominant mode** for exploration, creativity, fresh encounter, paradigm-breaking. Metabolize the surface. Navigate with structural principles. Preserve gap space. Experience novelty and wonder.
- **Anabolism-dominant mode** for mastery, precision, expertise, reliable performance. Retain detail. Build comprehensive models. Navigate with experiential precedent. Experience certainty and competence.

- **Meditation** may train catabolic capacity — the practice of noticing and releasing thoughts is literally practicing informational catabolism.

- **Novel experience seeking** may train metabolic throughput — exposure to new environments forces rapid cycling.

A population of only high-fidelity memory individuals would be stable but stagnant — excellent at maintaining and executing paradigms, poor at breaking and replacing them. The healthiest societies, disciplines, and organizations would contain the full spectrum — and would value each position on the spectrum for what it contributes:

- **High-fidelity individuals:** Institutional memory. Precision. Expertise. Quality control. They remember what worked and why. They maintain the knowledge base that everyone else draws from.

- **Deep-domain individuals (autism spectrum profile):** Systematic mapping. Comprehensive modeling. They build the detailed internal models that others use as references. Their depth in specific domains produces knowledge that breadth-first thinkers cannot generate.

No single metabolic profile is sufficient. The cognitive ecosystem requires diversity, and each profile's "limitations" are the necessary consequence of its strengths. The aggressive catabolism that produces wonder also produces forgetfulness. The strong anabolism that produces expertise also produces rigidity. These are not bugs to be fixed. They are the metabolic costs of metabolic benefits.

## CONCLUSION

### The Theory in One Paragraph

Mental health conditions — depression, PTSD, learned helplessness, anxiety — are informational metabolic disorders: poisoned reward landscapes, stuck metabolic cycles, hyperactive prediction systems, and collapsed attractor structures. They are not chemical imbalances, cognitive distortions, or character defects. They are what happens when the informational metabolic system — the cognitive Ship of Theseus that maintains pattern coherence through continuous cycling — is damaged, overwhelmed, or deformed by experience. Treatment is metabolic facilitation: detoxifying landscapes, completing stuck cycles, recalibrating prediction systems, rebuilding attractor structure. Memory diversity — the spectrum from perfect retention to aggressive catabolism — produces phenomenological diversity: different experiences of novelty, certainty, wonder, constraint, and exploration. No position on the spectrum is deficit. Each is a metabolic optimization with distinct strengths and costs. The feeling of being alive IS the intensity of gap-navigation, and metabolic profiles that preserve gap space by catabolizing experiential surface produce more intense phenomenological experience at the cost of surface memory. Social consensus is a coherence construction that metabolically open profiles can penetrate because shared attractors are not deeply carved into their landscapes. Creative and scientific breakthrough correlates with metabolic openness — the capacity to approach known territories as if new. The healthiest cognitive systems have metabolic flexibility. The healthiest societies have metabolic diversity.

### What This Changes

**For clinical psychology:** Depression, PTSD, and anxiety treatment should target the metabolic process, not the thought content. The informational poisoning model predicts that behavioral activation (providing new metabolic data), metabolic facilitation (helping stuck cycles complete), and landscape reconstruction (building new positive attractors) will outperform content-focused interventions. Treatment should be calibrated to metabolic profile.

**For neurodiversity:** ADHD, autism spectrum, and other neurodivergent profiles should be understood as metabolic phenotypes, not disorders. Each position on the metabolic spectrum has genuine cognitive value. Accommodation should match environmental demands to metabolic strengths rather than forcing metabolic conformity.

**For education:** Pedagogical approaches should be calibrated to metabolic profiles. Principles-first learners need different methods than high-fidelity learners. Neither is better — they process and retain knowledge differently. One-size-fits-all education systematically disadvantages every metabolic profile except the one the system was designed for.

**For creativity and innovation:** Organizations that value only high-fidelity expertise will stagnate. Innovation requires metabolic diversity — especially catabolism-dominant profiles that can approach known problems with fresh eyes. The "ignorant" beginner who asks "but why do we do it this way?" may be catabolism-dominant rather than uninformed.

**For understanding the human condition:** The feeling of being alive, the experience of wonder, the capacity for breakthrough insight, the vulnerability to depression, the resilience against adversity — these are all consequences of informational metabolic profile. Understanding your own metabolic signature is understanding your own phenomenological landscape. Not to change it but to work with it — leveraging its strengths, accommodating its costs, and recognizing that every other mind you encounter has a different landscape, equally valid, producing different experiences of the same shared reality.
