  "Crosses the BBB and protects
   neurons by reducing ROS,
   modulating mitochondrial function,
   inhibiting apoptosis."
  "Some human pilot trials: improved
   EEG profiles, reduced seizure
   frequency, decreased oxidative
   biomarkers."

MECHANISM B — SLEEP ARCHITECTURE:
  Post-ictal fatigue is profound.
  The post-ictal period is
  characterised by sleep pressure —
  the navigator is exhausted
  and requires deep sleep to
  begin recovery.
  Sleep is itself neuroprotective
  — during slow-wave sleep, the
  glymphatic system flushes
  inflammatory waste products
  from the brain extracellular
  space.

  glymphatic clearance = more
  residual inflammatory products

  sleep architecture post-ictally.
  It accelerates entry into
  restorative slow-wave sleep.

  mechanism.

  Melatonin is produced in the
  pineal gland and has complex
  interactions with brainstem

  serotonin — they share the
  same metabolic pathway.
  Adequate melatonin levels at
  night support normal serotonergic
  tone in the brainstem.
  This is the same brainstem
  serotonergic system identified

  interaction.

COST: $0.10-$0.20 per dose.
Almost nothing.

  This is a clinically addressable
  gap with near-zero cost.

  4000-5000 IU daily (higher

  As noted: enzyme-inducing AEDs

  the required dose, not reduces it.
  No dangerous interaction.

Arguably the highest return

### OTC COMPOUND 9: RESVERATROL

```
AVAILABLE: Supplement stores,
pharmacies, Amazon.
COST: $20-$40 per month (250-500mg).
Per dose: $0.65-$1.30.
WINDOW: T+1h to T+8h (partial)
OPERATION: SIRT1 activation →
indirect mTOR inhibition →
partial structural consolidation
reduction.

THIS IS THE MOST INDIRECT
COMPOUND ON THIS LIST.
IT IS INCLUDED BECAUSE OF THE
MECHANISM — NOT AS A STRONG
RECOMMENDATION.

MECHANISM:
  Resveratrol activates SIRT1
  (a sirtuin deacetylase).
  SIRT1 activation inhibits
  mTOR indirectly.
  mTOR inhibition (Window 4 target)
  reduces structural consolidation
  of post-seizure synaptic changes.

  This is a VERY indirect path
  to the Window 4 target.
  Rapamycin (prescription) hits
  mTOR directly.
  Resveratrol hits it through
  SIRT1 → multiple intermediaries.
  The effect on mTOR will be
  partial and weaker than
  rapamycin by a significant margin.

  HOWEVER:
  Resveratrol also has independent
  antioxidant effects and has
  been shown to reduce seizure-
  induced neuronal damage in
  animal models directly.
  It has multiple protective
  mechanisms that are not all
  through mTOR.

  It also inhibits NF-κB —
  the same transcription factor
  that drives neuroinflammation —
  giving it partial Window 5
  activity alongside its indirect
  Window 4 activity.

BIOAVAILABILITY PROBLEM:
  Resveratrol is poorly absorbed
  orally and rapidly metabolised.
  Standard doses achieve low
  plasma concentrations.
  Micronised or liposomal forms
  improve bioavailability.
  Trans-resveratrol is the active
  form (not cis-resveratrol).
  Quality matters significantly.

  THIS IS THE CAVEAT THAT
  LIMITS RESVERATROL:
  The mechanism is sound.
  The bioavailability at OTC
  doses is uncertain.
  It may be doing something
  useful. The evidence is not
  strong enough to say clearly.

DOSING: 250-500mg trans-resveratrol
with a meal (fat improves absorption).
Post-ictal: take with T+1h meal.

COST: $0.65-$1.30 per dose.
Not expensive. Evidence weaker
than the other compounds here.
Include if cost is not a concern.
Deprioritise if budget is tight.
```

### OTC COMPOUND 10: CURCUMIN
### (WITH Piperine or Liposomal Form)

```
AVAILABLE: Supplement stores,
health food stores, Amazon.
COST: $20-$35 per month
(standard with piperine).
Per dose: $0.65-$1.15.
WINDOW: 5 (T+6 to T+24h) via
NF-κB inhibition
OPERATION: Neuroinflammation
cascade modulation through
NF-κB pathway.

MECHANISM:
  Curcumin is the active compound
  in turmeric.
  It inhibits NF-κB — the master
  transcription factor that drives
  IL-1β, TNF-α, COX-2, and
  multiple other neuroinflammatory
  mediators.
  This is upstream of both COX-2
  (targeted by NSAIDs) and IL-1β
  (targeted by anakinra).
  Blocking NF-κB hits the whole
  inflammatory cascade at its
  transcriptional root.

  GEOMETRIC SIGNIFICANCE:
  NSAIDs block one downstream
  product (prostaglandins).
  Anakinra blocks one cytokine (IL-1β).
  Curcumin blocks the TRANSCRIPTION
  FACTOR that drives the whole
  inflammatory cascade.
  At the mechanism level, this
  is the more comprehensive target.
  The limitation is bioavailability,
  not the mechanism.

THE BIOAVAILABILITY PROBLEM:
  Plain curcumin from turmeric
  or standard curcumin capsules
  has extremely poor oral
  bioavailability — less than 1%
  reaches systemic circulation.
  The brain sees almost nothing.

  THE SOLUTIONS THAT WORK:
  1. Curcumin with piperine (black
     pepper extract, BioPerine):
     Piperine inhibits the enzyme
     that rapidly metabolises curcumin
     in the gut.
     This increases curcumin
     bioavailability by up to 2000%.
     This is the minimum acceptable
     form for any meaningful effect.
     Available in most combined
     curcumin/piperine supplements.

  2. Liposomal curcumin:
     Encased in phospholipid
     vesicles that protect curcumin
     from gut metabolism and
     facilitate direct absorption.
     Best bioavailability of OTC forms.
     More expensive.

  3. BCM-95 or CurcuWIN or
     Meriva (phytosome form):
     Patented enhanced bioavailability
     curcumin formulations.
     Look for these on labels.

  PLAIN TURMERIC POWDER:
  Essentially useless for CNS
  effects at normal doses.
  Fine in food. Not adequate
  as a therapeutic supplement
  for this purpose.

DOSING:
  Post-ictal: 500-1000mg curcumin
  with piperine at T+6 to T+8h.
  Take with a fat-containing meal
  (fat also enhances absorption).
  Daily baseline: 500mg once daily
  for ongoing anti-inflammatory
  support.

AED INTERACTIONS:
  Curcumin may inhibit CYP3A4
  and CYP2C9 — the same enzymes
  that metabolise carbamazepine,
  phenytoin, and diazepam.
  This could theoretically increase
  AED levels.
  This is a real interaction
  concern and MUST be discussed
  with a neurologist before use.
  It is not a reason to avoid
  curcumin — it is a reason
  to monitor AED levels if
  curcumin supplementation begins.
  Monitoring solves this.

COST: $0.65-$1.15 per dose
(with piperine form).
Liposomal: $2.00-$3.00 per dose.
```

## THE HONEST ASSESSMENT —
## WHAT THIS DOES AND DOES NOT DO

```
WHAT IT DOES:

  1. Addresses Window 5 (neuroinflammation)
     meaningfully. The naproxen +
     omega-3 + curcumin combination
     hits the same window from
     three different angles.
     This is the best OTC coverage
     in the protocol.

  2. Addresses the oxidative damage
     cascade meaningfully.
     NAC + melatonin + resveratrol
     + omega-3 provide overlapping
     antioxidant coverage from
     multiple mechanisms.
     Seizure-induced oxidative
     damage is substantially
     addressed by this combination.

  3. Addresses BASELINE landscape
     deterioration meaningfully.
     Daily D3 + magnesium + omega-3
     works continuously on the
     landscape between seizures.
     The 2024 RCT data showing
     30% seizure reduction from
     D3 alone in drug-resistant
     epilepsy is clinical evidence
     that this is not trivial.

  4. Partially addresses Window 1
     (calcium/NMDA) via magnesium.
     Not as effective as prescription
     memantine — but it is the
     endogenous mechanism that
     memantine mimics, so it is
     the right target, less potently.

  5. Costs $38-$56 per month at
     maximum implementation.
     Available without a prescription.
     Available today.


WHAT IT DOES NOT DO:

  1. Window 3 (TrkB-PLCγ1 —
     the primary basin deepening step).
     NO OTC SOLUTION EXISTS.
     This is the most important
     window and the OTC arsenal
     cannot reach it.
     This is the honest gap.
     pY816 peptide is required.
     PIC-001 small molecule
     is required.
     Until they exist: this
     window is unaddressed by
     either OTC or prescription
     compounds readily available.

  2. Direct mTOR inhibition (Window 4)
     at the potency needed.
     Resveratrol's indirect effect
     on mTOR via SIRT1 is a
     pale shadow of rapamycin.
     This window requires rapamycin
     (prescription) for real coverage.
     The OTC protocol provides
     partial, weak coverage here.

  3. Replace antiepileptic medications.
     None of these compounds are
     antiepileptic drugs.
     None should replace a prescribed
     AED regimen.
     This protocol runs alongside
     standard treatment —
     in the window between seizures
     that standard treatment
     does not address.

  4. Provide the precision of
     the prescription protocol.
     The prescription protocol
     (OC-EPILEPSY-008) is more
     precisely targeted at higher
     potency. The OTC protocol
     is the accessible, affordable,
     available-now version that
     covers the ground that can
     be covered without a prescription.
```
