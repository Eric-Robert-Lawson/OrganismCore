## The Virus's Structural Vulnerabilities

```
VULNERABILITY 1 — TRANSPORT DEPENDENCE:
  The virus cannot move through the
  CNS by itself.
  It requires axonal transport
  machinery — specifically retrograde
  dynein-mediated transport — to
  move from neuron to neuron.
  It has no independent mobility.
  It cannot jump synapses without
  using the synapse's own release
  machinery.
  It is entirely dependent on the
  host's infrastructure for spread.

  This means:
  Disrupt the transport machinery
  during active spread → arrest
  viral propagation through the CNS.

  The virus cannot adapt to this
  in real time.
  Its spread mechanism is fixed.
  The intervention point is structural
  and non-negotiable for the virus.

VULNERABILITY 2 — IMMUNE EVASION
IS INCOMPLETE:
  The virus suppresses innate immune
  responses within neurons through
  P protein interference with STAT
  signalling and IFN pathways.
  It also suppresses microglial
  activation in early stages.

  But the suppression is not total.
  It is sufficient for the virus
  to establish itself in normal
  conditions where the immune
  response is not amplified above
  the suppression threshold.

  If the immune response is
  amplified beyond what the P protein
  can suppress — through exogenous
  IFN and immune activation — the
  virus's evasion is overwhelmed.

  The virus has no mechanism to
  upregulate its own immune evasion
  in response to exogenous amplification.
  The P protein concentration is fixed
  by the viral load at the time
  of intervention.
  Exceed its suppression capacity:
  the immune system reaches the virus.

VULNERABILITY 3 — REPLICATION IS
NEUROLOGICALLY LOCALISED:
  The virus replicates within neurons.
  Not in glia. Not in blood. Not in CSF.
  In neurons.
  Specifically in the neuronal soma
  and along the axon.

  This means viral clearance requires
  the immune system to reach
  intraneuronal compartments.
  This is harder than clearing
  blood-borne pathogens.
  But it also means the virus is
  not systemically distributed.
  It is in a specific anatomical
  distribution that follows the
  spread pattern from the bite site.

  The distribution is partially
  predictable from the bite location,
  the time elapsed, and the
  symptom profile.
  The predictable distribution
  allows targeted therapeutic delivery.

VULNERABILITY 4 — THE LANDSCAPE
INVERSION IS PHARMACOLOGICALLY
REVERSIBLE:
  The agitation, hydrophobia,
  aerophobia, and autonomic
  instability are not caused by
  direct viral destruction of neurons.
  They are caused by viral disruption
  of neurotransmitter systems —
  specifically:
    Glutamatergic excitotoxicity.
    GABAergic suppression.
    Serotonergic disruption.
    Cholinergic dysregulation.
    Autonomic circuit destabilisation.

  These are pharmacologically
  modifiable states.
  The neurotransmitter systems
  are disrupted, not destroyed.
  They can be temporarily corrected
  pharmacologically while the
  antiviral and immune response
  address the underlying cause.

  Suppressing the excitotoxicity
  does not cure rabies.
  But it stops the landscape
  inversion from killing the navigator
  through excitotoxic neuronal death
  while the virus is being cleared.

  Neuroprotection is not treatment.
  It is holding the instrument
  intact while treatment proceeds.

### Layer 1 — Arrest Viral Spread (Transport Disruption)

```
TARGET:
  Dynein-mediated retrograde axonal
  transport — the mechanism the virus
  uses to move through the CNS.

MECHANISM:
  The virus requires dynein motor
  proteins to travel retrograde
  along axons. Without dynein
  function the virus is stranded
  in whatever neuron it currently
  occupies. It cannot spread.

AGENT:
  Ciliobrevin D — a dynein inhibitor.
  Demonstrated to inhibit rabies
  virus transport in neuronal
  cultures.
  Not currently used in clinical
  rabies treatment.

  Additionally: microtubule
  stabilisation reduces the
  efficiency of both viral transport
  and viral genome release.
  Taxanes have demonstrated antiviral
  activity against rabies in
  experimental systems.

ROUTE:
  Intrathecal or intraventricular
  delivery for CNS penetration.
  Systemic administration has
  limited BBB penetration.
  Direct CSF delivery reaches
  the compartment where the virus is.

TIMING:
  Immediately on diagnosis.
  Viral spread continues at
  approximately 50-100mm/day
  along axons.
  Every hour of uninhibited transport
  extends the viral distribution.
  This layer has the shortest
  therapeutic window.

GOAL:
  Arrest the spread.
  Do not need to kill the virus
  already present.
  Stop the advance.
  Define the current distribution
  as the maximum distribution.
  Allow subsequent layers to clear
  what is currently present.
```

### Layer 2 — Overwhelm Immune Evasion (IFN Amplification)

```
TARGET:
  The P protein — viral phosphoprotein
  that blocks STAT1/STAT2 signalling
  and suppresses type I IFN response.

MECHANISM:
  P protein blocks IFN signalling at
  two points:
    Prevents IRF3 activation upstream.
    Blocks nuclear translocation of
    STAT1/STAT2 downstream.
  If exogenous IFN is administered
  at concentrations that exceed
  P protein suppression capacity:
    The downstream IFN-stimulated
    genes are activated despite
    P protein interference.
    The antiviral state is established
    in neurons surrounding the
    infected zone.
    Viral spread into these neurons
    is impaired.

AGENTS:
  IFN-β — preferred over IFN-α
  for CNS application.
  Higher CNS penetrance.
  More potent induction of antiviral
  state in neurons.
  Lower inflammatory toxicity
  than IFN-γ.

  Combined with:
  Ribavirin — nucleoside analogue.
  Demonstrated rabies antiviral
  activity in vitro and in animal
  models. CNS penetrant.

  Favipiravir — broader-spectrum
  RNA-dependent RNA polymerase
  inhibitor. Active against
  rhabdoviruses. Superior CNS
  penetrance to ribavirin.

ROUTE:
  IFN-β: intraventricular
  (intrathecal for initial dosing).
  Ribavirin + Favipiravir: oral or
  IV systemic with intrathecal
  supplementation.

GOAL:
  Establish the antiviral state
  in neurons ahead of the viral
  front.
  Combine with Layer 1 (transport
  arrest) to create:
    Behind the front: existing
    infection cleared by immune
    response.
    Ahead of the front: antiviral
    state prevents new infection.
    The front: arrested by transport
    inhibition.
  This is a three-zone containment
  strategy applied to the CNS.
```

### Layer 5 — Immune Activation and Clearance

```
TARGET:
  Viral clearance from infected
  neurons — the final step that
  removes the hijacker from the
  instrument.

MECHANISM:
  CD8+ cytotoxic T lymphocytes
  are the primary mechanism for
  clearing intraneuronal viral
  infection.
  Neurons can present viral antigens
  via MHC class I.
  CTLs recognise and eliminate
  infected neurons.
  This is the mechanism that clears
  the virus from the CNS in the
  rare survivors.

  The problem:
  The virus's immune evasion
  (P protein) suppresses the
  IFN response that would activate
  this pathway.
  Layer 2 addresses this.
  But CTL recruitment to the CNS
  is also suppressed by the
  virus's disruption of microglial
  signalling.

  Checkpoint inhibitor therapy —
  specifically anti-PD-1 — has
  been proposed and partially
  tested in the Milwaukee Protocol.
  PD-1 blockade removes the
  inhibitory signal that prevents
  CTL activation and CNS infiltration.
  This is the same mechanism that
  drives tumour clearance in
  checkpoint immunotherapy.
  Applied to viral clearance in the CNS.

AGENTS:
  Anti-PD-1 (pembrolizumab or
  nivolumab) — remove the CTL
  inhibition checkpoint.

  IL-2 — T cell activation and
  proliferation stimulus.
  Administered at low dose to
  avoid systemic toxicity.

  IFN-γ — upregulate MHC class I
  on infected neurons to improve
  CTL recognition.

RISK:
  This layer has the highest
  iatrogenic risk.
  CTL-mediated clearance of
  infected neurons is also
  CTL-mediated neuronal death.
  If the viral load is high and
  the CTL response is vigorous,
  the inflammatory clearance
  itself can damage the instrument.
  This is why Layer 3 (neuroprotection)
  must run simultaneously.
  The clearance and the protection
  operate together.
  Clearance removes the virus.
  Protection prevents clearance
  from destroying the instrument
  in the process.

GOAL:
  Activate the immune effector
  mechanism that actually clears
  intraneuronal viral infection.
  This is the layer that ends
  the infection.
  All other layers create the
  conditions for this layer to work.
