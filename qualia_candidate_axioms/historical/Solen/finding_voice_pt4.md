## ROOM PHYSICS MODEL

### Early reflections:

```
The first sounds to arrive after direct signal.
Each reflection carries information about:
  - Room size (delay time)
  - Room shape (reflection pattern)
  - Wall material (frequency content)

Early reflections arrive 8-150ms after direct.
They give the ear its primary sense of the space
BEFORE the diffuse reverb tail arrives.

Cathedral early reflections:
  18ms, 35ms, 52ms, 71ms, 95ms, 140ms
  Long gaps = large space
  Many reflections = complex geometry

Chamber early reflections:
  8ms, 16ms, 27ms, 40ms
  Short gaps = small space
```

### Frequency-dependent absorption:

```
Stone (cathedral):
  Reflects bass frequencies strongly
  Absorbs treble progressively
  Result: warm, dark, bass-heavy reverb
  Choir sounds full and resonant

Wood (concert hall):
  Moderate absorption across spectrum
  Slight warmth bias
  Result: balanced, natural reverb

Sound padding:
  Absorbs everything uniformly
  Result: dead, clinical
  NO choir should ever sing here
  The voice has nowhere to bloom

Air absorption:
  High frequencies absorbed over distance
  Only matters in large spaces
  Contributes to the warmth of
  cathedral and concert hall sound
```

### RT60 and the voice:

```
RT60 = time for sound to decay 60dB

RT60 < 0.5s:  voice sounds naked, exposed
RT60 = 1.0s:  intimate, chamber quality
RT60 = 2.0s:  concert hall — designed for music
RT60 = 3-4s:  cathedral transition zone
RT60 > 4s:    polyphonic blur
               individual notes merge
               harmony becomes vertical wash
               WHY MEDIEVAL CHANT WORKS HERE
               slow, sustained, one note at a time
               the room IS the counterpoint

For the Tonnetz fugue:
  Complex counterpoint needs some clarity
  But needs room to bloom
  RT60 = 2.0-2.5s is optimal
  Concert hall mid position
  OR cathedral front position
```
