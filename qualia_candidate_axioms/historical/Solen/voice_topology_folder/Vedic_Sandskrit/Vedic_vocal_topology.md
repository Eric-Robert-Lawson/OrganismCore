## SECTION 2 — THE THREE DERIVATION STREAMS

### Stream A: The Śikṣā Tradition

**What it is:** The Vedāṅga corpus of phonological
treatises specifying the correct production of Vedic
sounds. Multiple independent texts (Pāṇinīya Śikṣā,
Yājñavalkya Śikṣā, Nāradiya Śikṣā, Prātiśākhyas)
transmitted across millennia through oral preservation
within distinct recitation schools (śākhās).

**Production conditions:** Internal to the Vedic
tradition. No access to acoustic measurement technology.
Derived entirely from careful articulatory observation
and transmission under ritual constraint. Multiple
independent school traditions provide cross-validation
within this stream.

**Key structural features:**
- Five canonical articulation places (sthāna):
  kaṇṭha (glottal/velar) → tālu (palatal) →
  mūrdhan (retroflex) → danta (dental) → oṣṭha (labial)
- Articulatory instrument and manner (karaṇa, prayatna)
  as secondary axis
- Breath (prāṇa) as energizing medium — causal chain:
  intention → prāṇa → shaped by articulatory places
- Three pitch accents: udātta (high, stable),
  anudātta (low, stable), svarita (falling trajectory)
- Six phonological dimensions (ṣaḍaṅga): phoneme,
  accent, duration, force, melody, continuity
- Self-calibrating structure: explicit fault taxonomy
  (doṣa) encoding deviations from correct production
- Matrix completeness: exhaustive tiling of the
  phonological space — no gaps by design

**Independence from other streams:** CONFIRMED.
The Śikṣā tradition predates modern acoustic phonetics
by millennia and has no historical contact with
Euler's mathematical work.

**What it is:** The modern scientific account of how

**Independence from other streams:** CONFIRMED.

**Independence from other streams:** CONFIRMED.

with phonological or physiological data.

## SECTION 5 — THE RESIDUAL

These are the genuinely open questions —
what this artifact cannot currently resolve.

**Residual 1: Original absolute pitch values**
The Rigvedic accent system's absolute
frequency values (the actual Hz of udātta
and anudātta in original performance) are
not recoverable from existing sources.
The structural portrait holds for relative
relationships; absolute values remain
in the residual.

**Residual 2: Torus vs. infinite plane topology**
Whether the Vedic tonal space is better
modeled as a toroidal Tonnetz (equal
temperament, closing on itself) or an
infinite-plane Tonnetz (just intonation,
non-repeating) remains undetermined.
The Śikṣā evidence slightly favors
just intonation (the tradition specifies
precise ratios that don't close cleanly
in equal temperament), but this requires
acoustic verification.

**Residual 3: Nasal vowel mapping**
The phonological status of Vedic
nasalized vowels (anusvāra, visarga)
and their position in the Tonnetz
mapping has not yet been analyzed.

**Residual 4: Cross-śākhā structural variation**
Whether the structural portrait holds
uniformly across all living Vedic
recitation traditions (Ṛgveda, Sāmaveda,
Yajurveda, Atharvaveda śākhās) or
whether regional variation reveals
structural sub-portraits has not
been tested.
