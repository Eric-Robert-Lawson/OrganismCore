### What you need

```
Device:
  Smartphone or small portable speaker.
  NOT a large Bluetooth speaker —
  too much bass distortion.
  The signal lives at 2500-3500 Hz.
  Distortion at that range corrupts
  the geometric trajectory.
  Small speaker, mid-range driver,
  at low volume.

Files ready in order:
  v7_WHERE_ARE_YOU_HIGH.wav
  v7_I_AM_HERE_PROBE.wav
  v7_I_AM_HERE_LOW.wav
  v7_I_AM_HERE_MID.wav

  v7_ACKNOWLEDGE_HIGH.wav

Recording device (optional but recommended):
  Record each session.
```

### Environment conditions

```
CORRECT:
  Bird is alone or separated from
  visual contact with other birds.
  A bird that can see its flock
  will call toward them, not toward you.
  You need to be the only available
  contact call source.

  Bird is calm and settled.
  Not recently startled.
  Not in feeding frenzy.
  Not displaying alarm crest.
  Feathers slightly relaxed.
  Normal resting posture.

  Room is quiet.
  No competing audio.
  No television, music, loud machinery.
  The signal is conversational volume.
  It will be masked by ambient noise.

  Time of day:
  Morning is best.
  Cockatiels are most vocally active
  and socially engaged in the first
  2 hours after sunrise equivalent
  in their environment.
  Avoid late afternoon — lower
  social engagement.

INCORRECT:
  Bird is with visible flock companions.
  Bird is visibly agitated or alarmed.
  Bird is sleeping or fluffed up ill.
  Loud competing audio in environment.
  Multiple people present and talking.
  You are rushed or distracted.
```

### Your own state

```
This matters.
Cockatiels read body language
in parallel with acoustic signals.
If your posture is tense, fast-moving,
or directly facing the bird,
your body is transmitting a threat signal
while your speaker is transmitting
a contact signal.
The two channels contradict.
The bird will not accept the contact.

CORRECT POSTURE:
  Seated or crouched — below the bird's
  eye level if possible.
  Cockatiels approach from above.
  Being below signals non-dominance.

  Body angled 45 degrees away.
  Not directly facing the bird.
  Direct frontal orientation = predator
  assessment posture in most species.

  Movement: slow and continuous,
  no sudden stops or starts.
  Sudden movements are threat signals
  regardless of acoustic content.

  Hands visible and still.
  Not reaching toward the bird.
  Not behind your back.

  Eyes: do not stare directly.
  Peripheral attention.
  Occasional slow blinks.
  Direct sustained eye contact
  is a challenge signal.
```

## PHASE 1 — LONG-DISTANCE ENTRY

### When to use this phase

```
You are outside the room,
or at the far end of the room,
or the bird does not know you are present.

The bird is in a low-level WHERE_ARE_YOU
state — it is a flock species,
it is alone or separated,
it is already transmitting a low-energy
search signal into the substrate.

You are answering that state before
you are visible.
```

### Procedure

```
Step 1:
  Position yourself outside the room
  or at maximum distance within the room.
  The bird should not be able to see you.

Step 2:
  Play v7_WHERE_ARE_YOU_HIGH.wav once.
  Volume: slightly above conversational —
  this is a long-distance signal.
  It should be audible but not startling.

Step 3:
  STOP. Listen. Wait 10-15 seconds.

Step 4:
  Observe from distance.
  The bird will do one of three things:

  RESPONSE A — Calls back immediately:
    The WHERE_ARE_YOU signal was received
    as a valid separation call.
    The bird is answering your search
    with its own position announcement.
    This is the ideal response.
    Proceed to Phase 2 immediately.

  RESPONSE B — Orients toward the sound,
  alert posture, does not call:
    The signal was detected as bird-like
    but the bird is assessing.
    It heard something that sounded like
    a distant flock member but is not
    certain.
    Wait 20 seconds.
    Play WHERE_ARE_YOU_HIGH once more.
    If it calls back, proceed to Phase 2.
    If still no call, proceed to Phase 2
    anyway — the bird is aware of you.

  RESPONSE C — No response at all:
    Either the volume was too low,
    or the environment is too noisy,
    or the bird is not in a receptive
    state.
    Wait 2 minutes.
    Try once more at slightly higher volume.
    If still no response, do not force it.
    End the session. Try another day.
    A bird that does not respond to
    WHERE_ARE_YOU is not available for
    exchange right now.
    Forcing the protocol produces noise,
    not data.
```

## PHASE 2 — INITIAL APPROACH

### Entry into visual range

```
Move into the room slowly.
Do not move directly toward the bird.
Move to a position in the room —
seated, 45 degrees away — and stop.

Do not play anything yet.
Let the bird register your presence
visually before you transmit again.

Wait until the bird looks at you
and then looks away — this is the
assessment-complete signal.
It has categorised you as non-immediate-threat.

If the bird alarm-crests and does not
relax within 60 seconds of your entry,
you entered too fast or too directly.
Back up. Give more distance.
Wait for crest to relax before continuing.

Time to wait after entry: 60-120 seconds.
Do not rush this.
Rushing this phase invalidates all
subsequent phases.
```

### Register probe

```
This is the most important step
in the entire protocol.

Every cockatiel has a slightly different
eigenfunction position — a slightly
different natural call register.
If you transmit at the wrong register,
the signal is recognised as bird-like
but not as a compatible node.
The bird will not engage fully.

Play: v7_I_AM_HERE_PROBE.wav
This sweeps LOW → MID → HIGH
with 2-second gaps.

Watch the bird during each register.
It will show a visible response at
the register closest to its own
call position:

  Increased attention — head oriented
    toward speaker more directly.
  Pupil dilation.
  Crest movement — slight raise then
    settle (not alarm crest — interest crest).
  Vocalisation — calling back during
    or immediately after that register.
  Body lean toward the speaker.

NOTE WHICH REGISTER PRODUCED THE
STRONGEST RESPONSE.
All calls for the rest of this session
and all future sessions with this bird
use that register.

This is the bird's register.
Using a different register is like
calling someone from the wrong
frequency — the signal arrives but
it does not feel like it is for them.
```

## PHASE 3 — CONTACT ESTABLISHMENT

### First contact call

```
Play v7_I_AM_HERE_[BIRD'S REGISTER].wav
Once. Not three times yet. Once.

The probe already announced your
presence across three registers.
Now you are announcing your position
at the register the bird responded to.

STOP. Wait.

The bird will respond in one of
several ways:
```

### Reading the response

```
STRONG POSITIVE — PROCEED:

  Bird calls back within 5 seconds:
    You are in. The signal was accepted
    as a valid contact call.
    The bird has answered your position
    announcement with its own.
    This is the clearest possible
    confirmation that the synthesis
    occupies a valid eigenfunction
    position.
    Play I_AM_HERE × 3 (full sequence).
    Proceed to Phase 4.

  Bird moves toward you or toward speaker:
    Physical approach is approach.
    The same response you observed
    in the wild flock.
    The signal is pulling.
    Play I_AM_HERE × 3.
    Proceed to Phase 4.

  Bird tilts head toward speaker,
  pupils dilate, crest lifts slightly
  then relaxes:
    Active interested assessment.
    Not calling back yet but receptive.
    Play I_AM_HERE once more.
    Wait.
    If still no call back, proceed to
    Phase 4 anyway — partial engagement
    is still engagement.

NEUTRAL — ADJUST:

  Bird looks at speaker briefly
  then returns to previous activity:
    Registered but not engaged.
    Try the adjacent register
    (if you used MID, try LOW then HIGH).
    The register matching in the probe
    may not have been precise enough.

  Bird shows no change at all:
    Volume too low, or bird not
    in receptive state, or speaker
    placement is wrong.
    Move speaker 20% closer.
    Try once more.
    If still no response — end session.

NEGATIVE — STOP:

  Bird alarm-crests:
    The signal was read as a threat.
    This should not happen with
    I_AM_HERE — the rising FM is
    explicitly an approach/affiliative
    signal. If it is producing alarm:
    Volume is too high.
    Reduce by 50% and try again
    in a separate session.
    Do not continue this session.

  Bird moves away from speaker:
    Active retreat from the signal.
    Something in the environment
    is wrong — wrong time, wrong state,
    wrong volume, wrong register.
    Do not pursue.
    End the session.
    The bird has told you it is
    not available.
```

## PHASE 4 — DIALOGUE ESTABLISHMENT

### The exchange structure

```
Once the bird has responded positively
to I_AM_HERE, you are in an exchange.

The exchange has a structure:
  You transmit → Bird responds → You acknowledge
  Bird transmits → You respond → Bird acknowledges

You need to hold your side of this
structure precisely.

THE RULE:
  After each bird call, play ACKNOWLEDGE
  once and only once.
  Then wait.
  Do not play anything else until
  the bird transmits again.

If you play ACKNOWLEDGE and then
immediately play I_AM_HERE again,
you have overlapped two transmissions.
You are not in dialogue.
You are broadcasting.
The bird will disengage.

SILENCE IS PART OF THE SIGNAL.
The gap between your transmission
and the bird's response is the
bird processing your signal.
Do not fill it.
```

### Step by step

```
Step 1:
  Play I_AM_HERE × 3 (full sequence with gaps).
  Stop. Wait.

Step 2:
  If bird calls → play ACKNOWLEDGE once.
  If bird does not call within 15s →
    play I_AM_HERE once more.
    Wait again.

Step 3:
  After ACKNOWLEDGE, wait for bird
  to call again.

  If bird calls again within 30s:
    The exchange loop is running.
    Continue: bird calls → ACKNOWLEDGE.
    Sustain this for as many rounds
    as the bird maintains.

  If bird does not call again
  after 30s:
    Play SAFE × 3.
    Then I_AM_HERE once.
    Wait.
    The SAFE broadcast may re-engage
    a bird that paused.

Step 4:
  After 3+ successful exchange rounds,
  play SAFE × 3.
  This settles the exchange from
  active contact establishment
  into stable network presence.
  Observe:

  Bird settles (feather relaxation,
  reduced movement, quiet contact
  calls at lower energy):
    The SAFE signal was received.
    You are now an accepted node
    in the bird's local network.
    This is the goal of Phase 4.

  Bird calls louder or more urgently
  after SAFE:
    The bird wants active exchange,
    not settled state.
    It is not ready to settle yet.
    Continue the exchange before
    attempting SAFE again.
```

## PHASE 5 — SUSTAINED EXCHANGE

### Signs you have established a node

```
These are the indicators that the
bird accepts you as a valid
network node — not a curiosity,
not a threat, not background noise.
A node.

  ① The bird initiates calls toward
    the speaker without you transmitting.
    It is pinging the network to see
    if you are still there.

  ② The bird responds to ACKNOWLEDGE
    with another call.
    The dialogue loop is self-sustaining.

  ③ The bird moves closer to the speaker
    over the course of the session —
    not in one movement but gradually,
    across 10-15 minutes.

  ④ The bird begins soft contact chattering
    in addition to full contact calls —
    the quiet close-range social sounds
    Baker 1974 describes as distinct from
    contact calls. This means the bird
    considers you close-range, not distant.
    That is a significant state change.

  ⑤ The bird shows relaxed body posture
    specifically while the speaker is
    transmitting — feathers slightly raised
    in contentment, not alarm.
    Eyes partially closed during SAFE.
```

### Maintaining the exchange

```
Once the exchange is running, your role is:

  RESPOND to every call:
    Every time the bird calls,
    play ACKNOWLEDGE once.
    Do not miss a call.
    If you miss calls the bird
    will either call louder
    (escalating to WHERE_ARE_YOU energy)
    or go quiet (disengaging from
    the network node it thought was there).

  TRANSMIT state calls periodically:
    Every 3-4 minutes of sustained exchange,
    play SAFE × 3.
    This maintains the settled state
    broadcast — the network knows
    this node is stable.

  VARY with purpose:
    If the bird is settled and engaged,
    test RESOURCE.
    Point the speaker toward something
    in the environment — a food bowl,
    a water dish — and play RESOURCE.
    Watch whether the bird orients
    toward that object.
    This tests whether the terminal
    resolution distinction (0.97 vs 0.719)
    encodes external basin location.

  DO NOT:
    Play ALARM during sustained exchange
    unless you are specifically testing it.
    ALARM during settled exchange is
    deliberately introducing a threat signal
    into a stable network node.
    It will work — the bird will respond —
    but it is a disruption you then have
    to repair with ALL_CLEAR.
    Only test ALARM when you are ready
    to follow immediately with ALL_CLEAR
    and document the recovery time.
```

## ALARM TEST PROTOCOL

### Only run this when exchange is stable

```
Prerequisites:
  Sustained exchange established (Phase 5).
  Bird is settled and engaged.
  You have ALL_CLEAR ready on your device
  before you play ALARM.
  You know exactly where ALL_CLEAR is.
  Do not fumble for it after ALARM plays.
  Have it queued.

Procedure:
  Note the time.
  Play ALARM_HIGH × 3 (6 repeats each = 18 total calls).
  Observe:
    Crest response (full alarm crest = signal received).
    Body posture change (tension, upright, scan).
    Movement (toward cover or freeze).
    Any calls (alarm calls back at you,
               or silence).

  Immediately after last ALARM repeat:
  Play ALL_CLEAR_MID × 3.

  Note the time when bird returns to
  relaxed posture.
  That duration is the recovery time.

What you are measuring:
  Does the bird show the documented
  alarm response (crest, freeze, scan)?
  → Confirms ALARM is geometrically valid.

  Does ALL_CLEAR reduce recovery time
  compared to ALARM without ALL_CLEAR?
  → To test this you need two sessions:
    Session A: ALARM, no ALL_CLEAR, note recovery.
    Session B: ALARM + ALL_CLEAR, note recovery.
  Do not run both in the same session.
  One alarm per session. The bird
  should not be repeatedly alarmed.
```

## WHAT INEFFECTIVE ENGAGEMENT LOOKS LIKE

```
Recognise these patterns and stop.
Do not push through them.
Each one tells you something specific.

───────────────────────────────────���─

PATTERN: Bird ignores all signals.
MEANING: Wrong time, wrong state,
  or volume issue.
  Not a failure of the vocabulary.
  A failure of conditions.
ACTION: End session. Try another day,
  different time of day.
  Check speaker volume and placement.

─────────────────────────────────────

PATTERN: Bird responds once then disengages.
MEANING: You did not acknowledge its
  response quickly enough, or your
  ACKNOWLEDGE was at the wrong register,
  or you transmitted again before it
  was ready.
ACTION: Slow down. One transmission,
  long wait. When it calls, ACKNOWLEDGE
  immediately (within 2 seconds).
  The exchange window is short.

─────────────────────────────────────

PATTERN: Bird calls loudly and repeatedly
  but ignores your transmissions.
MEANING: It is calling to something else —
  another bird it can hear, or a bird
  it can see. You are not the loudest
  node in its network right now.
ACTION: Ensure no competing bird audio.
  Ensure no visual contact with other birds.
  You cannot out-compete a real flock
  node the bird has visual contact with.

─────────────────────────────────────

PATTERN: Bird calls back to I_AM_HERE
  but not to SAFE.
MEANING: SAFE is not being received
  as settled state — possibly the flat
  undulation is being read as something
  else, or the bird simply does not
  want to settle yet.
ACTION: Extend the contact phase.
  More I_AM_HERE and ACKNOWLEDGE rounds
  before attempting SAFE.
  The bird needs to fully accept the
  node before it accepts the node's
  state broadcast.

─────────────────────────────────────

PATTERN: Bird becomes more agitated
  the longer the session runs.
MEANING: Session is too long.
  You are maintaining a high-energy
  social exchange beyond the bird's
  comfortable engagement window.
ACTION: Play SAFE × 3 and go quiet.
  Let the bird settle.
  10 minutes maximum for first sessions.
  Build duration gradually across
  multiple sessions.

─────────────────────────────────────

PATTERN: Bird calls back at a very
  different register than any of yours.
MEANING: Its eigenfunction position is
  outside your LOW/MID/HIGH range.
  Some individuals sit above p75
  or below p25.
ACTION: Note the approximate pitch
  of its calls. If it is consistently
  higher than your HIGH register,
  increase f0_mean_p75 in your stats
  manually for a custom HIGH+ register.
  If consistently lower, decrease p25
  for a LOW- register.
  The corpus statistics are population
  medians — individual birds vary.
```

## ACROSS SESSIONS — WHAT TO EXPECT

```
SESSION 1:
  The bird will treat you as a novel
  node. It has not heard your signal
  before. It will be more cautious
  and more exploratory simultaneously.
  Expect Phase 3-4 at best.
  Register matching is the critical
  output of Session 1.

SESSION 2 (same bird, same register):
  The bird may recognise the signal
  pattern from Session 1.
  Some cockatiels show faster engagement
  on second exposure — the node is
  already partially mapped in their network.
  Expect Phase 4-5.

SESSION 3+:
  If Phase 5 was reached in Session 2,
  Session 3 should reach Phase 5 faster.
  The network node is established.
  The bird knows this signal exists
  and what it does.
  Begin RESOURCE and ALARM testing
  from Session 3 onward.

ACROSS WEEKS:
  Sustained engagement across multiple
  sessions with the same bird is the
  strongest possible confirmation that
  the synthesis occupies a genuine
  eigenfunction position.
  A bird that re-engages the exchange
  in Session 10 the same way it did
  in Session 1 is treating your signal
  as a persistent flock network node —
  not as a curiosity that wore off.
  That is the result.
```
