## CRITICAL SUBJECT CONTEXT

### Species profile — red-winged blackbird,
### April, northern Illinois

Red-winged blackbirds (*Agelaius phoeniceus*)
in northern Illinois in April are at peak
spring territorial behaviour. Males have
returned from winter grounds and are actively
establishing or defending territories,
competing for females, and patrolling
foraging circuits.

Key substrate-relevant characteristics:

- **Contact call frequency range:**
  1,500–5,000 Hz — direct overlap with
  observer's LOW and MID register V7
  synthesis (2,514–2,911 Hz centre)
- **Territorial behaviour:** High. Males
  will investigate, challenge, or flee
  from signals that read as territorial
  intrusion from competing nodes
- **Flock behaviour:** Loose mixed flocks
  with grackles, cowbirds, starlings during
  foraging outside breeding territory.
  Individual males maintain foraging circuits
  within and adjacent to defended territory
- **Site fidelity:** Strong. A male that
  identifies a reliable food source will
  return to it on a consistent daily circuit
- **Individual recognition capacity:**
  Documented in literature. Red-winged
  blackbirds recognise individual conspecifics
  by call. Cross-species individual node
  recognition is the working hypothesis
  for this subject's behaviour

### Why this subject is significant

This is the first wild, untamed, free-ranging
subject in this project. All prior subjects —
Juicy, Sora, Melon — were captive birds in
indoor environments. This subject:

- Has no prior relationship with observer
  beyond one day of feeder visitation
- Is under active spring territorial pressure
- Is in its natural environment with full
  freedom of movement
- Responded to observer's signals within
  a single session
- Showed differential register response
  within the same session

The outdoor field result with a wild
territorial male is categorically different
in evidential weight from captive bird results.
It is the framework's first test against
a subject with no domestication gradient
accommodation and no environmental
constraint keeping it in the space.

### Event 1 — Food placed, observer exits,
### calls delivered, subject arrives

**Configuration:** Food already at feeder
before observer exited. Observer opened door,
placed additional food, delivered signal
sequence. Subject arrived immediately
following door opening and call delivery.

**Observations:**
- Food was present at feeder before
  observer appeared
- Observer opened door and placed
  additional food
- Observer delivered calls — specific
  sequence to be confirmed but consistent
  with established feeder protocol
- Subject arrived immediately following
  door opening and signal delivery
- Arrival was immediate — not gradual
  approach over several minutes

**Assessment:**
The food was already present before the
observer appeared. The subject did not
arrive when the food appeared. The subject
arrived when the observer appeared and
signalled.

This is a node-recognition response,
not a food-location response. The food
location was established on Day 1. If
food alone were the trigger, the bird
would have been present before the
observer emerged. The correlation of
arrival with observer presence and
signal delivery is the finding.

Three possible explanations in order
of parsimony:

1. **Door sound conditioning** — subject
   learned on Day 1 that door sound
   correlates with observer presence
   which correlates with food availability.
   Classical conditioning to environmental
   cue. Real and significant but not
   substrate communication.

2. **Visual node recognition** — subject
   saw observer emerge and associated
   observer's visual presence with the
   resource location from Day 1.
   Real and significant.

3. **Acoustic node recognition** — subject
   was in the area, heard observer's calls,
   recognised them as signals from the
   node associated with the Day 1 resource,
   arrived in response to acoustic signal
   specifically.

All three may be operating simultaneously.
The test to distinguish them is documented
in Next Steps. For now all three are
live hypotheses.

**What is confirmed:**
Immediate arrival correlated with observer
presence and signal delivery on Day 2.
Subject was not present before observer
emerged. Subject arrived after observer
emerged and signalled.

### Event 3 — LOW I AM HERE delivered,
### subject departs immediately

**Configuration:** Photos complete.
Observer switched from MID to LOW register.
Delivered I AM HERE LOW.

**Observations:**
- Subject departed immediately following
  I AM HERE LOW delivery
- Departure was immediate — not gradual
  drift away but active flight departure
- Direction of departure not recorded
  — to be noted in future sessions

**Assessment:**
This is the most important single event
of the session.

The subject was stable throughout the
MID register sequence. One variable changed —
register dropped from MID to LOW on
I AM HERE. Subject departed immediately.

This is a within-session register
differential response test. Unplanned
but clean. One variable changed.
One behaviour changed. The correlation
is unambiguous.

**Why LOW I AM HERE produced departure:**

LOW register in the flock eigenfunction
substrate encodes a node at the floor
of the space. For a territorial male
red-winged blackbird in April, a LOW
register I AM HERE does not read as a
gentle or subordinate greeting.

I AM HERE is a position announcement —
it announces that a node is present and
wants to be integrated into the network.
The subject had already established
tolerance for the observer as a MID
register node — a peer-level presence.

When LOW I AM HERE was delivered from
the same location, the substrate received
a signal that reads as: a second node,
at floor-level position, announcing
presence on this territory.

For a territorial male, an unclassified
node announcing position from a
subordinate or concealed register on
his foraging territory is a territorial
intrusion signal. The response —
immediate departure — is consistent
with either:

a) Territorial displacement response:
   the male left rather than challenged,
   consistent with the intrusion being
   perceived as ambiguous rather than
   direct challenge

b) Signal parsing failure: LOW register
   is below the subject's primary social
   processing threshold, and an
   unclassifiable signal from a
   previously-trusted node produced
   departure as a precautionary response

Either interpretation produces the
same operational conclusion:

**LOW register I AM HERE is a departure
trigger for this individual.**

**MID register is the only confirmed
stable register for this subject.**

## FREQUENCY ALIGNMENT ANALYSIS

### V7 synthesis vs red-winged blackbird

| V7 Register | Centre Hz | RWBB Range | Overlap |
|---|---|---|---|
| LOW | ~2,514 Hz | 1,500–5,000 Hz | Yes — inside range |
| MID | ~2,911 Hz | 1,500–5,000 Hz | Yes — inside range |
| HIGH | ~3,386 Hz | 1,500–5,000 Hz | Yes — inside range |

All three V7 registers fall inside the
red-winged blackbird's contact call
frequency range. This is better alignment
than with chickadee (4,000–8,000 Hz
primary range) or goldfinch (3,000–8,000 Hz).

The departure response to LOW I AM HERE
was therefore not a frequency issue.
LOW register at 2,514 Hz is within the
bird's hearing and processing range.
The departure was a geometric and
positional response — the message
the register carried, not the
frequency it was delivered at.

This is an important distinction.
The bird heard the LOW register call
clearly. It chose to leave because of
what the signal meant, not because
it could not detect it.

**The substrate is processing geometry,
not just frequency. Confirmed.**

## METHODOLOGICAL NOTES

### The accidental register test

The most valuable experimental result
of this session was not planned. Observer
switched to LOW I AM HERE after photos
were complete — the session's primary
goal was already achieved. The switch
was exploratory, not designed.

The result was the cleanest register
differential test the project has
produced: one variable changed, one
behaviour changed, in real time, with
a wild subject, on film — partially.
Photos document the stable MID state.
The LOW departure is observer-reported.

This is the ignorance superpower
operating in the outdoor field for
the first time. The unplanned test
produced cleaner data than a designed
test would have, because a designed
test would have required the bird
to be naive to the experimental
manipulation. The organic session
flow produced the manipulation
naturally.

### The instinct confirmation

Observer ran the correct four-call
sequence on instinct. This is a
methodological finding about observer
training. The geometric grammar of
the substrate is beginning to operate
intuitively in the observer after
four sessions. The observer did not
need to consult the protocol. The
protocol had been internalised
sufficiently to operate in real time
with a wild subject.

This is relevant to the project's
long-term scalability. If the protocol
can be internalised to instinct level,
it does not require a field guide in
the moment. It operates as natural
behaviour — which is exactly the
correct mode for substrate communication.
Communication from a node that is
consulting a manual reads differently
in the substrate than communication
from a node that is simply present.

### The door sound confound

The door sound as a conditioning cue
is a genuine alternative explanation
for the immediate arrival result and
must be controlled for. It does not
invalidate the finding. It is the
next experimental question. See
Next Steps.

Day A: Open door, place food, deliver
no calls. Record time to subject arrival.

When subject is settled and feeding
at MID register — deliver one
I AM HERE HIGH, single delivery.

has returned and been stable at MID
