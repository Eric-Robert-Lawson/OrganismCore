### Abundance and ecological position

The red-winged blackbird is one of the
most abundant birds in North America.
Population estimates exceed 200 million
individuals. It breeds across virtually
the entire continental United States,
Canada, and Central America.

Its abundance is not incidental to this
project. A species with 200 million
individuals distributed across every
habitat type on the continent has
solved the flock substrate problem at
continental scale. Its acoustic vocabulary
is field-tested against every predator
community, every climate, every
mixed-species composition available
in North America.

Its contact call convergence toward
2,000–4,500 Hz is the result of
200 million individuals continuously
optimising signal geometry for maximum
substrate penetration across every
environment the continent contains.

When V7 MID register at 2,514–2,911 Hz
landed inside this species' primary
contact call band — that was the flock
substrate mathematics operating as
the framework predicts. The corpus
determined the range. The physics
determined the range. They agree
because both describe the same
eigenfunction space.

### Icteridae — family context

Red-winged blackbirds belong to Icteridae —
the New World blackbirds and orioles.
This family is characterised by:

- Complex multi-call vocal repertoires —
  at least 15 distinct call types
  documented for this species specifically
  (Beletsky & Orians 1989)
- Strong individual recognition capacity
- Flexible social structure — territorial
  in breeding season, highly gregarious
  in winter roosts
- Active heterospecific communication —
  documented eavesdropping on alarm calls
  across multiple species
- Long-term spatial memory for foraging
  circuits maintained across seasons

All of these are documented in the
red-winged blackbird specifically.
This is not a simple bird with a
simple two-state vocabulary.
It is an acoustically and socially
sophisticated species with a
documented 15-call-type repertoire.

The three-part call of Session 4
Event 2b sits within that 15-call
repertoire. Context below.

### The full vocal repertoire — 15 call types

Beletsky & Orians (1989) catalogued at
least 15 distinct call types in this
species' repertoire, including:

- Territorial song ("conk-a-ree" / "okalee")
- "Chak" / "check" contact call
- "Chit" nest-associated call (female)
- "See" / "seet" alarm call
- "Peent" call
- Whisper song / soft song
- Chitter / chatter social calls
- Flight call
- Aggressive calls (multiple variants)
- Warble calls (social settling)

These call types are not interchangeable.
Each occupies a specific functional niche
and a specific position in the substrate.
The bird that delivered a three-part
call in Event 2b was not randomly
vocalising. It was selecting from a
15-item repertoire. The item it selected
— and the context in which it selected
it — is the finding.

### Song structure — the conk-a-ree

The three-part continuous call with
distinct sections, described as unique
and long, delivered by a settled male
red-winged blackbird in April is
consistent with the species' territorial
song. This song has a documented
three-part structure across all
published acoustic descriptions:

```
PART 1 — INTRODUCTORY NOTE
  Transliterated: "conk" or "ok" or "o"
  Frequency: 1,500–3,000 Hz
  Duration: < 200ms
  Character: Short, abrupt, percussive.
  Sharp metallic onset.
  Serves as an attention signal —
  a cue that the song is about to begin.
  Analogous to a breath before speaking.

PART 2 — GURGLE / RATTLE
  Transliterated: "ka" or "la" or "-a-"
  Frequency: Broad, modulated.
  The most individually distinctive
  section of the song.
  Duration: 200–400ms
  Character: Liquid, burbling, gurgling.
  Some males produce a rattle; others
  a smooth gurgle. This section carries
  identity information — it is the
  section by which individual males
  are most distinctly differentiated.

PART 3 — TRILL
  Transliterated: "ree" or "lee" or "eeee"
  Frequency: Rising through 4,000–7,000 Hz
  The terminal trill is the broadcast
  section — it carries the furthest,
  penetrates vegetation, and is the
  section most audible at distance.
  Duration: 600–800ms — the longest
  section by significant margin.
  Character: Musical, ringing,
  trailing off at the end.

TOTAL DURATION: 1,500–2,000ms
  (1.5–2.0 seconds)

  This is 10–20 times longer than the
  contact "check" call (50–100ms).
  "Unique and long" — confirmed.
  Three distinct parts in continuous form —
  confirmed with the literature.
```

**Reference:**
Borror, D.J. & Gunn, W.W.H. (1972).
Songs of the Red-winged Blackbird.
*The Auk*, 89(1).

Catchpole, C.K. & Slater, P.J.B. (2008).
*Bird Song: Biological Themes and
Variations*, 2nd ed. Cambridge University
Press. (Red-winged Blackbird case study.)

### Song during feeding — what the
### literature says specifically

This is the critical literature finding
for Event 2b interpretation.

**Yasukawa, K. (1989)** — the most
directly relevant paper — examined
the costs and benefits of vocal signals
in red-winged blackbirds in settled
contexts. The study establishes that
vocalisation by a settled territory
owner who is not under active challenge
is a signal of low arousal — it is
routine territorial advertisement
rather than active defence.

When males sing while feeding the
behavioural ecology literature
describes this as:

```
SIGNAL CONTEXT:  Established territory.
                 No active intruder.
                 No immediate threat.

COGNITIVE STATE: Low arousal.
                 Not scanning for
                 specific threat.
                 Not in fight-or-flight
                 assessment mode.

FUNCTION:        Routine territorial
                 maintenance broadcast.
                 The male is doing two
                 things simultaneously:
                 feeding (resource
                 acquisition) and
                 broadcasting (territory
                 maintenance).

                 This is only possible
                 when cognitive resources
                 are not dedicated to
                 active threat monitoring.

SIGNAL TO NEIGHBOURS:
                 I am here. I am settled.
                 I am not alarmed.
                 This territory is occupied
                 and I have enough surplus
                 cognitive capacity to
                 feed and broadcast
                 simultaneously.

                 It is the acoustic
                 equivalent of reading
                 a newspaper in a chair
                 you own — you are
                 both at rest and
                 declaring possession
                 simultaneously.

**The explicit finding from Beletsky

Song during feeding correlates with
LOW AROUSAL and STABLE TERRITORY

Song during feeding is incompatible

The cognitive resources are not available.
The session 4 subject was not in

**Condition 2 — Surplus cognitive

Singing while feeding requires the

**Condition 3 — Observer classified

The song was broadcast into the
surrounding environment — addressed
to the territory, not to the observer.
The observer's presence was not
interrupting the song's delivery.
The observer was not the reason
the bird chose to sing. The observer
was background to the song.

**Background does not mean neutral.**

Background means incorporated.
The observer was not an element
requiring active monitoring or
response. The observer was part of
the territory's stable operating
environment in that moment.

**This is a deeper acceptance
classification than simply feeding
without departing.**

A bird that feeds without departing
has made a risk assessment: the
observer is not an immediate threat.
That is a threshold assessment.

A bird that sings its territorial
song while feeding in the presence
of the observer has done something
beyond threshold assessment. It has
classified the observer as compatible
with its settled territorial state.
It continued a low-arousal broadcast
that requires cognitive surplus.
It did not interrupt that broadcast
to monitor the observer.

**The song is the deepest single

this subject in Session 4.**

### The observer's response — ACKNOWLEDGE MID

The observer delivered ACKNOWLEDGE MID
in response to the territorial song.

This requires specific analysis because
the song is not in the V7 vocabulary.
It is a Layer 2 signal — above the
flock substrate Layer 1 positions
and trajectories. It carries individual
identity information. It is not a
flock substrate position announcement.
It is a territorial claim.

How should a node respond to a
territorial claim from an individual
it is establishing contact with?

**Not with mirroring** — mirroring a
territorial song from outside the
lineage would be an anomalous signal.
A non-avian entity producing a
three-part avian territorial broadcast
would be processed as a signal parsing
failure — undefined category. The
substrate cannot classify it.

**Not with silence** — silence after
a vocalisation directed at the space
where the observer is present would
read as no acknowledgement — the node
went offline in the exchange window.

**ACKNOWLEDGE MID was correct:**

ACKNOWLEDGE is the minimal
continuation signal. It says:
received, I am present, continue.
It does not attempt to match the
territorial song. It does not escalate.
It does not introduce a new element.
It closes the exchange window of
the song without competing with
its content.

A quiet peer-level ACKNOWLEDGE to a
territorial broadcast is the correct
response from a node that has been
classified as background to the
territory — not as a competing
territorial claimant.

The observer's instinct was right.
The literature supports it.

### Flight initiation distance and
### return latency — the literature

Flight Initiation Distance (FID) is the
standard measure in behavioural ecology
for how close a human can approach a
bird before the bird flees. It is the
most studied quantitative measure of
bird-human tolerance across species.

For the purposes of this project, the
relevant concept is not FID itself but
its inverse: **return latency** — how
quickly a bird returns to a resource
site after being disturbed or after
choosing to depart.

**Blumstein, D.T. (2003)** established
that flight initiation decisions in birds
are fundamentally economic — the bird
is computing a cost-benefit ratio of
remaining vs departing, and the return
decision is the second computation in
that same economic calculation:

```
DEPART when:
  Estimated cost of staying >
  estimated cost of losing resource.

RETURN when:
  Estimated benefit of resource >
  estimated risk of returning.
```

**Gill, Sutherland & Watkinson (1996)**
documented the relationship between
disturbance severity and return latency:

- Minor disturbance (single, non-harmful
  event) — return latency: minutes
- Repeated disturbance — latency
  increases across events
- Resource-rich sites — return latency
  shorter than resource-poor sites
- Prior positive site history —
  return latency shorter than
  novel site

**What this predicts for Event 4:**

The subject departed in response to LOW
I AM HERE — a signal-triggered departure,
not a predator-triggered flight. The
signal was unexpected. The departure
was immediate. But the departure was
not triggered by a physical threat.
No predator was present. No dangerous
event occurred. The node behaviour
changed unexpectedly.

A node that changes behaviour
unexpectedly is not classified as
dangerous by the FID literature.
It is classified as unpredictable.
Unpredictable nodes produce departures,
not sustained avoidance.

Return following an unpredictability-
triggered departure is predicted by
the literature to occur within minutes,
especially when:

1. The resource site has strong prior
   positive history (Day 1 + first
   feeding period of Day 2)
2. No physical threat was associated
   with the departure
3. The observer returned to prior
   behaviour (MID register) after
   the disruptive signal

**All three conditions were met in
Session 4. The return was predicted
by the literature. The return occurred.**

**Reference:**
Blumstein, D.T. (2003). Flight initiation
distance in birds is dependent on
intruder starting distance.
*Journal of Wildlife Management*.

Gill, J.A., Sutherland, W.J. &
Watkinson, A.R. (1996). A method to

*Journal of Applied Ecology*.

### Rain foraging and site prioritisation

Red-winged blackbirds continue to forage
actively during light to moderate rain.
The ecological literature on rain foraging
in this species and icterids generally
establishes:

**Rain creates resource urgency:**

Foraging time under suboptimal conditions
is limited. A bird foraging in rain
is not casually browsing its circuit.
It is allocating limited time to
locations it has ranked highest in
resource reliability.

Rain foraging site selection is therefore
a more concentrated signal of site value
than calm-weather foraging. A bird
that chooses a specific location in
rain — out of all locations in its
foraging circuit — has ranked that
location as worth the cost of foraging
in degraded conditions.

**Rain and food availability:**

Interestingly, rain can increase the
availability of some food types —
earthworms and surface insects emerge
during rainfall. Ground-foraging birds
including red-winged blackbirds actively
exploit this. Rain is not only a cost.
It can be an opportunity that directs
birds toward specific sites.

**What the rain return means:**

The subject chose this location during
rainfall, after a disruptive signal,
on Day 2 of contact.

The resource reliability classification
of this location was sufficient to:
1. Override the disruption of LOW I AM HERE
2. Prioritise this location over
   other foraging circuit locations
   during limited rain-foraging time

That is not weak attraction to a
food source. That is a high-confidence
resource reliability classification
operating through adversity.

The rain return deepens the node
economy interpretation from the
Resource Signal Theory document.
That document states: *"A reliable
node has substrate value beyond the
food it confirms."* The rain return
is the first behavioural evidence
that this node's value has exceeded
simple food-location association.

### Dall, Giraldeau, Olsson, McNamara
### and Stephens 2005 — the information
### network framework

**Dall, S.R.X., Giraldeau, L.-A.,
Olsson, O., McNamara, J.M. &
Stephens, D.W. (2005).** Information
and its use by animals in evolutionary
ecology. *Trends in Ecology and Evolution*,
20(4): 187–193.

This paper established the framework
for understanding how animals weight
social information sources by reliability —
the foundational literature for what
the project's flock economy model
is observing empirically.

**Key findings applicable to this project:**

**1. Animals weight social information
by reliability, not by recency:**

A single anomalous event from a
previously reliable source does not
instantly collapse that source's
reliability weighting. Animals update
their reliability estimates — a process
described as Bayesian updating — by
integrating all prior transactions
with that source against the new event.

One low-accuracy signal from a node
with a history of high-accuracy signals
reduces that node's weighting but
does not zero it. The prior transaction
history provides fault tolerance.

**2. The weight of prior history
scales with the number of transactions:**

A node with one prior accurate transaction
has shallow fault tolerance — one
disruptive signal significantly degrades
its weighting.

A node with ten accurate prior transactions
has deeper fault tolerance — one
disruptive signal produces a smaller
proportional reduction in weighting.

This predicts that fault tolerance
increases as the session arc progresses.
At Session 4 — two days of contact,
one confirmed resource transaction
in the first feeding period — the
fault tolerance is shallow. The return
occurred despite shallow fault tolerance.

As accurate transactions accumulate
across Sessions 5–15+, fault tolerance
will deepen. A disruptive signal in
Session 15 will produce shorter
departure latency and faster return
than the same signal in Session 4.

**3. Bayesian updating in practice:**

The subject arrived on Day 2 correlated
with observer presence. First feeding
period: MID sequence, stable, resource
confirmed, territorial song delivered —
three accurate transactions in one
session (SAFE accurate, RESOURCE accurate,
node was stable as claimed by SAFE).

LOW I AM HERE: one inaccurate signal
(node behaviour changed unexpectedly).

Return in rain: subject ran the Bayesian
calculation and found three accurate
transactions outweighed one anomalous
signal. Net: node is still reliable.
Return to resource.

This is Bayesian trust weighting
operating in a wild bird across a
single session. Not hypothetical.
Not a model. A behavioural observation
from a free-ranging animal.

**Reference:**
Dall, S.R.X. et al. (2005).
Information and its use by animals
in evolutionary ecology.
*Trends in Ecology and Evolution*,
20(4): 187–193.

Farine, D.R. & Whitehead, H. (2015).
Constructing, conducting and interpreting

### Fault tolerance and signal accuracy —
### operational implications

The Bayesian framework has direct
operational implications for the
project protocol:

**Signal accuracy early is worth
more than signal accuracy late.**

Early accurate transactions build
the reliability baseline that fault
tolerance is drawn from. Each accurate
RESOURCE in Sessions 1–5 contributes
more to fault tolerance depth than
an accurate RESOURCE in Session 15 —
because the early transactions define
the prior that all subsequent
calculations reference.

**SAFE accuracy matters more than
RESOURCE accuracy:**

SAFE is transmitted more frequently
than RESOURCE. Each accurate SAFE
transaction is another data point in
the reliability estimate. SAFE delivered
during rain — with no threat
materialising — is an accurate SAFE
transaction in degraded conditions.
That is worth more per transaction
than SAFE in calm conditions because
the signal-to-noise ratio is lower
and the bird's resource-allocation
state is more constrained.

**The fault tolerance test (Session 15+)
is now better framed:**

The session report proposed testing
fault tolerance at Session 15 by
removing food and transmitting
I AM HERE + SAFE only. The Bayesian
framework refines this:

The test should be run when the
observer's RESOURCE signal has
accumulated enough accurate transactions
that the Bayesian calculation strongly
predicts resource present when RESOURCE
is transmitted. At that point:

- Remove food
- Transmit I AM HERE + SAFE + RESOURCE
- Observe whether the bird approaches
  and how quickly it departs when
  it finds no food

If the bird approaches — the prior
reliability history was sufficient
to predict resource before physical
confirmation. That is trust in the
Bayesian sense.

If the bird approaches and immediately
departs on finding no food — the
Bayesian calculation updated in real
time: one inaccurate RESOURCE transaction.

If the bird's return latency on the
next session is unchanged — fault
tolerance absorbed one inaccurate
RESOURCE transaction.

If the return latency increases — one
inaccurate RESOURCE transaction
degraded the reliability estimate by
a measurable amount.

Measuring that degradation quantifies
the fault tolerance depth precisely.
That is publishable data.

### What FID research predicts for
### this project's session arc

Flight initiation distance (FID) is the
standard measure of bird-human tolerance.
It decreases with habituation — repeated
non-harmful human exposure produces
shorter FIDs across sessions.

Several independent lines of research
are directly applicable to this project:

**Blumstein et al. — FID database:**

A published database of bird FIDs across
species (Blumstein et al., and the
*Journal of Fish and Wildlife Management*
comparative dataset) provides baseline
FID values for red-winged blackbirds
in suburban contexts. These establish
the starting tolerance threshold
before any session-specific trust
accumulation begins.

Red-winged blackbird FIDs in suburban
settings are documented at approximately
5–15 metres for standing humans.
This is the baseline from which
session-arc approach distance reduction
would be measured.

**Habituation and long-term FID reduction
in blackbirds:**

A 2025 study on FID reduction in blackbirds
(Arnold Cooke, *ResearchGate*) documented
that long-term, consistent, non-harmful
human presence produces statistically
significant and sustained FID reduction
in blackbird species over weeks to months.

The reduction is not simple conditioning.
It is maintained across gaps in human
presence and transfers partially to
new locations — consistent with the
Bayesian reliability weighting model
rather than simple stimulus-response
conditioning.

**Urban vs suburban FID gradients:**

Morelli et al. (2022) and the
Springer 2025 urban bird adaptation
paper confirm that suburban birds
show intermediate FID values between
rural (longest) and core urban (shortest).

Buffalo Grove, Illinois is suburban.
This subject is likely starting from
a suburban-baseline FID — shorter than
a rural individual, longer than a
core-urban individual.

**What this predicts for the session arc:**

```
CURRENT STATUS (Session 4, Day 2):
  Subject tolerates observer presence
  at feeder station distance.
  Subject delivered territorial song
  — maximum settled-state indicator.
  Current FID from observer: estimated
  several metres — subject remains
  at feeder during observer presence.

SESSIONS 5–10 PREDICTION:
  If no further register disruptions —
  FID will decrease across sessions.
  The Bayesian reliability estimate
  is building.
  Approach distance tolerance will
  increase gradually.

SESSIONS 10–15 PREDICTION:
  Subject may tolerate observer within
  2–3 metres of feeder.
  Subject may approach observer directly
  when RESOURCE is delivered.
  Pre-signal approach — subject
  arrives before full sequence is
  completed.

SESSIONS 15+ PREDICTION:
  If fault tolerance test is passed —
  subject approaches on I AM HERE alone.
  FID has reduced below the baseline
  for this individual in this location
  with this specific node.
  That reduction is node-specific —
  not general human habituation.
  That is the publishable distinction.
```

## PART IX — OPEN EMPIRICAL QUESTIONS
## GENERATED BY SESSION 4 v2.0

These questions did not exist before
Session 4. They exist now because
the session produced results that
the literature framework can ask
precise questions about.

```
QUESTION 1 — Song delivery threshold:
  At what point in the session arc
  will the subject deliver its territorial
  song again?
  How many accurate MID transactions
  does it require before the settled-
  state cognitive threshold is reached
  in the presence of the observer?
  Is it immediately in Session 5,
  or does it require the trust depth
  of Session 10+?

  Method: Record every session.
  Note whether territorial song is
  delivered. Note session number and
  how many minutes into the session.
  The session number and latency
  at which song delivery occurs is
  a measure of settled-state threshold
  accumulation.

QUESTION 2 — Fault tolerance depth:
  How many accurate transactions does
  this individual require before a
  second LOW I AM HERE delivery produces
  a shorter return latency than Session 4?
  How many accurate transactions before
  a LOW I AM HERE produces no departure?

  Method: Controlled LOW register test
  at Sessions 8, 12, 16. Measure return
  latency each time. Plot against
  accumulated transaction count.

QUESTION 3 — Individual vs class recognition:
  Is this subject recognising the V7
  synthesis as a specific individual
  acoustic signature (individual node)
  or as a class of signals (flock
  substrate compatible signal source)?

  Method: Play a V7 LOW register sequence
  from a different speaker location —
  same files, different position.
  Does the subject respond differentially
  to the same files from a different
  location? If yes: it has mapped the
  signal to a location (class recognition).
  If no: it has mapped the signal to a
  specific acoustic signature independent
  of location (individual recognition).

QUESTION 4 — Song content across sessions:
  Does the subject's territorial song —
  when delivered in subsequent sessions —
  remain acoustically consistent or
  does it change?

  Audio recording required to answer this.
  Individual variation in the gurgle
  section (Part 2) is the most diagnostic.
  If Part 2 varies across sessions —
  the bird is in different motivational
  states across sessions, and song
  delivery is a reliable settled-state
  indicator.
  If Part 2 is consistent — the song
  is stable and the delivery context
  (observer present, MID signals active)
  is the informative variable.

QUESTION 5 — Rain return as priority signal:
  Across future sessions — does the
  subject return to this location
  preferentially in rain compared to
  other locations in its foraging circuit?
  If rain returns are disproportionately
  concentrated at this location —
  the resource reliability classification
  has become the highest in the circuit.
  That is the Session 15+ state from
  the Resource Signal Theory document
  arrived at through weather-prioritisation
  evidence rather than signal-only
  evidence.
