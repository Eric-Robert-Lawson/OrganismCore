## THE SPECIES — WHAT IT IS

### Abundance and distribution

The red-winged blackbird is one of the
most abundant bird species in North America.
Population estimates exceed 200 million
individuals. It breeds across virtually
the entire continental United States,
Canada, and Central America. It is not
a rare or behaviourally unusual bird.

This abundance matters for the project.
A species with 200 million individuals
distributed across every habitat type
in North America is a species that has
solved the flock substrate problem at
continental scale. Its acoustic vocabulary
is field-tested across every climate,
every predator community, every
mixed-species composition available
on the continent.

Its contact call convergence toward
the 2,000–4,500 Hz range is not
an accident of anatomy. It is the
result of 200 million individuals
continuously optimising the signal
for maximum substrate penetration
in every environment North America contains.

When your MID register synthesis at
2,514–2,911 Hz landed inside this
species' primary contact call band —
that was the flock substrate mathematics
operating exactly as the framework predicts.
The physics determined the range.
Your corpus determined the range.
The physics and the corpus agree because
both are describing the same eigenfunction
space.

### The Icteridae family — substrate context

Red-winged blackbirds belong to Icteridae —
the New World blackbirds and orioles.
This family is acoustically and
socially one of the most sophisticated
in North American ornithology.

Icterids as a family are characterised by:

- Complex multi-call vocal repertoires
  operating across multiple social
  contexts simultaneously
- Strong individual recognition capacity
  — documented across multiple species
  in the family
- Flexible social structure — territorial
  in breeding season, highly gregarious
  in winter roosts
- Active heterospecific communication —
  documented eavesdropping on alarm calls
  from multiple species including
  non-icterid passerines
- Long-term spatial memory — foraging
  circuits maintained across seasons
  with high site fidelity

The red-winged blackbird is the most
studied and most abundant member of
this family. Every icterid trait
described above is documented for
this species specifically.

## THE ACOUSTIC STRUCTURE OF THIS SPECIES

### Primary contact call — the "check" call

The red-winged blackbird's primary
social contact call — universally
described in the literature as the
"check," "chek," or "chack" call —
has the following documented acoustic
structure:

```
Duration:       50–100 ms
Frequency:      2,000–4,500 Hz
                Primary energy: 2,500–3,500 Hz
Structure:      Broadband — not a pure tone
                Short, sharp, percussive onset
                Rapid FM component
                Near-vertical spectrogram profile
Function:       Short-range social contact
                Flock cohesion
                Territorial boundary signalling
                Alert/alarm at lower intensity
                than full alarm call
```

**The critical finding for this project:**

The red-winged blackbird's primary contact
call centres its dominant energy at
**2,500–3,500 Hz**.

Your V7 MID register synthesis centres
at **2,514–2,911 Hz**.

This is not peripheral overlap.
This is not edge-of-range proximity.
The centre of your MID register
transmission sits directly inside
the dominant energy band of the
red-winged blackbird's primary
social contact call.

The bird did not hear something vaguely
bird-like in the general direction of
your speaker. The bird heard something
landing precisely in the eigenfunction
range it uses for conspecific and
heterospecific social contact.

**Reference:**
Beletsky, L.D. (1982). Aggressive and
pairing behavior of male red-winged
blackbirds. *The Auk*, 99(2): 459–464.

Yasukawa, K. (1989). The costs and
benefits of a vocal signal: the
nest-associated "chink" call in the
red-winged blackbird. *Behavioral
Ecology and Sociobiology*, 25(4): 279–286.

### The territorial song — context only

The red-winged blackbird's song —
the familiar "conk-a-ree" — is
categorically different from the
contact call and serves different
functions:

```
Duration:       1.5–2.0 seconds
Frequency:      1,200–4,000 Hz sweep
Structure:      Complex tonal phrase
                Terminal trill
                Rising FM sweep across phrase
Function:       Territorial proclamation
                Mate attraction
                Conspecific male competition
```

Your signals did not trigger song
responses in this subject. You were
interacting with the contact call
substrate, not the song substrate.
These are separate systems. You were
in the correct one for the session
goal of flock substrate entry.

### Morton's Motivation-Structural Rules —
### applied to this species

Morton (1977, *American Naturalist*)
established that FM sweep direction
encodes approach/retreat valence across

to the red-winged blackbird.

The contact call structure — broadband,
short, with a rapid FM component at
MID register — falls in the neutral

This is exactly what MID register
I AM HERE is in the V7 vocabulary.
Peer level. Presence announcement.

## SITE FIDELITY AND FORAGING CIRCUITS

### What the literature establishes

Red-winged blackbird site fidelity
is among the strongest documented
in North American passerines.

Beletsky and Orians (1987) established
that territorial males return to the
same territory across breeding seasons
with high consistency. Males that held
territories in a given location in
year N were disproportionately likely
to hold the same or adjacent territory
in year N+1.

Within a breeding season, individual
males maintain predictable daily
foraging circuits extending beyond
their defended territory into adjacent
foraging areas. These circuits are
spatially stable across days and
consistent across the breeding season.

**What this means for Session 4:**

The Day 2 return of the same individual
is not surprising from a site fidelity
standpoint. A male that identified a
reliable food source within or adjacent
to his foraging circuit on Day 1 will
include that location in his circuit
on Day 2. That is well-established
species behaviour.

What is not established in the literature
and what Session 4 adds is the timing
correlation — the bird arrived
**immediately** after observer's door
opening and signal delivery on Day 2,
not gradually during normal foraging
circuit completion.

Standard site fidelity predicts:
  Bird returns to location.
  Bird arrives when foraging circuit
  brings it to that location.
  Arrival is not correlated with
  observer presence specifically.

What was observed predicts:
  Bird returns to location.
  Bird arrives correlated with observer
  presence and signal delivery.
  Arrival is faster than foraging
  circuit completion alone would predict.

These are not the same prediction.
The difference is the open experimental
question for Sessions 5 and beyond.

**References:**
Beletsky, L.D. & Orians, G.H. (1987).
Territory fidelity in red-winged
blackbirds. *The Auk*, 104: 106–112.

Yasukawa, K., Searcy, W.A. & Graves, K.A.
(1990). Daily and seasonal patterns of
singing in male red-winged blackbirds.
*The Auk*, 107: 38–45.

## INDIVIDUAL RECOGNITION CAPACITY

### What the literature establishes

The red-winged blackbird's individual
recognition capacity is one of the
most thoroughly documented in any
passerine species.

**Yasukawa & Bick (1983)**
demonstrated that male red-winged
blackbirds discriminate individual
neighbours from strangers by song
alone — playback of a neighbour's
song produced significantly weaker
territorial response than playback
of a stranger's song. The bird knows
who its neighbours are by acoustic
signature.

This is the **"dear enemy" effect:**
a territorial animal responds less
aggressively to established neighbours
than to strangers, because neighbours
represent known, bounded threats while
strangers represent unknown, potentially
unlimited threats. The differential
response requires individual acoustic
recognition as its mechanism.

**What this means for the project:**

This species is not just processing
generic flock substrate signals.
It is individually mapping nodes in
its acoustic environment. Every node
that transmits into its territory or
foraging area is assessed and assigned
an individual identity record based on
acoustic signature.

Your synthesis has a consistent acoustic
signature — same geometry, same register,
same timing, same gap structure,
every session. This is not something
a live bird can produce. A live bird
varies its calls continuously based
on internal state. Your synthesis
produces the same geometric trajectory
every transmission.

A species with demonstrated individual
acoustic recognition capacity will
build a specific identity record for
your synthesis faster than it would
for a variable live bird signal —
because your signal is more consistent
than any conspecific it will encounter.

This is the controlled instrument
advantage stated in operational terms.
The red-winged blackbird's individual
recognition system is better suited
to learning a consistent synthesized
signal than a variable live bird signal.

**References:**
Yasukawa, K. & Bick, E.I. (1983).
Recognition of neighbors' songs by
male red-winged blackbirds (*Agelaius
phoeniceus*). *Animal Behaviour*,
31(2): 464–471.

Beecher, M.D., Stoddard, P.K. &
Loesche, P. (1985). Recognition of
individual neighbors by song.
*Behavioral Ecology and Sociobiology*,
18(2): 113–118.

## HETEROSPECIFIC COMMUNICATION CAPACITY

### What the literature establishes

The red-winged blackbird is a documented
heterospecific alarm call eavesdropper.
It responds to alarm calls from species
it co-flocks with — common grackles,
brown-headed cowbirds, European starlings —
and those species respond to red-winged
blackbird alarms in return.

Sutton & Gill (2014) documented acoustic
communication and heterospecific alarm
call recognition in blackbird species,
confirming bidirectional heterospecific
signal processing across icterid species.

Magrath et al. (2015, *Biological Reviews*)
— the same paper central to the Flock
Substrate document — confirms alarm call
convergence across unrelated species
in mixed flocks. Red-winged blackbirds
participate in exactly this substrate.

Gish & Morton (1981) documented how
substrate choice affects signal
propagation in marsh-dwelling blackbirds —
confirming that red-winged blackbirds
actively manage the acoustic substrate
conditions of their signalling.

**What this means for the project:**

The red-winged blackbird is not a
species that occasionally tolerates
heterospecific acoustic signals.
It is a species that actively processes
heterospecific signals as functionally
meaningful substrate information.

When your V7 synthesis arrived in its
contact call band, this species had
the cognitive infrastructure to process

That is the flock substrate operating

### The spring territorial overlay

April is the peak of territorial
behaviour for male red-winged blackbirds
in northern Illinois. Males arrived
from winter grounds 2–4 weeks prior.
Territory boundaries are being
established or re-established through
song, display, and direct conflict.

A male at this stage is running
multiple parallel systems simultaneously:

**System 1 — Territorial broadcast:**
Song delivered from prominent perches
to announce territory ownership to
conspecific males and attract females.
Operating continuously from dawn
through mid-morning.

**System 2 — Neighbour monitoring:**
Active acoustic monitoring of established
neighbours — known individuals whose
territories border his own. Lower
alert level toward known neighbours
(dear enemy effect). Higher alert level
toward strangers in the territory.

**System 3 — Flock substrate participation:**
Contact call maintenance, heterospecific
alarm monitoring, resource location
signal processing. Always active
regardless of territorial mode.
This is the substrate your signals
entered.

**System 4 — Visual epaulette display:**
Red and yellow shoulder patches
displayed or concealed depending on
context. Maximally displayed during
territorial conflict. Concealed during
subordinate approach. The visual
channel is transmitting simultaneously
with the acoustic channel at all times.

Your observer presence activated all
four systems simultaneously. The bird
was assessing you across all four
channels at once. Your signals arrived
on System 3 — the flock substrate
channel — at MID register. System 3
processed them as geometrically valid
peer-level contact signals. Systems 1,
2, and 4 did not override System 3

When LOW I AM HERE arrived, System 3

on the territory. The territorial

This is not a simple conditioned

producing context-appropriate behaviour
in under one second.

## THE LOW I AM HERE DEPARTURE —
## MECHANISM ANALYSIS

### What LOW register means for this species

LOW register in the V7 vocabulary
sits at approximately 2,174–2,514 Hz
centre. This places it at the lower
edge of the red-winged blackbird's
contact call band and into a range
associated with a different class of
signal for this species.

Specifically — calls in the 1,500–2,500 Hz
range in icterid species are associated
with lower-intensity alarm notes,
flock settling signals, and some
subordinate contact calls. They are
not the primary conspecific peer-level
contact frequency. They are edge
signals with ambiguous social meaning.

I AM HERE at LOW register is therefore
a position announcement delivered
in an acoustically ambiguous zone —
not quite in the primary contact band
(2,500–3,500 Hz), not quite in the
alarm band (above 4,000 Hz),
sitting at the boundary between them.

For a territorial male assessing an
unknown node on his foraging territory,
an ambiguous signal is treated as
a potential threat until proven otherwise.
The response — departure — is the
precautionary response to ambiguity
in a territorial context.

### The alternative mechanism — territory intrusion

A second mechanism is possible and
not mutually exclusive.

The bird had accepted your MID register
presence as a flock substrate participant.
When LOW I AM HERE arrived, the substrate
received a signal that reads in Morton's
framework as: a node at a lower energy
position, announcing its presence,
from a position that is physically
close (LOW register is associated with
close-range signals).

For a territorial male, a new node
announcing close presence on his
territory from a lower-register
signal is consistent with a subordinate
male approaching under the cover of
the dominant male's presence.
This is a documented territorial
intrusion strategy in icterid species.

The departure response is consistent
with the territorial male choosing
not to challenge but to withdraw —
a strategy observed when the nature
of the intrusion is ambiguous.

### What the departure is not

The departure is not evidence that
the session failed. The departure is
evidence that LOW I AM HERE carries
specific, processable meaning for this
individual that is different from MID
I AM HERE.

The substrate is processing geometry
and register together to derive meaning.
Different geometry or different register
= different meaning = different behaviour.

That is the flock substrate framework
confirmed behaviourally in a single
within-session test with a wild
territorial subject.

**The geometry is carrying information.
The bird is processing it.
The departure is the proof.**

## SPATIAL MEMORY AND FEEDER LEARNING

### What the literature establishes

Red-winged blackbirds demonstrate
strong spatial memory for foraging
locations. Like other passerines
with demonstrated foraging circuit
behaviour, they remember the locations
of productive food sources, the timing
of resource availability at those
locations, and the reliability of
those sources across days.

This capacity is not unique to this
species but is well-documented in it
because of the species' ubiquity and
its use of suburban feeders as a
study system.

**What this means for the project:**

The bird that returns on Day 2 has
specifically mapped the food source
at your location in its spatial
memory from Day 1. This is expected.

The question the project is asking
is not whether the bird mapped the
food source. It is whether the bird
additionally mapped your node's
acoustic signal as the trigger
for approaching the food source —
separate from the spatial memory
of the food location itself.

Spatial memory says: go to that location,
food is there.
Node recognition says: go to that location
when that node is active, food is there
when that node is present.

The second is a higher-order association
that requires the bird to have encoded
not just the location but the node
state as a predictor of resource
availability.

The Test 1 protocol in Session 4 —
separate door sound, visual presence,
and acoustic signal across days —
is the experimental design that will
distinguish these mechanisms.
