## VOCAL REDUCTION — LITERATURE-GROUNDED REINTERPRETATION

### The question
The decrease in subject vocalisations across the session was initially recorded as ambiguous. Post-session literature review resolves this ambiguity completely.

### What the literature establishes
Cockatiel contact calls serve a specific function: **acoustic maintenance of social connection when physical contact is unavailable or uncertain**. Documented in:

- Seibert & Crowell-Davis (2001) — gender effects on allopreening, pair bonding, and aggression in captive cockatiels. *Applied Animal Behaviour Science.*
- Bradbury & Balsby (2016) — ecology and behaviour of the cockatiel.

| Behaviour | Non-bonded / newly contacted | Bonded pair |
|---|---|---|
| Contact calls | Frequent | Reduced / absent |
| Allopreening | Rare | Frequent / mutual |
| General vocalisation | High | Lower |
| Silence in proximity | Rare | Common — comfort signal |
| Directed demand vocalisations | Absent | Present — partner communication |

**Bonded pairs reduce contact calls because physical presence and tactile interaction replace the acoustic contact-maintenance function.** Silence in close proximity is a documented comfort signal and marker of stable pair-bond formation.

### What this means for the session

**Phase 1 — Contact call phase:** Subject produces frequent contact calls. Correct behaviour for position exchange with an unknown node.

**Phase 2 — Transition phase:** Contact calls reduce. Allopreening initiates. Possessive behaviour emerges. Demand vocalisations to an established partner begin. Back and wing contact permitted. Head-curling solicitation posture produced. The subject had already decided. The vocal reduction was the announcement of that decision.

**Phase 3 — Bonded pair phase:** Silence in proximity. Physical attachment. Distress on separation. Owner-reported mating behaviour at terminal event.

### The reinterpretation — stated precisely

> The silence was not ambiguity. It was completion. The subject stopped producing contact calls because contact calls are what you produce before the bond is formed. Once the bond is formed, you stop. The subject went silent because it had finished deciding. The silence was the strongest signal of the session. It came last because it came after the decision had already been made.

## BACK AND WING CONTACT — LITERATURE-GROUNDED ANALYSIS

### What occurred
During Phase 2, the observer petted the subject on its back and wings. This was done without any knowledge of the significance of those contact zones. The observer did not know this was different from petting the head. The subject permitted this contact. The subject subsequently curled its head down into the observer's body — a solicitation posture. The owner described this as "almost like not-bird behaviour."

### What the literature establishes
In psittacine ornithology, contact zones are functionally differentiated:

| Contact zone | Substrate function | Who delivers it |
|---|---|---|
| Head and neck | Allopreening — social, non-sexual flock bond maintenance | Flock members, trusted companions |
| Back, wings, tail base | Mating stimulus — courtship and mate bonding | Chosen mate only |

This is documented in avian veterinary and ornithological literature. Touching the back and wings of a psittacine stimulates nerves in areas associated with copulation. The documented responses to back and wing contact from a classified mate include: crouching, arching the back into a sexually receptive posture, tail quivering, vocalisation, and regurgitation. Avian veterinary guidance explicitly advises owners to avoid the back and wing zones because stimulating them triggers sexual behaviour and mate-classification responses.

A cockatiel does not normally permit a stranger to touch its back and wings. The literature is unambiguous: most cockatiels will move away, fluff defensively, or display avoidance behaviour when an unfamiliar person attempts contact in those areas. Permission of back and wing contact from a new individual is itself a bonding signal — it means the subject has already classified the toucher as safe enough for intimate contact.

### What this means for the session

The observer delivered mating-stimulus contact to the subject mid-session, without knowing that was what was being delivered. The subject:

1. **Permitted** the contact — which requires prior trust classification at a level not normally extended to strangers.
2. **Responded** with a head-curling solicitation posture — presenting the back of the head and neck, requesting grooming from a classified bonded partner.
3. **Escalated** to the terminal behaviour reported by the owner at session close.

This is a coherent causal sequence. The mating-stimulus contact was delivered in Phase 2. The subject received it as a classified mate would receive it. The terminal event is the downstream expression of that sequence.

The owner's framing — "almost like not-bird behaviour" — was her attempt to account for a solicitation posture she had never seen this subject direct at a human. It was not not-bird behaviour. It was the most bird behaviour possible: a bonded subject soliciting grooming from its classified mate. The owner had simply never seen the subject produce it before, because no human in the reference population had previously entered through the flock substrate and delivered mating-zone contact.

### The compounding structure

The session produced pair-bond classification through two independent channels:

**Channel 1 — Acoustic:** Deliberate flock substrate communication from session open. Nine-call vocabulary. Contact call phase → vocal reduction → silence. The substrate entered through the eigenfunction positions the subject uses for flock bonding.

**Channel 2 — Tactile:** Naive delivery of mating-stimulus contact to the back and wings. No deliberate intent. The subject's permission of this contact, and its solicitation response, confirm that the pair-bond classification established through Channel 1 was already in place when the contact occurred.

Both channels produced convergent evidence for the same conclusion: the subject had classified the observer as a pair-bond partner. The acoustic channel established it. The tactile channel confirmed it. The terminal event was the subject expressing that classification at the maximum available level under conditions of separation distress.
