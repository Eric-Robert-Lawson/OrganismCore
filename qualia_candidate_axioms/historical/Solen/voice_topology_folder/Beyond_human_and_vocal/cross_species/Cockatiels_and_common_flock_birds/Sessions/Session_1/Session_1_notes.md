## OBSERVER PROFILE — RELEVANT TO INTERPRETATION

The following details are documented because they bear directly on the attachment outcome and its interpretation.

- Observer is **not a bird person**. No prior experience handling or bonding with birds. No practiced ease with avian subjects.
- Observer displayed **visible discomfort** during the session: startled when subject flew onto them; uncomfortable when defecated upon. No hostile behaviour at any point, but the observer was not relaxed or open in the way conventionally associated with fast animal bonding.
- Observer had **seen the subject before in passing** but had never interacted with it directly. This was the first substantive contact.
- The subject has an established **reference population** of humans with varying levels of familiarity and affinity: the owner (primary bond), the owner's sons (regular contact, one with notable affinity for the bird), one son's friend (also liked by the bird), and the observer's girlfriend (has held the subject previously).
- The owner, upon observing the session outcome, characterised the observer as a **"bird whisperer"** — reaching for a dispositional property of the observer rather than a property of what the observer did.

This framing is documented as an instance of the **type-fixity error**: attributing the result to a fixed personal attribute of the observer rather than to the method deployed. The observer is not a bird whisperer. The observer is not experienced with birds, was not comfortable with the bird, and was not especially open or warm in the way that characterisation implies. The bird did not respond to the observer's ease. It responded **despite** the observer's unease.

The reference population detail sharpens the result: the owner has observed the subject's responses to multiple humans across varying levels of familiarity and affinity. The outcome of this single session was distinguishable from that entire reference population — clearly enough that the owner named it. This is not anecdote. It is a comparison with an existing baseline.

## PHASE SUMMARY

### Phase 1 — Initial Contact
- **Signals used:** I AM HERE, SAFE, WHERE ARE YOU, ACKNOWLEDGE
- **Response:** Strong. Subject responded verbally and auditorially upon initial contact. Oriented toward observer. Reciprocal vocalisations produced.
- **Assessment:** Eigenfunction position was recognised on first delivery. Verbal reciprocation on first contact is the expected Stage 2 behaviour (Position Exchange) per `Cockatiel_Engagement_Protocol.md`. The subject initiated the exchange; the observer was not the only active party. This vocal behaviour is consistent with the literature on non-bonded or newly contacted nodes: contact calls are the instrument for establishing and maintaining connection with an unknown node before physical proximity is achieved.

### Phase 2 — Sustained Engagement and Assimilation Arc
- **Observation:** Verbal response from subject decreased as session progressed. Non-verbal and behavioural responses remained active and increased in intensity throughout. Physical attachment behaviour escalated continuously. Subject was observed preening the observer. Subject was observed directing possessive behaviour toward the observer. Subject was observed vocalising in a manner the owner described as "yelling" — insistent, directed vocalisations communicating specific desires to the observer (e.g., indicating desire to have its head rubbed when the observer was not doing so).
- **Assessment:** The decrease in verbal contact calls co-occurred with an increase in physical bonding behaviour, allopreening, and directed communicative demands. This is now understood — in light of the literature — to be the expected arc of pair-bond formation. See full analysis in the Vocal Reduction section below. The "yelling" behaviour is significant: it is not flock substrate signalling. It is the subject communicating individual desire to the observer as a bonded partner — demanding reciprocal care from a node it had already classified as its pair bond. This is qualitatively different from flock substrate exchange. It is the subject treating the observer as an established intimate partner and expressing needs accordingly.

### Phase 3 — Attachment and Assimilation
- **Observation:** Subject displayed strong, escalating preference for observer over owner. Sought physical proximity to observer. Showed distress when separated. Displayed submission and comfort postures (head bowing, nuzzling, curling up against observer). Showed no distress when owner left the room briefly.
- **Assessment:** The subject assimilated the observer as a high-value node within a single session. The rate of assimilation exceeded what the owner considered normal or expected. This is the central unexpected result of this session and is documented in full below.

### Phase 3 — Terminal Event (End of Session)
- **Observation:** Subject was placed into its enclosure at session end. Subject displayed visible distress and panic-adjacent behaviour. Observer placed a finger into the enclosure to comfort the subject. The subject scrambled to make contact with the observer's hand. At this point, the owner — not the observer — noted and verbally reported that the subject appeared to be attempting to mate with the observer's hand.
- **Attribution:** This interpretation is the owner's, stated aloud during the event. The observer was not attending closely to the subject's specific behaviour at this moment and cannot independently confirm the behavioural classification. The owner is the most qualified person present to make this interpretation, having observed the subject's full behavioural repertoire across its lifetime. The statement was made spontaneously, not in response to prompting.
- **Assessment:** Documented as owner-attributed behavioural observation. Confidence level: owner-reported, not independently confirmed by observer. See full analysis below.

## CALL-BY-CALL RESPONSE LOG

### ACKNOWLEDGE
- **How used:** In deliberate response to subject vocalisations, as an affirmative signal. Also used to establish deliberate connection before physical interaction (e.g., prior to attempting to touch the subject's head — to alert, affirm, and make intentional contact explicit). Used as the primary reciprocal signal throughout the session.
- **Response:** Subject responded to acknowledgement signals with orientation, engagement, and continued interaction. The signal appeared to function as intended: establishing that the observer was a deliberate, communicating agent rather than a passive presence.
- **Assessment:** ACKNOWLEDGE appears to have been a structurally critical signal in this session. It was the primary instrument for signalling deliberate agency to the subject. The subject's sustained engagement is consistent with the interpretation that it was registering intentional reciprocation, not random sound production. This is the signal most directly responsible for the subject recognising the observer as a communicating node rather than an environmental stimulus.

### ALARM
- **How used:** Deployed deliberately in a non-threatening environment to test signal reception and reliability.
- **Response:** Every deployment produced a consistent, clear alarm response:
  - Subject stopped preening immediately
  - Subject oriented toward observer
  - Feathers on head straightened (crest raised)
  - Visible stress indicators present
  - Response occurred without fail on every delivery
- **Observer note:** This signal was treated with care. Alarm is a global radial survival signal; the expectation was that it must produce a response without fail, as failure to respond to alarm in a flock context is not survivable. That prediction was confirmed — the response was not optional or variable. It was structurally obligate.
- **ALL CLEAR was always administered after ALARM** to alleviate the false signal. The alleviation effect was immediate and observable.
- **Assessment:** ALARM is confirmed as the most structurally robust signal in the vocabulary. It is the hardest signal to fake — the response is not learned, it is survival-wired. Every successful delivery confirms the signal is landing in the correct eigenfunction position. Every delivery in this session confirmed this. This is the most falsifiable test in the vocabulary and it passed on every trial.

### ALL CLEAR
- **How used:** Primarily as immediate follow-up to ALARM. Also used in combination with SAFE as part of the comfort protocol established during the session.
- **Response:** Immediate calming observable on every deployment following ALARM. When combined with SAFE, produced visibly increased relaxation and affiliative behaviour.
- **Assessment:** The subject tracked the state transition from alarm to safe every time. This is a higher-order result than simple alarm response — it confirms the subject is reading trajectory through eigenfunction space, not just registering isolated signals. The alarm → all clear sequence is a two-point trajectory confirmation. It passed on every trial.

### SAFE
- **How used:** As a de-stressor and relationship signal. Combined frequently with ALL CLEAR and I AM HERE. Became one of the three primary comfort signals in the session alongside I AM HERE and ALL CLEAR.
- **Response:** Visibly affectionate response. Subject became calmer and more physically affiliative following SAFE signals.
- **Observer note on the 'love bombing' assimilation protocol:** The combination of I AM HERE + SAFE + ALL CLEAR + ACKNOWLEDGE formed an integrated comfort and relationship-building protocol during this session. The ordering was fluid, but the combined effect was consistent: the subject de-stressed, oriented toward observer, and deepened affiliative behaviour. This was not accidental — it was a deliberate attempt to build trust rapidly via the flock substrate.
- **Assessment:** SAFE is confirmed as a direct de-stressor. The response was affectionate and clear. The integrated comfort protocol (I AM HERE + SAFE + ALL CLEAR + ACKNOWLEDGE) produced measurable de-stressing and affiliative response and appears to have been the primary driver of rapid trust accumulation.

### I AM HERE
- **How used:** As the primary self-identification and node-entry signal. Used at session opening and throughout as a relational anchor. Used as part of the integrated comfort protocol. Functioned both as "I am present, I am a node, I am here to be found" and as a personalisation/familiarisation signal.
- **Response:** Responsive from first delivery. Subject oriented, vocalised in return initially. Remained a structurally central signal throughout the session — not the most dramatic in terms of observable response, but the foundation for everything else.
- **Assessment:** I AM HERE appears to have functioned as intended: establishing the observer's eigenfunction position in the substrate and making the observer locatable and relatable to the subject. It was the entry signal and also the relationship signal. Its role in the rapid assimilation observed is foundational — without it, SAFE and ALL CLEAR would not have had a node to attach to.

### WHERE ARE YOU
- **How used:** As a query signal — asking the subject to identify its position in return.
- **Response:** Initial verbal response on first delivery, then verbal response ceased. Behavioural orientation continued.
- **Assessment:** The initial verbal response confirms the signal was decoded as a position exchange invitation and the subject responded to it. The cessation of verbal response is now understood, in light of the literature, as the expected consequence of pair-bond formation — not a decoding failure or loss of engagement. See Vocal Reduction section below.

### COME NOW
- **How used:** As a recruitment signal. Also tested in combination with physical gesture (extending finger toward subject as a landing target — offered as invitation rather than command).
- **Response:** Subject looked around and appeared confused. Orientation observed. No directed movement toward observer as a result of the signal alone.
- **Assessment:** Contextual inapplicability, not signal failure. The subject was already in proximity to the observer and in an affiliative, settled state. A recruitment signal to a node that has already arrived is structurally redundant. The orientation response suggests the signal was registered but the subject had no appropriate action to take in response.

### MOVING

- **Response:** Owner reported the subject appeared happy. Observer could not independently confirm the response.
- **Assessment:** Least applicable signal in this context. Indoor static proximity reduces navigational signal relevance. Context effect, not signal failure.

### RESOURCE
- **How used:** Deployed while placing a physical resource (food/water/toys) near the subject's usual resource area.
- **Response:** Initial orientation and response. Subject then showed greater interest in the observer than in the indicated resource. Signal use faded toward end of session.
- **Assessment:** The subject was in a primary socialisation state, not a resource-seeking state. The observer had become the higher-value node. Initial orientation suggests the signal was decoded; subsequent prioritisation of the observer over the resource is consistent with the assimilation arc — and with the pair-bond classification hypothesis. ## VOCAL REDUCTION — LITERATURE-GROUNDED REINTERPRETATION

### The question
The decrease in subject vocalisations across the session was initially recorded as ambiguous — interpreted as possible loss of engagement or a limit of the communication attempt. Post-session literature review resolves this ambiguity completely.

### What the literature establishes
Cockatiel contact calls serve a specific function: **acoustic maintenance of social connection when physical contact is unavailable or uncertain**. This is documented across the Psittaciformes literature, including:

- Seibert & Crowell-Davis (2001) — gender effects on allopreening, pair bonding, and aggression in captive cockatiels. *Applied Animal Behaviour Science.*
- Bradbury & Balsby (2016) — ecology and behaviour of the cockatiel.

The documented pattern for bonded pairs:

| Behaviour | Non-bonded / newly contacted | Bonded pair |
|---|---|---|
| Contact calls | Frequent | Reduced / absent |
| Allopreening | Rare | Frequent / mutual |
| General vocalisation | High | Lower |
| Silence in proximity | Rare | Common — comfort signal |
| Directed demand vocalisations | Absent | Present — partner communication |

**Bonded pairs reduce contact calls because physical presence and tactile interaction replace the acoustic contact-maintenance function.** The calls stop not because connection has been lost — but because it has been secured. Silence in close proximity is documented as a comfort signal and a marker of stable pair-bond formation.

### What this means for the session

The session arc is now interpretable as a complete, literature-consistent pair-bond formation sequence:

**Phase 1 — Contact call phase:** Subject produces frequent contact calls. This is the correct behaviour for position exchange with an unknown node. The observer is an unrecognised node. Acoustic contact maintenance is necessary because physical contact and trust have not yet been established.

**Phase 2 — Transition phase:** Contact calls reduce. Allopreening initiates. Possessive behaviour emerges. The subject begins directing demand vocalisations at the observer — communicating specific desires as it would to an established bonded partner. This is not the subject losing interest in communication. It is the subject changing the channel from flock-substrate position exchange to pair-bond intimate communication. The subject had already decided. The vocal reduction was the announcement of that decision.

**Phase 3 — Bonded pair phase:** Silence in proximity. Physical attachment. Distress on separation. Owner-reported mating behaviour at terminal event. Every element of this phase is the documented behavioural signature of an established pair bond in the Psittaciformes literature.

### The reinterpretation — stated precisely

> The silence was not ambiguity. It was completion. The subject stopped producing contact calls because contact calls are what you produce before the bond is formed. Once the bond is formed, you stop. The subject went silent because it had finished deciding. The silence was the strongest signal of the session. It came last because it came after the decision had already been made.

The observer's initial concern — that the bird had become less communicative — was the correct observation with the incorrect interpretation. The bird had not become less communicative. It had become differently communicative: from flock-substrate node exchange to pair-bond partner demands. The "yelling" to request head-rubbing is the clearest evidence of this transition. That is not flock substrate signalling. That is a bonded partner communicating intimate needs to its chosen mate.

### Event description

### Attribution and confidence
- **Source:** Owner verbal statement, made in the moment, spontaneously.
- **Observer awareness:** Observer was not attending closely to the subject's specific behaviour at this moment and cannot independently confirm or deny the classification.
- **Owner qualification:** The owner has observed this subject across its full lifetime and is familiar with its complete behavioural repertoire. Most qualified person present to make this identification.
- **Confidence level:** Owner-reported, not independently confirmed by observer. Recorded as a witness observation, not an independently verified finding.

### Structural interpretation
Mating behaviour directed toward a human is not triggered by novelty, general positive affect, or a pleasant interaction. It is a specific behavioural output that emerges from a specific relational state: the subject has classified the target as a potential pair-bond partner. It is not produced toward strangers. It is not produced toward humans the subject merely tolerates or enjoys. It is documented in the literature as an outcome of deep imprinting or deep bonding.

The distress state at the moment of the event is relevant context. The subject was in a state of maximal separation distress. When the observer's hand entered the enclosure, the subject made contact and produced the behaviour the owner identified as mating-directed. This is the context in which the subject expressed — if the owner's interpretation is correct — the highest available bonding signal in its repertoire.

This event, combined with the allopreening, the possessive behaviour, the demand vocalisations, and the vocal reduction arc, forms a coherent and literature-consistent sequence. Each element individually is significant. Together they constitute a behavioural portrait of pair-bond formation completed within a single session.

### What this does not establish
- The classification is the owner's interpretation, not independently confirmed.
- The observer cannot confirm it.
- A single instance in a high-distress state cannot be taken as a stable expression of pair-bond classification without further observation.
- Video review is required.

### What this does establish
- The owner — most qualified observer present — interpreted the behaviour as mating-directed.
- The behaviour occurred at the moment of highest bonding expression in the session.
- It is structurally coherent with the full session arc.
- It is documented with full attribution clarity: owner-stated, observer-unconfirmed.
