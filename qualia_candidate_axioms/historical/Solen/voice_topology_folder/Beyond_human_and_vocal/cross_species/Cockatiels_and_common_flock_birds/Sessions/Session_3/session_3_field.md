## SIGNAL PROTOCOL

### Signal hierarchy for this session

**Priority 1 — I AM HERE**
Delivered first on entry. Quietly.
Not directed at Melon specifically.
Delivered to the room as ambient
node presence announcement.
Repeat periodically throughout session.
This is the foundation signal.
Melon needs to know a node is present
before any other signal is meaningful.

**Priority 2 — SAFE**
Primary signal for this session.
Delivered continuously as ambient
substrate signal throughout.
Not directed at Melon specifically.
To the room. To the space.
Let Melon receive it as environmental
information rather than as direct
address — direct address from an
unclassified node to a wild-type
subject can read as threat behaviour.
SAFE delivered as ambient signal
does not trigger that response.

**Priority 3 — ALARM (single test)**
Deliver once only in this session.
Observe response precisely.
Follow immediately with ALL CLEAR.
Do not repeat alarm testing in
this session unless Melon's response
to the first delivery is ambiguous
and a second test is needed for
confirmation. Maximum two ALARM
deliveries this session.

**Timing of ALARM test:**
Do not deliver ALARM early in the session.
Allow at least 10-15 minutes of SAFE
and I AM HERE signals to accumulate
in the substrate before testing ALARM.
Deliver ALARM from a position of
established ambient presence, not
as the first signal Melon receives.

**Priority 4 — ALL CLEAR**
Delivered immediately after every
ALARM without exception.
Also delivered if Melon shows any
spontaneous alarm or stress behaviour
during session — observer does not
need to have produced the alarm.
If Melon alarms for any reason,
deliver ALL CLEAR immediately.

**Priority 5 — ACKNOWLEDGE**
Delivered in response to any
vocalisation from Melon toward
observer or toward the room.
Every vocalisation. Without exception.
Immediately. Do not let a vocalisation
go unacknowledged.
A wild-type bird that vocalises
toward a new node and receives
no acknowledgement learns that
the node does not participate
in the substrate exchange.
Every acknowledgement is a step
toward node recognition.

### Signals withheld this session

**COME NOW** — Do not recruit.
Do not attempt to bring Melon toward
observer through direct signal.
Let any approach be fully voluntary
and substrate-driven. Recruitment
before trust is established produces
retreat, not approach.

**RESOURCE** — Not this session.
Resource signals from an unclassified
node to a wild-type subject can
produce competition behaviour
rather than affiliation behaviour.
Withhold until trust baseline established.

**Pair-bond substrate signals** — Not this session.
Do not deploy pair-bond formation
signals with Melon in Session 3.
The substrate is not ready.
Attempting pair-bond signals before
safe-node classification is established
will produce no response at best
and threat response at worst.
The bonding arc begins with safety.
Safety comes first.

## BEHAVIOURAL OBSERVATION GUIDE

### What to watch — Melon specifically

**Crest position**
- Flat or slightly raised: relaxed to mildly alert
- Fully raised: alert, assessing, potentially alarmed
- Slicked back flat against head: fear response
Document crest position at session start,
after each signal delivery, and at session end.
Track trajectory across session.

**Body posture**
- Upright, feathers tight: alert, watchful
- Feathers slightly fluffed, body relaxed: comfort
- Body lowered, feathers tight, beak forward:
  threat display imminent or active
- Feathers fluffed, eyes closing: settling, safe

**Eye behaviour**
- Both eyes tracking observer: active assessment
- One eye toward observer, one away: peripheral
  monitoring, reduced threat assessment
- Eyes softening, blinking slowly: comfort
  state developing
- Eyes closing: deep comfort, trust present
  (significant if occurs in Session 3 —
  above threshold expectation)
- Pinning (pupils rapidly dilating/contracting):
  high arousal, excitement or agitation

**Distance from observer**
- Measure approximate distance at session start
- Record any change in distance across session
- Direction of change matters more than magnitude:
  decreasing distance = positive trajectory
  increasing distance = substrate not yet ready
  stable distance = assessment ongoing

**Orientation**
- Body oriented toward observer: active engagement
- Head turned toward observer, body neutral:
  monitoring without commitment
- Body oriented away: disengagement or avoidance
- Periodic glancing toward observer: peripheral
  awareness, not yet direct engagement

**Vocalisation types to document**
- Contact calls directed toward observer:
  positive, above threshold for first session
- Contact calls directed to room generally:
  ambient substrate signal, note frequency
- Alarm calls: note what preceded them
- Silence: note context — is it watchfulness
  or is it settled? What preceded it?
- Threat vocalisations (hissing, growling):
  note what triggered them, observer position
  at time of occurrence

## EXPECTED RESPONSE SEQUENCE

This is the expected arc for a wild-type
subject in a first individual session.
It may not complete fully in one session.
Document how far along the arc Melon reaches.

### Stage 1 — Threat assessment
**Expected duration:** First 5-15 minutes
**What it looks like:**
- Crest raised
- Body upright, feathers tight
- Eyes tracking observer continuously
- Possible retreat to maximum available distance
- Silence or low-level alarm vocalisations
- No orientation toward observer's signals

**What to do:** Nothing different.
Deliver SAFE and I AM HERE quietly.
Do not move. Do not approach.
Let Stage 1 run its course.
Attempting to accelerate past Stage 1
will reset it.

### Stage 2 — Sustained assessment
**Expected duration:** Variable — may be
most of the session for a first encounter
**What it looks like:**
- Crest position fluctuating — not fully raised,
  not fully flat
- Body posture slightly less rigid
- Eye tracking continues but with occasional
  breaks — Melon looks away and looks back
- Possible preening — self-preening in the
  presence of a new node is a mild comfort
  signal, not indifference
- Silence continues or mild contact calls
  begin to emerge

**What to do:** Maintain signals.
SAFE and I AM HERE continuously.
If Melon preens, do not move toward Melon.
Preening is a comfort signal but it is
not an invitation.

### Stage 3 — Provisional acceptance
**Expected duration:** May not reach this
stage in Session 3. Document if it appears.
**What it looks like:**
- Crest relaxed or partially relaxed
- Body posture softer — feathers slightly
  less tight
- Distance may decrease slightly —
  Melon moves to a closer perch without
  being directed to
- Possible contact call reciprocation
- Orientation toward observer during signals

**What to do:** Continue exactly as before.
Do not reward Stage 3 with movement toward
Melon. Let Melon hold Stage 3 at Melon's
chosen distance. Any movement toward Melon
at this point may reset to Stage 1 or 2.

### Stage 4 — Active approach
**Not expected in Session 3.**
Document immediately if it occurs.
This would be an above-threshold result
and would suggest Melon is further toward
the domesticated end of the gradient
than currently assessed.

## ALARM TEST PROTOCOL

The alarm test is the most structurally
important signal delivery of this session.
It confirms the signals are landing in
the correct eigenfunction positions in
a wild-type substrate — independent of
any bonding or trust behaviour.

### Procedure
1. Wait until at least 10-15 minutes of
   ambient SAFE and I AM HERE have been
   delivered and Melon is in sustained
   assessment state (not peak alarm state)
2. Deliver ALARM
3. Observe immediately:
   - Freeze response
   - Crest response
   - Vocalisation
   - Physical movement — direction and distance
   - Eye behaviour
4. Deliver ALL CLEAR immediately — within
   seconds of alarm response peak
5. Observe de-stressing:
   - Crest return to pre-alarm position
   - Body posture softening
   - Resumption of prior behaviour
   - Vocalisation change

### What to document
- Melon's state immediately before ALARM delivery
- Latency of response — how quickly did alarm
  behaviour appear after signal
- Nature of response — what specific behaviours
- Peak intensity of response
- ALL CLEAR delivery timing
- Latency and completeness of de-stressing
- Melon's state 2-3 minutes after ALL CLEAR

### Why this matters for the gradient
Alarm response latency and intensity in
a wild-type subject may differ from
a domesticated subject. A wild-type bird
may show faster, more intense alarm response
— survival pressure is higher in the
wild-type substrate. The de-stressing
arc after ALL CLEAR may be slower —
a wild-type substrate holds the alarm
state longer as an adaptive response.

Document both the alarm and the recovery
with equal precision. The recovery arc
is as important as the alarm response
for understanding how the signal operates
in a wild-type substrate.

## WHAT TO DO IF SESSION GOES POORLY

### If Melon shows escalating threat displays
- Stop moving immediately
- Do not retreat
- Hold position
- Deliver SAFE quietly and repeatedly
- Wait for display to subside
- If display does not subside within
  2-3 minutes, end session
- Do not force continued presence
  against sustained defensive behaviour
- Document what preceded the escalation

### If Melon retreats to maximum distance
and maintains it throughout
- Do not follow
- Continue signals from current position
- This is Stage 1 holding — correct
  for wild-type first session
- End session before Melon escalates
  from retreat to active defence
- Document distance held and any
  changes in posture or orientation
  across the session duration

### If Melon shows no response to ALARM
- Deliver a second ALARM after 2-3 minutes
- If no response on second delivery,
  this is a significant finding —
  document precisely and note
  signal delivery method, distance,
  and Melon's state at time of delivery
- Do not interpret as signal failure
  without first reviewing delivery
  — confirm signal was deployed correctly

### If Melon attacks observer
- Move away calmly and slowly
- Do not make sudden movements
- Do not vocalise sharply
- End session
- Document what preceded the attack
- This would indicate Melon's substrate
  classified observer as threat before
  safe-node signals could accumulate
- Session 4 with Melon would require
  redesigned approach — longer distance,
  shorter duration, higher signal density

## SESSION CLOSE PROTOCOL

### How to end the session correctly
Do not end the session abruptly.
Do not stand up suddenly.
Do not move quickly toward the exit.

Slow down signals 2-3 minutes before
intended end. Deliver SAFE and ALL CLEAR
as the final signals. Rise slowly.
Move to exit slowly. Do not make direct
eye contact with Melon as you leave.

**Why this matters:**
How you leave is what Melon's substrate
records last. A calm, non-threatening
departure reinforces the safe-node
classification that the session has
been building. An abrupt departure
can register as threat behaviour
and partially reset the substrate
work done during the session.

Leave the way you want Melon to
remember you were there.
