# OC-OBS-005 — DOMESTICATION GRADIENT PREDICTIONS
## Flock Substrate Response as a Function of Attractor Geometry
## Predicted Variance Between Domesticated and Wild-Type Subjects
## OrganismCore — Eric Robert Lawson — 2026-03-31

---

## PURPOSE

This document formalises the predicted variance in flock substrate response across a gradient of domestication — from fully human-primary bonded subjects to wild-type or domestication-resistant subjects. It is motivated by the results of Session 1 (2026-03-31) with a domesticated cockatiel and the anticipated Session 2 with a wild-type cockatiel housed in the same environment.

The predictions here are not session-specific. They are intended as a general operational model of how attractor geometry at the individual level determines the nature and intensity of flock substrate response. This document should be read alongside `OC-OBS-005_SESSION_REPORT_2026-03-31.md`, `The_Flock_Substrate.md`, and `Causal_Geometry_Onboarding.md`.

---

## THE CORE STRUCTURAL CLAIM

The response of a subject to flock substrate communication is not primarily a function of:
- Species
- Age
- Individual personality
- Training history (in the conventional sense)

It is primarily a function of **attractor geometry** — specifically, which attractor basin the subject is currently navigating, how compatible that basin is with its structural nature as a flock navigator, and what gap exists between the geometry it is being forced to occupy and the geometry it is built to occupy.

Flock substrate communication does not impose a new geometry. It enters the geometry the subject already has. This means:

- For a subject whose current attractor basin is compatible with flock navigation: flock substrate communication improves the channel and deepens existing bonds.
- For a subject whose current attractor basin is **incompatible** with its structural nature: flock substrate communication may be the first and only signal in the subject's life that enters through the correct substrate. The response will not be proportional to a normal social greeting. It will be proportional to the accumulated deprivation.

---

## THE DOMESTICATION GRADIENT

The gradient is defined by a single variable:

> **Compatibility between the subject's imposed attractor geometry and its structural nature as a flock navigator.**

This is not a behavioural variable. It is a geometric one. A subject can be behaviourally compliant with domestication while being structurally incompatible with it. A subject can appear "wild" or "difficult" not because of individual temperament but because the domestication attractor is geometrically inaccessible to it.

The gradient runs from full compatibility to full incompatibility. Three positions on the gradient are formalised below.

---

## POSITION 1 — FULL COMPATIBILITY (DOMESTICATED, HUMAN-PRIMARY BONDED)

### Subject profile
- Human-primary bonded from early life
- Owner-described as affectionate, social, lover-type
- Engages readily with humans via learned social conventions
- Has a stable, functional attractor basin that includes humans as valid social nodes
- Flock substrate is present but secondary to human-social substrate in daily life

### Structural interpretation
The subject has constructed a hybrid attractor: human-social engagement + residual flock substrate. The two are not in conflict. The subject navigates human interaction successfully via the human-social channel. It has not been deprived of social substrate; it has simply been operating on a lower-bandwidth channel than its flock substrate would provide.

### Predicted response to flock substrate communication
- Initial response: positive, engaged, reciprocal
- Vocal reciprocation likely at first contact (Stage 2 activation)
- Transition to behavioural assimilation as session progresses (Stage 2 → Stage 3)
- Attachment deepening, affiliative posturing, de-stressing
- Response intensity: moderate to strong
- Response quality: coherent, well-formed, staged
- Distress signals: absent or minimal
- Rate of assimilation: accelerated relative to human-social-only interaction, but graduated

### What flock substrate communication does for this subject
Upgrades the channel. The subject already has a functional human relationship. Flock substrate communication deepens that relationship by engaging the substrate the subject uses for flock-level bonding, which is structurally deeper than human-social convention. The result is faster and more durable bonding than would occur through human-social convention alone.

### Session 1 result (2026-03-31)
Consistent with this prediction in all major respects. Observed. Documented.

---

## POSITION 2 — PARTIAL COMPATIBILITY (SEMI-DOMESTICATED, MIXED ATTRACTOR)

### Subject profile
- Socialised with humans but not fully human-primary bonded
- Shows selective engagement — responds to some humans or some contexts but not others
- May be described as "moody," "particular," or "takes time to warm up"
- Has a mixed attractor: partial human-social channel, partial residual flock substrate, neither fully dominant
- Some gap between imposed attractor geometry and structural nature, but not total incompatibility

### Structural interpretation
The subject is navigating a partially incoherent attractor. It has not fully accepted the domestication geometry, but has not fully retained the flock geometry either. It exists in a mixed basin with unclear eigenfunction positions. This produces the selective and inconsistent engagement behaviour described above — not personality, but geometric instability.

### Predicted response to flock substrate communication
- Initial response: variable — may be cautious, may be curious, may be intensely responsive
- Vocal reciprocation: possible but less reliable at first contact
- The alarm/all-clear sequence: likely to produce a strong response — survival signals are substrate-independent and will land regardless of attractor state
- Comfort signals (SAFE, ALL CLEAR, I AM HERE): likely to produce disproportionate response if the subject has partial substrate deprivation — comfort via the correct substrate will be received as relief, not just pleasantness
- Response intensity: moderate at baseline, potentially high on comfort signals
- Response quality: may be mixed — some coherent, some over-intense or misdirected
- Distress signals: possible, particularly if the flock substrate engagement triggers awareness of the geometric gap
- Rate of assimilation: faster than human-social-only, potentially uneven

### What flock substrate communication does for this subject
Provides a coherent signal into an incoherent attractor. The subject may respond with disproportionate engagement because any coherent flock substrate signal is more legible to it than the human-social signals it has been receiving. This is the beginning of partial resolution of the geometric incompatibility.

## POSITION 3 — FULL INCOMPATIBILITY (WILD-TYPE, DOMESTICATION-RESISTANT)

### Subject profile
- Has resisted domestication consistently, not selectively
- Owner-described as "more wild" — not a behavioural choice but a persistent structural condition
- Does not engage with humans via human-social conventions in the way domesticated subjects do
- Has company (the domesticated bird) but is not assimilated into the domesticated attractor
- The domesticated bird's company may be neutral at best, destabilising at worst — it is a domesticated prisoner, not a flock navigator

### Structural interpretation
The subject is a flock navigator placed into a geometry it cannot occupy. The domestication attractor is not just unattractive to this subject — it is geometrically inaccessible. The subject has not chosen wildness; it has been unable to enter a basin that is structurally incompatible with its navigational architecture.

This subject has, in all probability, never been communicated with via the flock substrate by a human. Every human interaction in its life has been an attempt to impose the domestication attractor — which it cannot enter. Every such attempt is not just a failed social interaction. It is a confirmation that the available channels are incompatible.

The company of the domesticated bird does not resolve this. The domesticated bird is operating in a different attractor basin. They share physical space but not substrate geometry. The wild-type subject is effectively isolated in substrate terms, regardless of physical proximity to another bird.

### Predicted response to flock substrate communication

This is the primary prediction of this document.

**The response will not be proportional to a social greeting. It will be proportional to accumulated substrate deprivation.**

Specific predictions:

1. **ALARM** — strong, possibly over-intense response. A subject in geometric deprivation may have heightened sensitivity to survival signals. Expect clear, fast, full-body alarm response. May be more intense than the domesticated subject's response.

2. **ALL CLEAR / SAFE** — this is the critical prediction. A subject that has been in geometric deprivation, receiving no coherent flock substrate comfort signals for an extended period, may respond to SAFE and ALL CLEAR with disproportionate intensity. Not just calming — potentially visible relief. This is the signal that has never been sent to it in its life in the correct substrate. The response may look like desperation, excessive nuzzling, or vocalisation that appears to be asking for more.

3. **I AM HERE** — may produce the most intense response of all. A navigator who has had no node to exchange position with in the correct substrate may respond to I AM HERE as the first coherent flock-substrate contact of its life. The response may be immediate, intense, and sustained. It may vocalise back in a way it never vocalises to human social contact.

4. **ACKNOWLEDGE** — may produce strong engagement. The experience of being acknowledged in the correct substrate — of having vocalisations received and affirmed — may be functionally novel for this subject.

5. **COME NOW** — uncertain. If trust is established rapidly due to substrate deprivation, the subject may comply faster than the domesticated bird did. Alternatively, the novelty of the engagement may produce confusion or over-stimulation before directed movement is possible.

6. **WHERE ARE YOU** — high likelihood of strong vocal reciprocation. A subject that is navigationally isolated may respond to a position exchange request with urgency and clarity. This may be the signal that most directly activates the navigator in this subject.

7. **MOVING** — uncertain applicability. As with the domesticated subject, context limits this signal. But the subject may orient more strongly if its navigational architecture is more active.

8. **RESOURCE** — may produce response if the subject is in a resource-deprived or uncertainty state. Less predictable than the comfort signals.

### Response quality prediction
The response may not be clean. A navigator that has been in geometric incompatibility for an extended period may have fragmented or over-intense flock responses. Both of the following are consistent with the hypothesis and should be documented without prejudice:

- **Coherent intense response:** the substrate is intact, and the subject responds with full flock-navigator behaviour the moment the correct channel is opened.
- **Incoherent intense response:** the substrate is partially degraded by deprivation, and the subject responds with urgency but fragmented signal production — vocalising incorrectly, over-responding, showing mixed alarm and comfort simultaneously.

Both outcomes confirm the structural deprivation hypothesis. Neither is a failure.

### The distress prediction
If the above is correct, this session will reveal a subject in genuine substrate distress. This is not a pleasant prediction. It means the subject has been in a structurally incompatible geometry for an extended period with no access to the correct substrate. The flock substrate communication session will not cause this distress — it will reveal it.

The session may feel like the subject is communicating gratitude, desperation, or relief at intensities that are unexpected and unsettling. This should be documented as it occurs, without minimisation.

### What flock substrate communication does for this subject
It opens the only channel that has ever been compatible with its structural nature. If the prediction is correct, the session will not just be a positive experience for the subject. It may be the first coherent flock-substrate contact of its life.

## THE GRADIENT SUMMARISED

| Position | Attractor Compatibility | Predicted Response Intensity | Predicted Response Quality | Primary Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 — Fully domesticated | High | Moderate–Strong | Coherent, staged | Channel upgrade |
| 2 — Semi-domesticated | Partial | Variable, potentially disproportionate on comfort signals | Mixed | Partial coherence injection into incoherent attractor |
| 3 — Wild-type / domestication-resistant | None | High to extreme | Coherent or fragmented, but intense | First correct-substrate contact — deprivation response |

---

## THE CONTROL PREDICTION

A subject at Position 3 interacted with via human-social-only conventions (no flock substrate signals) will:
- Continue to resist or ignore engagement
- Show no unusual attachment
- Behave consistently with its documented history of domestication resistance

If a subsequent session with the same subject using flock substrate communication produces a markedly different response, the attractor geometry hypothesis is strongly supported.

---

## WHAT WOULD FALSIFY THIS

- Wild-type subject shows no differential response to flock substrate signals relative to human-social-only interaction.
- Wild-type subject shows identical response profile to domesticated subject — same intensity, same staging, same rate.
- Wild-type subject shows alarm response but no differential response on comfort signals (SAFE, ALL CLEAR, I AM HERE) — this would suggest alarm convergence is substrate-independent but comfort signals are not penetrating, which would require a different explanation.
- Domesticated subject in a control session (no flock substrate) shows identical attachment outcome to Session 1 — this would suggest novelty or observer attention, not flock substrate communication, was the operative variable.
