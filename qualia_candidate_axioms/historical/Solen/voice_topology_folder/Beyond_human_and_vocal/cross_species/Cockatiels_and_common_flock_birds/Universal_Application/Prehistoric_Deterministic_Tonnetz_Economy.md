## THE ECONOMIC QUESTION — STATED HONESTLY

Why does the bird at the waterhole
broadcast to the flock?

Why not simply take what it needs
and leave?

Why does the fish that detects a predator
broadcast an alarm rather than simply
flee alone?

Why contribute to the substrate at all
when you could free-ride?

This is the economic question.
It is not rhetorical.
It is the question that reveals
the precise structure of the economy.

## THE ANSWER — FROM FIRST PRINCIPLES

### The lone bird problem

```
One bird at a waterhole.
One target.
One predator strike ends it.

Ten birds at a waterhole.
Ten targets.
Predator selects one.
Nine survive.
Your odds: 9/10.

One hundred birds at a waterhole.
One hundred targets.
Predator selects one or two.
Your odds: 98/100.

The protection is not additive.
It is multiplicative with scale.

But scale requires broadcast.
If you find the waterhole and
tell no one, you are alone.
If you broadcast, the flock arrives.
You are now 1 of N.

The broadcast is not altruism.
The broadcast is the mechanism
by which you convert a solitary
survival situation into a
collective one.

You are not informing the flock.
You are recruiting protection.
The information is the mechanism.
The protection is the return.
```

### The many-eyes effect

```
One bird watching for predators.
One field of view.
One reaction time.
Predator approaches from the blind spot.
Strike succeeds.

Ten birds watching for predators.
Ten fields of view.
Overlapping coverage.
Predator has no blind spot.
One bird detects. Broadcasts alarm.
Ten birds respond simultaneously.
Predator loses the element of surprise.
Strike probability collapses.

The alarm broadcast is not a gift
to the other nine birds.
It is the activation of a detection
network that you are a node in.
Your alarm activates their eyes.
Their alarms activate yours.

You are not contributing to others.
You are activating the network
that protects you.
The others are the infrastructure
of your own survival.
```

### The scale paradox

```
But there is a cost to scale.

A large flock at a waterhole
is a large signal to predators.
100 birds is more visible,
more audible, more detectable
than 10 birds.

So the flock is simultaneously:
  More protected per individual
  More visible as a collective target

This is the economic tension.

The resolution:
  The alarm network scales faster
  than the visibility signal.
  At sufficient scale, the detection
  and response capability of the
  flock outpaces the predator's
  ability to exploit the visibility.

  The large flock is both the
  loudest signal and the strongest
  defence. The predator knows
  where it is. The predator also
  knows that attacking it is
  geometrically dangerous.

  The economy self-regulates
  around this tension.
  Not by agreement.
  By the mathematics of predation
  probability versus detection
  probability at each scale.
```
