## CORE PRINCIPLE

The current paradigm in sea turtle conservation is reactive.
A turtle strands. A team responds. The turtle is treated
or recorded. The data enters STSSN. Next season the same
beaches receive the same monitoring because the historical
density is the same.

This framework proposes a different paradigm:

**Author the navigational landscape toward the true
attractor rather than reacting to the results of false
attractors.**

The distinction is fundamental. Reactive conservation
addresses the outcome. Landscape authorship addresses
the cause. The force model built in OC-OBS-002 makes
landscape authorship possible for the first time because
it identifies:

1. Where in the landscape the false attractor dominates
2. The magnitude of disruption at each point
3. Which transmitters are causing the disruption
4. What targeted modification does to the force ratio
5. The cascade risk of each intervention before
   it is deployed

## COST-EFFECTIVENESS — WHAT THE LITERATURE CONFIRMS

The peer-reviewed conservation literature is
unambiguous on the proactive vs reactive question.

```
REACTIVE REHABILITATION COSTS (published data):

  Simple cases (minor injuries):    $1,000–$5,000
  Moderate cases (shell, dehydration): $5,000–$10,000
  Severe cases (surgery, long rehab):  $10,000–$40,000

  New England Aquarium cold-stun cases: $1,500–$15,000
  Texas Sea Turtle Hospital annual
  operating cost in high-stranding
  years: several hundred thousand USD

  Source: Stacy et al. (2017), Sea Turtle Health
  and Rehabilitation, CRC Press.

WHAT THAT MEANS AT SCALE:

  Top 5 high-risk segments (from risk_map_2026.csv)
  ~500 migration-season strandings per segment
  per year at conservative estimate.

  500 × 5 segments × $5,000 average = $12.5M/year
  in reactive costs for 5 segments alone.

  Voluntary seasonal power reduction at the dominant
  station per segment: near-zero marginal cost to
  operator for 6-8 weeks at 20-30% ERP reduction.

  Cost asymmetry: enormous.
```

```
WHAT THE LITERATURE FINDS:

  Mazaris et al. (2017):
    Early proactive intervention more cost-effective
    than emergency translocations or artificial
    incubation. Prevention addresses root cause.

  Finkbeiner et al. (2011):
    TEDs (turtle excluder devices) in fishing gear —
    high initial cost, cost-effective over time by
    reducing rescue and rehabilitation demand.

  Benson et al. (2015):
    Proactive interventions address root causes and
    yield sustained benefits. Reactive actions offer
    limited short-term returns.

  Game et al. (2010):
    Prevention consistently more cost-effective than
    cure for migratory species specifically.

  Hamann et al. (2010):
    Reactive measures frequently address symptoms,
    not sources — leading to recurring costs with
    no reduction in underlying cause.
```

The literature supports the cost-effectiveness
argument for landscape authorship strongly. Every
precedent in sea turtle conservation points the
same direction: intervene at the source, not the
outcome.

## THE LIGHT POLLUTION PRECEDENT

This is the closest existing analogue and it matters.

Florida has legally mandated turtle-friendly lighting
ordinances on nesting beaches for decades. The
regulatory pathway has already been walked for sea
turtles and artificial EM disruption at a different
wavelength.

```
LIGHT POLLUTION FRAMEWORK (existing):

  Mechanism:
    Artificial light disorients hatchlings and
    deters nesting females. Measured population
    impact. Regulatory response.

  Intervention:
    Mandatory modification of the light landscape
    during nesting season. Shielding, amber LEDs,
    curfews, vegetation barriers.

  Regulatory basis:
    US Endangered Species Act.
    Florida state statute.
    Inter-American Convention for Sea Turtles.

  Cost-effectiveness:
    Retrofitting cheaper than declining turtle
    populations and lost ecotourism revenue.
    Turtle-friendly LEDs deliver long-term savings.
    Education amplifies effect at low additional cost.

THIS FRAMEWORK (proposed):

  Mechanism:
    AM broadcast infrastructure creates false
    attractor in geomagnetic landscape. Disrupts

  Intervention:
    Targeted seasonal modification of EM landscape
    at dominant nodes during migration window.

  Regulatory basis:
    Same ESA framework.

  Difference from light pollution:
    Stronger quantitative evidence base.
    Force model provides exact intervention
    magnitude and predicted outcome.
```

The light pollution framework is not an analogy.
It is a template. The regulatory pathway has already

## THE CASCADE INCOHERENCE QUESTION

**Can landscape authorship make the EM landscape
more disordered rather than less? Can intervention
cascade into secondary disruption?**

This is the right question to ask before acting.
The answer depends entirely on which intervention
is chosen.

```
INTERVENTIONS THAT REMOVE FORCE FROM THE LANDSCAPE:

  Scale 3 — Targeted transmitter power reduction.

  A power reduction does not introduce new signals.
  It reduces the magnitude of an existing signal.
  The force ratio at the critical point moves toward
  the geomagnetic baseline.
  It does not create a new false attractor.
  It partially restores the landscape toward what
  existed before the transmitter was built.

  CASCADE RISK: LOW.

  One screenable secondary risk exists:
  If the dominant station is reduced, a previously
  masked secondary station may become relatively
  more influential at that location. This is
  computable from the force model before any
  intervention is made. Screen for it first.
  Run the force model with the dominant station
  at reduced ERP. Check whether any secondary
  station crosses the disruption threshold.
  If yes, include that station in the reduction
  agreement. Surgical and targeted.

INTERVENTIONS THAT ADD FORCE TO THE LANDSCAPE:

  Scale 2 — Local signal reinforcement.

  Introduces a new calibrated magnetic signal
  into the landscape. Designed to reinforce the
  true geomagnetic signal at the disruption point.

  CASCADE RISK: MODERATE TO UNKNOWN.

  The literature on artificial magnetic reinforcement
  in a field-deployed marine context is essentially
  absent. Known risks:
    - Secondary disruption at edges of reinforced zone
    - Whether loggerhead magnetoreception can
      distinguish artificial reinforcement from
      natural geomagnetic signal is unknown
    - Regulatory status of intentional magnetic
      emission in coastal marine environments
      is unresolved

  This intervention requires laboratory and field
  validation before any deployment. It is flagged
  here as an option to investigate, not to deploy.
  Do not deploy Scale 2 without validation.

THE PRINCIPLE:

  Remove force before adding force.
  Screen for secondary effects before intervening.
  The force model makes both possible.
  Intervention without screening is how you
  author incoherence instead of correcting it.
```

## THE DUAL APPROACH

Landscape authorship does not replace reactive
conservation. It operates alongside it. The two
approaches address different points in the causal
chain.

```
CAUSAL CHAIN:

  Transmitter → False attractor → Force ratio tips →
  Corridor scatter → Wrong coastal approach →
  Stranding → Death

REACTIVE INTERVENTION:
  Acts at: Stranding → Death
  Prevents: Individual death after displacement
  Scale: Dozens of turtles per season per team
  Cost: $1,000–$40,000 per turtle (literature)
  Timing: After disruption has completed
  Literature support: Strong. Well documented.
  Cascade risk: None.

PREDICTIVE INTERCEPTION:
  Acts at: Corridor scatter → Wrong coastal approach
  Prevents: Stranding before it occurs
  Scale: Hundreds of turtles per season per corridor
  Cost: Moderate (boat positioned in geomagnetic
        corridor offshore of high-risk segment)
  Timing: During active migration, before commitment
          to wrong coastal approach
  Literature support: Novel. No direct precedent.
  Cascade risk: None. No landscape modification.

LANDSCAPE AUTHORSHIP:
  Acts at: Transmitter → False attractor
  Prevents: Disruption before it begins
  Scale: Every turtle navigating that corridor,
         every season, automatically
  Cost: Near zero per turtle at scale (voluntary)
        to low (regulatory)
  Timing: Seasonal — active during migration windows
  Literature support: Structural precedent (light
        pollution, vessel speed, acoustic mgmt).
        Direct precedent: absent. Creating it now.
  Cascade risk: Low for Scale 3 (removes force).
                Moderate-unknown for Scale 2
                (adds force — validate first).
```

The three approaches are not alternatives.
They are layers. Deploy all three where resources
allow. Prioritise by position in the causal chain
— the earlier the intervention, the more animals
it protects per dollar spent.

## FOUR SCALES OF LANDSCAPE AUTHORSHIP

### Scale 1 — Individual Corridor Interception

**What it is:**
A boat or drone positioned in the geomagnetic corridor
10-20 km offshore of a high-risk segment during peak
migration. The turtle is following its precise natal
corridor. The AM force is beginning to scatter it.
Physical or acoustic redirection keeps the turtle on
the geomagnetic corridor through the high-disruption
zone.

**What the force model provides:**
- The geomagnetic approach bearing for each segment
  (the true corridor — 3.52 km population precision)
- The onset distance at which AM disruption becomes
  significant (derivable from force ratio gradient
  moving shoreward from the station)
- The peak disruption zone location per segment

**Literature support:**
No direct precedent for this specific intervention.
Predictive interception as a concept is supported
by the general proactive conservation cost-
effectiveness literature (Game et al. 2010,
Benson et al. 2015).

**Cascade risk:** None. No landscape modification.

**Pragmatic note:**
Most immediately deployable intervention.
Requires no regulatory action, no transmitter
modification, no new infrastructure. Requires
a boat, a team, and the approach corridor bearings
from risk_map_2026.csv. Deployable April 2026
with existing NOAA/FWC assets repositioned.

---

### Scale 2 — Local Signal Reinforcement

**What it is:**
A temporary low-power calibrated magnetic reference
emitter positioned in the high-disruption zone during
peak migration. Designed to reinforce the true
geomagnetic signal at the precise point where the
force ratio tips toward the false attractor.

**What the force model provides:**
- The exact location where force ratio tips
  (computable from k = 5.751217e+05 and local
  geo_magnitude_nT)
- The reinforcement magnitude required to restore
  the ratio below disruption threshold
- The bearing the reinforcement signal needs
  to encode

**Literature support:**
Essentially absent for field-deployed marine
magnetic reinforcement. Laboratory studies on
magnetic noise and magnetoreception exist but
do not address artificial reinforcement at
population scale in wild animals.

**Cascade risk:** Moderate to unknown.
Introduces new force into landscape.
Secondary disruption at zone edges possible.
Magnetoreception response to artificial
reinforcement unvalidated in wild loggerheads.

**Do not deploy without:**
- Laboratory validation of loggerhead response
  to artificial magnetic reinforcement
- Field pilot with tracked animals
- Assessment of edge-zone secondary effects
- Regulatory clearance for intentional magnetic
  emission in coastal marine environment

**Status:** Investigate. Do not deploy yet.

---

### Scale 3 — Targeted Transmitter Modification

**What it is:**
The dominant AM station per high-risk segment
negotiates voluntary or regulated seasonal power
reduction during the peak migration window.
April through June, August through November.
20-30% ERP reduction at the dominant station
reduces the force ratio at the critical point.

**What the force model provides:**
- Dominant station per segment (single transmitter
  contributing most weight to the false attractor)
- Quantified ERP reduction to force ratio reduction
  relationship (derivable from r = 0.59 dose-response)
- Predicted reduction in corridor scatter per unit
  of ERP reduction
- Secondary station screening — run force model
  at reduced ERP before agreeing reduction to
  confirm no secondary station crosses disruption
  threshold

**Literature support:**
Structural precedent strong. Light pollution
ordinances for sea turtle nesting beaches —
same ESA basis, same class of intervention,
different wavelength. Vessel speed restrictions
for manatees. Acoustic management for cetaceans.
All establish the regulatory template.

**Cascade risk:** Low. Removes force from landscape.
Screenable secondary risk — computable before
intervention using existing force model.

**Cost to operator:**
Minimal. 20-30% ERP reduction for 6-8 weeks
per year. Coverage reduction at margins of
broadcast footprint only. Not a commercially
significant operational constraint for most
stations.

**Regulatory pathway:**
- Voluntary agreement: fastest. No regulatory
  process. Operator acts on published evidence.
  Approach after peer-reviewed publication.
- FCC NEPA environmental review: medium term.
  Requires peer-reviewed publication and NOAA
  formal citation of result.
- ESA Section 7 consultation: long term.
  Requires USFWS formal determination that
  AM infrastructure constitutes threat to
  listed species.

**This is the highest-leverage intervention.**
One seasonal power reduction at one dominant
station protects every turtle navigating that
corridor for the full migration window —
potentially thousands of animals per season,
automatically, at near-zero marginal cost.

### Scale 4 — Network-Level Landscape Design

**What it is:**
Full mapping of the AM broadcast landscape against
the complete migration corridor network for all
magnetically navigating species. Identification of
high-disruption nodes dominating the false attractor
across multiple species and corridors simultaneously.
Targeted modification of those nodes restores
navigational geometry across the entire landscape.

**What the force model provides:**
- Force landscape computable for any point from
  am_stations_clean.csv and WMM-2025
- High-disruption nodes follow inverse square law —
  small number of high-power clear-channel stations
  dominate the landscape disproportionately
- Network analysis identifies which 50-100 stations
  out of 13,784 resolve the majority of disruption
  across all corridors
- Cascade screening computable at network scale
  before any modification is implemented

**Literature support:**
No direct precedent at this scale. The multi-species
evidence base (OC-OBS-001 through OC-OBS-003) and
the peer-reviewed result are prerequisites. This
scale requires the regulatory framework to be
formally engaged. Years away.

**Cascade risk:** Manageable if screened.
Network-level force model screens all secondary
effects before any node is modified. The same
pipeline that identified the disruption can
map the cascade before intervention.

**Pragmatic note:**
The computation exists today. The data exists today.
The pipeline exists today. What is required is
the peer-reviewed foundation, the regulatory
engagement, and the multi-species evidence base.
This is the long-term vision. It is technically
possible now. It is institutionally years away.

## IMPORTANT UNCERTAINTIES

This framework is built on a strong observational
foundation. The causal mechanism is physically
grounded and the dose-response is confirmed.
However, the following must be acknowledged before
operational deployment:

```
1. Magnetoreception mechanism in loggerheads is
   not fully characterised. Whether AM frequencies
   interact with magnetite-based or radical-pair-
   based magnetoreception — or both — affects the
   precise form of the disruption. The force model
   is confirmed phenomenologically. The biophysical
   mechanism is an open research question.

2. The 3.52 km corridor precision is a population
   mean, not an individual guarantee. Individual
   variation in natal homing precision is real.
   Age, sex, reproductive status, and prior
   navigation experience modulate susceptibility.
   The risk map is a population-level tool.

3. Scale 2 (signal reinforcement) carries unknown
   cascade risk and requires laboratory and field
   validation before any deployment. Do not deploy
   Scale 2 without validation. This is non-
   negotiable given the cascade incoherence risk.

4. Scale 3 (transmitter modification) requires
   cascade screening via force model at reduced ERP
   before any approach to operators. The screen is
   fast and computable from existing pipeline.
   Do not skip it.

5. Voluntary transmitter modification requires
   operator cooperation. The regulatory pathway
   is available but slow. Both tracks should be
   pursued in parallel after publication.

6. This framework is currently validated for
   Caretta caretta on the US Atlantic and Gulf
   coast AM broadcast landscape. Extension to
   other species and geographies requires
   species-specific validation runs using the
   same pipeline architecture.

7. This is an observational result. The causal
   mechanism is physically grounded and the
   dose-response is confirmed. Formal causal
   proof requires experimental manipulation —
   either natural experiments (transmitter
   shutdowns, power changes) or controlled
   field studies. Analysis B (FWC cable cohort)
   and the longitudinal transmitter change
   analysis are the next steps toward formal
   causal confirmation.
```
