## PART I: WHAT WAS CONFIRMED
## IN FEBRUARY 2025

### The UNC study

Researchers at the University of
North Carolina at Chapel Hill
conducted a behavioral conditioning
experiment with loggerhead sea
turtle hatchlings.

**The setup:**

Hatchlings were placed in a
controlled tank surrounded by
a magnetic coil system capable
of simulating any combination
of magnetic field inclination
and intensity.

Two distinct artificial magnetic
field signatures were created —
corresponding to the actual
magnetic parameters of two
different geographic locations
(different points in the Atlantic
Ocean, with different inclination/
intensity combinations).

Hatchlings were trained to
associate one magnetic signature
with food reward and the other
with no reward.

**The result:**

The hatchlings learned the association.

When later presented with the
two magnetic environments, they
performed the food-seeking behavior
(described in press as "dancing"
— rapid orienting movements
toward the food reward zone) in
response to the learned magnetic
signature and not in response
to the control signature.

**The retention result:**

The association was retained for
months. Hatchlings tested long
after initial conditioning still
showed the conditioned response.

This is long-term magnetic memory
in a hatchling that, in the wild,
would use exactly this capacity
to remember the magnetic signature
of the beach it emerged from —
and to return to that beach
20–30 years later.

**What the study confirmed:**

1. Loggerhead hatchlings can
   distinguish between two magnetic
   field signatures differing only
   in inclination and intensity.

2. They can associate a specific
   magnetic signature with a
   learned outcome (food = here).

3. This association persists for
   months — long enough to be
   carried into adult life.

4. The imprinting window is
   during the frenzy swim: the
   period immediately after
   emergence from the nest when
   the hatchling first encounters
   the open ocean's electromagnetic
   environment.

**What the study did not address:**

Whether an artificial magnetic
anomaly (such as that produced
by a submarine power cable)
during the frenzy swim would
corrupt the learned address.

This was not the study's question.
But it is the question that
follows directly from the study's
answer.

## PART II: THE PHYSICS OF
## ADDRESS CORRUPTION

### What a submarine cable does
### to the magnetic environment

**HVDC cables (DC power transmission):**

A high-voltage direct current
submarine cable carrying load
produces a static magnetic field.
The field magnitude directly
at the seabed surface above
the cable: 200–2,000 nT.
(Varies with cable design,
depth of burial, and current load.
CEFAS 2023; TU Delft 2024.)

**HVAC cables (AC power transmission):**

An AC cable at 50 or 60 Hz
produces an alternating magnetic
field. Magnitude at seabed surface:
tens to several hundred nT.
Falls as 1/r² with distance from cable.

**Context: Earth's ambient field
at the Florida coast:**

Total field intensity: ~45,000–55,000 nT
Inclination: ~55–65° (field dips
steeply downward at mid-latitudes)
Declination: ~3–7°W (slight
westward deviation from geographic north)

A 2,000 nT anomaly superimposed
on a 50,000 nT ambient field:

Change in total intensity: +4%
Change in inclination:
   ΔI = arctan(2000 sin(θ) / 50000) ≈ 2°
   (where θ is the angle of the
   anomaly field relative to ambient)

**What 2° inclination change means
for address writing:**

The magnetic address of a natal
beach is defined by the specific
combination of inclination and
intensity at that location on
that night.

If a hatchling emerges from a
beach where the ambient inclination
is 60.0°, its address is encoded
as: (intensity ≈ 47,000 nT,
inclination ≈ 60.0°).

If a submarine cable 100 m
offshore is carrying full load,
the field the hatchling encounters
during the frenzy swim is:
(intensity ≈ 47,000 + 400 nT,
inclination ≈ 61.5°).

The address that gets written is:
(47,400 nT, 61.5°).

The "real" address of this beach
in the Earth's field, without
the cable: (47,000 nT, 60.0°).

**The address has been corrupted
by 1.5°.**

When this hatchling matures and
returns to nest 20–30 years later,
it will navigate to the location
on Earth whose magnetic parameters
match its remembered address:
(47,400 nT, 61.5°).

That location is approximately
50–80 km along the coast from
the actual natal beach, displaced
in the direction where the Earth's
gradient of inclination produces
a 1.5° increase.

The turtle does not nest on the
wrong beach because it is
confused. It nests on the wrong
beach because it is navigating
accurately to the address it
was given.

**The corruption is in the writing,
not the reading.**

### The spatial specificity of corruption

The cable's field anomaly is
not uniform along the coastline.
It creates a local magnetic
fingerprint — a specific deviation
from ambient that exists only
in the zone of cable influence.

Within 50–100 m of the cable:
field anomaly > 200 nT. Detectable.
Potentially address-corrupting.

At 200 m from the cable:
field anomaly < 50 nT. Below
the detection threshold of the
loggerhead magnetic compass
(estimated ~50–100 nT behavioral
sensitivity from conditioning studies).

At 500 m from the cable:
field anomaly < 5 nT. Negligible.

**This means:**

Only hatchlings from nesting
beaches where the frenzy swim
path crosses through the cable
corridor (within ~100 m of the
cable) would be at risk of
address corruption.

The cable corridors approach
shore at specific angles and
locations. They converge at
cable landing stations.

Cable landing stations on the
Florida coast: multiple, concentrated
at certain counties (Palm Beach,
Broward, Miami-Dade, and Monroe
counties on the Atlantic side
of Florida).

Nesting beaches immediately north
and south of cable landing
corridors: these are the at-risk
nesting beaches.

    coordinates as a proxy for the approach point.
    The actual cable corridor extends ~1 km on
    either side of the landing station along the coast.
    """
    df = pd.read_csv(path)

    required = ['cable_name', 'installation_year',
                'landing_lat', 'landing_lon']
    for col in required:
        if col not in df.columns:
            raise ValueError(
                f"Cable data missing: {col}\n"
                f"Available: {list(df.columns)}"
            )

    print(f"  Loaded {len(df)} cable landing stations")
    return df.reset_index(drop=True)


def classify_beaches(turtle_df, cable_df,
                     cable_threshold=CABLE_CORRIDOR_THRESHOLD_KM,
                     control_threshold=CONTROL_BEACH_THRESHOLD_KM):
    """
    For each turtle, classify its proxy natal beach
    (= first nesting location on record) as:
    - 'cable': within cable_threshold km of a cable corridor
    - 'control': beyond control_threshold km from all cables
    - 'intermediate': between thresholds (excluded)

    Returns turtle_df with 'beach_class' and
    'nearest_cable_km' columns.
    """
    # Get first nesting record per turtle (proxy natal beach)
    first_nests = turtle_df.groupby('turtle_id').first().reset_index()

    beach_classes = []
    nearest_km_list = []

    for _, row in first_nests.iterrows():
        turtle_point = (row['nest_lat'], row['nest_lon'])

        # Find nearest cable landing
        nearest_km = np.inf
        for _, cable in cable_df.iterrows():
            cable_point = (cable['landing_lat'],
                          cable['landing_lon'])
            try:
                d = geodesic(turtle_point, cable_point).km
                if d < nearest_km:
                    nearest_km = d
            except Exception:
                continue

        nearest_km_list.append(nearest_km)

        if nearest_km <= cable_threshold:
            beach_classes.append('cable')
        elif nearest_km >= control_threshold:
            beach_classes.append('control')
        else:
            beach_classes.append('intermediate')

    first_nests['beach_class'] = beach_classes
    first_nests['nearest_cable_km'] = nearest_km_list

    # Merge back to full dataframe
    turtle_df = turtle_df.merge(
        first_nests[['turtle_id', 'beach_class',
                     'nearest_cable_km']],
        on='turtle_id', how='left'
    )

    print(f"\n  Beach classification:")
    for cls in ['cable', 'control', 'intermediate']:
        n = (first_nests['beach_class'] == cls).sum()
        print(f"    {cls}: {n} turtles")

    return turtle_df


def compute_individual_spread(turtle_df):
    """
    For each turtle with >= MIN_NESTING_RECORDS,
    compute the geographic spread of nesting locations.

    Spread metric: mean great-circle distance from
    each nesting site to the individual's mean
    nesting centroid.

    Returns DataFrame with one row per turtle:
      turtle_id, n_nests, spread_km,
      centroid_lat, centroid_lon,
      beach_class, nearest_cable_km
    """
    results = []

    for turtle_id, group in turtle_df.groupby('turtle_id'):
        if len(group) < MIN_NESTING_RECORDS:
            continue

        lats = group['nest_lat'].values
        lons = group['nest_lon'].values
        beach_class = group['beach_class'].iloc[0]
        nearest_km  = group['nearest_cable_km'].iloc[0]

        centroid_lat = np.mean(lats)
        centroid_lon = np.mean(lons)
        centroid = (centroid_lat, centroid_lon)

        # Mean distance from centroid
        distances = []
        for lat, lon in zip(lats, lons):
            try:
                d = geodesic(centroid, (lat, lon)).km
                distances.append(d)
            except Exception:
                pass

        spread_km = np.mean(distances) if distances else np.nan

        results.append({
            'turtle_id': turtle_id,
            'n_nests': len(group),
            'spread_km': spread_km,
            'centroid_lat': centroid_lat,
            'centroid_lon': centroid_lon,
            'beach_class': beach_class,
            'nearest_cable_km': nearest_km
        })

    df = pd.DataFrame(results).dropna(subset=['spread_km'])
    print(f"\n  Spread computed for "
          f"{len(df)} turtles (>= {MIN_NESTING_RECORDS} nests)")
    return df


def run_cohort_analysis(spread_df):
    """
    Compare nesting spread between cable and
    control beach cohorts.
    """
    cable   = spread_df[spread_df['beach_class'] == 'cable']
    control = spread_df[spread_df['beach_class'] == 'control']

    print("\n" + "="*60)
    print("COHORT SPREAD COMPARISON")
    print("="*60)

    print(f"\n  Cable cohort:   n={len(cable)}")
    print(f"    Median spread: "
          f"{cable['spread_km'].median():.2f} km")
    print(f"    Mean spread:   "
          f"{cable['spread_km'].mean():.2f} km")
    print(f"    IQR:           "
          f"{cable['spread_km'].quantile(0.25):.2f} – "
          f"{cable['spread_km'].quantile(0.75):.2f} km")

    print(f"\n  Control cohort: n={len(control)}")
    print(f"    Median spread: "
          f"{control['spread_km'].median():.2f} km")
    print(f"    Mean spread:   "
          f"{control['spread_km'].mean():.2f} km")
    print(f"    IQR:           "
          f"{control['spread_km'].quantile(0.25):.2f} – "
          f"{control['spread_km'].quantile(0.75):.2f} km")

    if len(cable) < 5 or len(control) < 5:
        print("\n  WARNING: Too few turtles in one group "
              "for reliable statistics.")
        return

    # Mann-Whitney U test (one-tailed: cable > control)
    stat, p = mannwhitneyu(
        cable['spread_km'],
        control['spread_km'],
        alternative='greater'  # H1: cable spread > control
    )

    # Effect size: rank-biserial correlation
    n1, n2 = len(cable), len(control)
    r_rb = 1 - (2 * stat) / (n1 * n2)

    print(f"\n  Mann-Whitney U test (one-tailed, H1: cable > control):")
    print(f"    U = {stat:.1f}")
    print(f"    p = {p:.4f}")
    print(f"    r_rb (effect size) = {r_rb:.3f}")
    print(f"    (r_rb: 0.1=small, 0.3=medium, 0.5=large)")

    # ── Result classification ──────────────────────────────
    print("\n" + "="*60)
    print("RESULT")
    print("="*60)

    if p < 0.05:
        print(f"\n  POSITIVE: Cable-beach turtles show "
              f"significantly higher")
        print(f"  nesting spread than control-beach turtles.")
        print(f"  p = {p:.4f}, r_rb = {r_rb:.3f}")
        print(f"\n  Interpretation consistent with:")
        print(f"  Address corruption during frenzy swim")
        print(f"  in cable corridor → adult nesting")
        print(f"  centroid displaced from natal beach")
        print(f"  → higher observed spread in subsequent seasons")
    else:
        print(f"\n  NULL: No significant difference in nesting")
        print(f"  spread between cable and control cohorts.")
        print(f"  p = {p:.4f}")
        print(f"\n  Interpretation:")
        if p < 0.20:
            print(f"  Marginal signal. May be underpowered.")
            print(f"  Check sample sizes and cable proximity threshold.")
        else:
            print(f"  No evidence for address corruption effect")
            print(f"  at the tested spatial threshold.")

    return {
        'n_cable': len(cable),
        'n_control': len(control),
        'median_cable': cable['spread_km'].median(),
        'median_control': control['spread_km'].median(),
        'U': stat, 'p': p, 'r_rb': r_rb
    }
