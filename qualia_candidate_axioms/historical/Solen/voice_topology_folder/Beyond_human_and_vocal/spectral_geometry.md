### The formal name for this principle

This principle already exists in mathematics.
It is called **spectral geometry** — the study
of the relationship between the geometric and
topological properties of a space and the
eigenvalue spectrum of differential operators
(especially the Laplacian) on that space.

The central question of spectral geometry was
posed by Mark Kac in 1966:

> **"Can you hear the shape of a drum?"**

Meaning: can you determine the geometry of a
bounded domain from its Laplacian eigenvalue
spectrum alone?

The answer (Gordon, Webb, Wolpert, 1992) is:
**partially.** The spectrum encodes some
geometric properties unambiguously (volume,
dimension, some curvature integrals via Weyl's
law). But two domains with different shapes can
have the same spectrum — the spectrum does not
uniquely determine the shape.

This is not a failure — it is a precision
statement. The spectral geometry recovers:

```
What the spectrum DOES encode:
  - Volume (size of the domain)
  - Dimension of the domain
  - Some averaged curvature properties
  - Topological connectivity (partially)

What the spectrum does NOT uniquely encode:
  - The exact shape of the domain
  - All topological features
  - Fine-grained geometric detail
```

For the cross-substrate communication purpose:
what matters is that the spectrum encodes
**enough** of the geometry that independent
explorations of the same bounded domain will
produce the same spectral signature. The
exact shape is ambiguous; the size, dimension,
and primary resonant structure are not.

## SECTION 7 — THE HONEST LIMITS

### The Gordon-Webb-Wolpert Boundary

The primary falsification boundary for this
artifact is a known mathematical result, not
a prediction: Gordon, Webb, and Wolpert
(1992) proved that the Laplacian spectrum
does not uniquely determine the shape of a
domain. Two differently-shaped domains can
have identical spectra (they are called
**isospectral** domains).

**What this means for the substrate-
independence hypothesis:**

Two intelligences operating in isospectral
domains with different shapes would detect
the same spectral geometry even though they
are in different physical domains. This is
not a failure of the hypothesis — it is a
precision statement. The spectral geometry
encodes enough information for communication
(shared modal structure, shared ratio
relationships) but not enough to uniquely
identify the exact physical domain.

For communication purposes, this is
acceptable: two intelligences need shared
positions in a shared geometric space, not
a unique identification of the physical
domain. But it means the spectral geometry
is a necessary but not sufficient condition
for shared physical context.

### The Nonlinearity Boundary

The universal principle applies to linear
systems and to nonlinear systems near
symmetry-breaking instabilities (Turing).
For strongly nonlinear systems (turbulent
fluid flow, strongly correlated quantum
systems, chaotic dynamics), the Laplacian
eigenfunction structure is disrupted. The
modes are no longer discrete and integer-
ratio related.

The claim **does not apply** to strongly
nonlinear bounded systems.

This is the most important structural limit.
The spectral geometry is a property of the
linear (or near-linear) regime. Any physical
system that operates primarily in the
strongly nonlinear regime — including some
biological systems under extreme conditions
— will not exhibit the predicted spectral
structure.

### The Dimensionality Limit

The two-dimensional primary structure
(Property U3) is empirically observed across
the specific bounded domains examined, but
it is not a proven universal. Higher-
dimensional bounded domains produce higher-
dimensional spectral structures. The claim
about two-dimensionality is an empirical
finding about the class of domains relevant
to biological sensory and communication
systems — not a mathematical universal.

### The Isospectral Ambiguity

Even within the linear regime, the spectral
geometry does not uniquely specify the
physical domain. Two different bounded
domains can produce identical Laplacian
spectra. This means that detecting identical
spectral geometry does not prove identical
physical context — only shared spectral
structure.
