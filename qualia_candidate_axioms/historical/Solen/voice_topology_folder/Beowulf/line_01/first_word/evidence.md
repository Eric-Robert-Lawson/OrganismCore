## WHAT THIS WORD IS

The opening word of Beowulf.
The oldest substantial text in the English language.
An attention command — the Old English equivalent
of "Listen!" or "Hear me!" delivered to a mead hall.

Not an interrogative.
Not "what" in the modern sense.
A performative utterance that commands silence
and announces the beginning of the poem.

Every Anglo-Saxon audience knew this word
was coming. Its acoustic texture was the signal
that the scop had begun.

## PHONEME STRUCTURE

Three phonemes:
  [ʍ]  —  voiceless labiovelar fricative onset
  [æ]  —  low front unrounded vowel nucleus
  [t]  —  voiceless alveolar stop coda

## STREAM 1: ORTHOGRAPHIC
## Confidence: STRONG

The spelling HW is systematic across
Old English manuscripts.

HW before vowels in Old English consistently
records the voiceless labiovelar fricative [ʍ].
This is the same cluster preserved in:
  Scottish English: which [ʍɪtʃ] ≠ witch [wɪtʃ]
  Some American dialects: where, when, while

The HW digraph was not decorative.
Old English scribes recorded phonemic distinctions.
HW and W were different sounds to them.
The spelling is direct evidence of [ʍ].

The Æ spelling records the low front vowel [æ].
The ash character (Æ/æ) was specifically created
for Old English to represent this sound.
It is not [a], not [e], not [ɑ].
It is the vowel of modern English "cat" —
front, low, unrounded.

The T spelling records the voiceless alveolar stop.
No ambiguity. T in Old English = [t].

Convergence: all three spellings are
unambiguous and systematic.
Confidence: STRONG

  Icelandic: þat — "that"
    The cognate of "that" preserves front vowel.

The Old English [æ] is essentially identical
to the modern English "short a" in "cat".
No reconstruction uncertainty on the vowel.
Confidence: STRONG

## STREAM 4: ORTHOEPIST / DOCUMENTARY
## Confidence: MODERATE (indirect)

No orthoepist wrote about Old English pronunciation
directly — the tradition of explicit pronunciation
commentary begins after the Norman Conquest,
by which time the HW simplification was underway.

Indirect evidence:
  The Ormulum (c. 1180 CE) — written after
  the Conquest — shows HW still distinct from W
  in some dialects.

  Scottish English preservation of [ʍ] is
  the living documentary evidence —
  a dialect that was isolated enough from
  southern English influence to preserve
  the pre-Conquest distinction.

  The simplification of HW → W in southern
  English dialects is documented from
  approximately 1200–1400 CE.
  This is negative evidence: the sound
  existed until it was lost, and we can
  date approximately when it was lost.

Confidence: MODERATE
(absence of direct commentary is expected
given the date; indirect evidence is consistent)
